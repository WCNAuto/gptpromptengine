## Annual Review Drafts

> You are an HR manager writing a year-end performance review for a high performer being considered for promotion.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}, {years_tenure} years at the company
> Rating: {exceeds_expectations / meets_expectations / below_expectations}
> Top achievements:
> {achievement_1}
> {achievement_2} 
> {achievement_3}
> Growth areas: {primary_development_area} and {secondary_development_area}
> Promotion timeline: {this_quarter / next_quarter / within_6_months}
> Company tone: {supportive / direct / formal}
>
> Write a 400-500 word performance review using the SBI model. Open with specific recognition of their standout achievement. Address development areas as growth opportunities tied to the next role level. Close with promotion readiness assessment and next quarter goals.

When to use it: Thursday afternoon when you're finalizing reviews for Friday's calibration meeting and need promotion-ready language.

Pro tip: If they're borderline for promotion, change the closing to "areas to strengthen before next review cycle" instead of assuming promotion readiness.

>

> Department: {department}

> Write a 350-400 word review that acknowledges early success, addresses the performance shift without speculation, and sets clear expectations for improvement. Use a concerned but supportive tone. End with specific next steps and timeline for check-ins.

When to use it: When you need to document declining performance while keeping the door open for recovery.

Pro tip: Avoid guessing at personal reasons for the decline. Stick to observable behaviors and measurable outcomes only.

## Difficult Conversation Scripts

> You are preparing to deliver a below-expectations performance review in person.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Main performance gaps: {gap_1}, {gap_2}, {gap_3}
> Previous feedback given: {yes_documented / yes_informal / minimal / none}
> Employee's likely reaction: {defensive / receptive / emotional / argumentative}
> Improvement timeline: {30_days / 60_days / 90_days}
> Support offered: {training / mentoring / reduced_scope / different_projects}
> Consequences if no improvement: {different_role / pip / termination}
> Your relationship: {good / neutral / tense}
>
> Write a 400-500 word conversation script that opens with care, presents facts clearly, listens for their perspective, and ends with concrete next steps. Include specific language for difficult moments and transition phrases between topics.

When to use it: The night before a tough performance conversation when you need exact words, not just talking points.

Pro tip: Write their likely objections in the margin and practice your responses. Defensive employees follow predictable patterns.

> You need to address an employee who received feedback from their review but is now arguing with the assessment.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Original rating: {rating}
> Their main objections: {objection_1}, {objection_2}
> Documentation you have: {strong / adequate / limited}
> Specific examples: {example_1}, {example_2}, {example_3}
> Their proposed rating: {what_they_want}
> Meeting context: {follow_up_meeting / email_response / their_request}
> Escalation risk: {low / medium / high}
> Manager support: {backing_you / neutral / unknown}
>
> Write a 250-300 word response script that acknowledges their concerns, restates your assessment with examples, and maintains the original decision. Stay professional but firm. End with clear next steps and no ambiguity about the final rating.

When to use it: When an employee challenges their review rating and you need to hold your ground professionally.

Pro tip: Never negotiate ratings in the moment. Take their feedback, review your notes, and respond within 24 hours with a decision.

## Performance Improvement Plans

> You are creating a 30-day performance improvement plan for an employee with specific skill gaps.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Primary skill gaps: {gap_1}, {gap_2}, {gap_3}
> Impact on work: {missed_deadlines / quality_issues / team_delays / client_complaints}
> Support provided: {training_resource}, {mentor_name}, {check_in_schedule}
> Success metrics: {metric_1}, {metric_2}, {metric_3}
> Check-in dates: {week_1_date}, {week_2_date}, {week_3_date}, {final_review_date}
> Manager commitment: {daily_feedback / weekly_meetings / project_review / open_door}
> Consequences: {role_change / termination / extended_plan}
>
> Write a 400-450 word PIP that clearly states expectations, provides specific support, sets measurable goals, and explains consequences. Use supportive but serious tone. Include exact dates and metrics that remove ambiguity about success.

When to use it: When informal coaching hasn't worked and you need formal documentation with clear improvement timeline.

Pro tip: Make the metrics objective and observable by others. Avoid subjective measures like "better attitude" or "improved communication."

## Follow-up Documentation

> You need to send a follow-up email after delivering a difficult performance review to summarize key points and next steps.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Review meeting date: {date}
> Rating delivered: {rating}
> Main discussion points: {point_1}, {point_2}, {point_3}
> Their reaction: {receptive / defensive / emotional / argumentative / professional}
> Commitments they made: {commitment_1}, {commitment_2}
> Support you offered: {resource_1}, {resource_2}
> Next check-in: {date}
> Documentation needed: {PIP / follow_up_goals / training_plan / schedule_change}
> HR involvement: {required / recommended / not_needed}
>
> Write a 200-250 word follow-up email that professionally summarizes the conversation, confirms mutual understanding, restates next steps, and maintains a supportive tone. Include specific dates and commitments from both sides.

When to use it: Within 24 hours of any performance conversation that could become contentious or needs clear documentation.

Pro tip: Send this even after positive reviews with development discussions. It shows professionalism and prevents misunderstandings later.
