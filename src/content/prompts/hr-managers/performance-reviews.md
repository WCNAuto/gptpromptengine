## Year-End Performance Reviews

> You are an HR manager writing a comprehensive year-end performance review for a team member being considered for promotion.
> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Current role: {job_title}
> Tenure: {years_at_company}
> Overall rating: {exceeds_expectations/meets_expectations/below_expectations}
> Key achievements:
> {three_major_wins_this_year}
> Areas for development:
> {two_growth_areas}
> Promotion readiness: {ready/needs_development/not_ready}
> Manager tone preference: {supportive/direct/formal}
> 
> Write a 400-500 word performance review using the SBI framework (Situation, Behavior, Impact). Start with specific recognition of their biggest win. Address development areas as growth opportunities, not failures. End with two concrete goals for next year tied to their career progression.

When to use it: Thursday afternoon when promotion reviews are due Friday morning and you need a complete draft fast.

Pro tip: If the employee is borderline for promotion, emphasize specific skill gaps they need to close rather than personality traits they need to change.

> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Years in role: {tenure}

> This year's contributions: {three_steady_achievements}

> Write a 300-350 word review that acknowledges their reliability while encouraging them to take on bigger challenges. Use specific examples. Frame the growth area as an opportunity to increase their impact, not fix a problem.

When to use it: When you need to write a fair review for someone who does good work but doesn't make waves.

Pro tip: Mention specific projects or moments where they delivered exactly what was needed—solid performers often go unrecognized because they make your job easier.

## Mid-Year Check-ins

> You are conducting a mid-year performance conversation with someone who is on track but could benefit from stretch assignments.
> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Current performance: {meeting_goals/slightly_ahead/coasting}
> Strengths to leverage: {two_skills_they_excel_at}
> Potential stretch opportunity: {project_or_responsibility}
> Their career interest: {wants_promotion/wants_skill_development/content_in_role}
> Time available: {bandwidth_for_extra_work}
> 
> Write a 200-250 word conversation guide that acknowledges their solid work and introduces a growth opportunity. Frame it as a chance to build skills they care about, not just more work.

When to use it: Mid-year one-on-ones when you want to challenge someone who's doing well but playing it safe.

Pro tip: Tie the stretch assignment to a skill they'll need for their next career move, even if that move isn't immediate—it makes the extra work feel like investment, not busy work.

When to use it: July check-ins when someone is struggling and you need to decide whether to adjust expectations or provide more support.

Pro tip: Separate issues they can control from external factors—focus your energy and theirs on what they can actually change in the next six months.

## Difficult Performance Conversations

> You are preparing for a conversation with an employee whose performance has declined and needs immediate improvement.
> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Specific performance issues: {missed_deadlines/quality_problems/attendance_issues}
> Timeline of decline: {started_when}
> Previous conversations: {informal_coaching/formal_warning/first_discussion}
> Their typical response: {receptive/defensive/makes_excuses}
> Consequences if no improvement: {pip/role_change/termination}
> Support available: {training/mentoring/workload_adjustment}
> 
> Write a 300-350 word conversation script that is direct but professional. State the problems clearly, explain the impact, and outline specific expectations for improvement. Include timeline and consequences.

When to use it: When informal coaching isn't working and you need to have a formal performance conversation that could lead to disciplinary action.

Pro tip: Document the conversation immediately after it happens, including their response and any commitments they made—you'll need this if performance doesn't improve.

When to use it: When someone does good work but their behavior is affecting team morale or productivity.

Pro tip: Focus on observable behaviors and business impact, not how they make people feel—it's harder to argue with facts than emotions.

## Promotion and Development Reviews

> You are writing a promotion recommendation for someone who is ready to move to the next level.
> 
> Candidate: {employee_name}
> Current role: {job_title}
> Desired role: {target_position}
> Key qualifications demonstrated: {three_examples_of_readiness}
> Leadership examples: {times_they_led_projects_or_people}
> Business impact: {measurable_results_they_delivered}
> Areas they'll grow into: {skills_they'll_develop_in_new_role}
> Urgency: {ready_now/ready_in_6_months/flight_risk_if_not_promoted}
> 
> Write a 400-450 word promotion recommendation that makes a compelling business case. Include specific examples and metrics. Address any potential concerns about their readiness.

When to use it: When promotion planning meetings are next week and you need a strong written case for someone you want to advance.

Pro tip: Include examples of them performing at the next level already, not just doing their current job well—decision makers want to see evidence they can handle bigger challenges.

Pro tip: Give them a small piece of the target role to own right now—acting experience is more valuable than theoretical development and helps them decide if they really want the promotion.

## Team and Organizational Reviews

> You are writing a team performance summary for your manager covering your direct reports' year-end reviews.
> 
> Team: {team_name_or_function}
> Team size: {number_of_direct_reports}
> Overall team performance: {exceeded_goals/met_goals/mixed_results}
> Top performers: {names_and_brief_achievements}
> Development cases: {names_and_support_needed}
> Retention concerns: {flight_risks_and_why}
> Promotion recommendations: {who_is_ready_for_advancement}
> Budget implications: {salary_increases/bonus_recommendations}
> 
> Write a 500-600 word team summary that gives your manager a clear picture of your team's performance, development needs, and retention risks. Include specific recommendations for compensation and advancement.

When to use it: When your manager needs a comprehensive overview of your team's performance for calibration meetings or budget planning.

Pro tip: Rank your people in order of retention priority, not just performance—your manager needs to know where to focus limited promotion and compensation resources.
