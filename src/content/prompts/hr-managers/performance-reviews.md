## Annual Review Season

> You are an HR manager writing a year-end performance review for a high performer being considered for promotion.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}, {tenure} at the company
> Rating: {exceeds_expectations / meets_expectations / below_expectations}
> Top wins this year:
> {three_specific_achievements}
> Growth areas:
> {two_development_opportunities}
> Promotion readiness: {ready_now / needs_6_months / needs_12_months}
> Review tone: {supportive / direct / formal}
>
> Write a 400-500 word performance review using the SBI model. Open with specific recognition of their biggest win. Address growth areas as development opportunities in the middle section. Close with clear next steps for promotion timeline and two actionable goals for the coming year.

**When to use it:** December through February when you're rushing through annual reviews and need consistent quality across multiple high-performer evaluations.

**Pro tip:** If the employee is promotion-ready, mention specific leadership opportunities they could take on. If not ready, give concrete skill gaps they need to close first.

> You are writing a mid-year check-in review for an employee who started strong but performance has declined recently.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Department: {department}
> Months employed: {tenure_months}
> Strong start period: {initial_achievements}
> Recent concerns: {specific_performance_issues}
> Manager's relationship: {supportive / distant / new_manager}
> Suspected causes: {workload / personal / skill_gap / motivation}
>
> Write a 300-350 word mid-year review that acknowledges early success, addresses recent decline without being punitive, and creates a clear improvement plan. Use a coaching tone that assumes positive intent. Include specific metrics or behaviors where possible and end with a 90-day checkpoint plan.

**When to use it:** Mid-year reviews when you need to address performance dips while maintaining the employee relationship and motivation.

**Pro tip:** Always lead with what they did well initially - it shows the capability exists and frames the decline as solvable rather than a character flaw.

> You are conducting a performance review for a solid contributor who consistently meets expectations but never exceeds them.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Years with company: {tenure_years}
> Role: {job_title}
> Consistent strengths: {reliable_contributions}
> Areas where they could stretch: {growth_potential}
> Team impact: {positive / neutral / occasionally_negative}
> Career aspirations: {leadership / specialist / content_current_level}
> Company needs: {need_growth / value_stability}
>
> Write a 250-300 word review that values their consistency while encouraging growth. Avoid the "meets expectations" trap that sounds mediocre. Frame their reliability as a strength, then present one stretch opportunity that aligns with their interests. Keep tone appreciative but forward-looking.

**When to use it:** For your dependable team members who do solid work but might be coasting - you want to encourage growth without making them feel undervalued.

**Pro tip:** Don't push for growth if they're genuinely content and you need stability. Sometimes "meets expectations" employees are your backbone - acknowledge that value.

## Difficult Conversations

> You are writing a formal performance improvement plan (PIP) review after an employee has failed to meet basic job requirements.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Specific performance failures: {documented_issues}
> Previous feedback given: {verbal_warnings_dates}
> Impact on team/company: {business_consequences}
> Improvement timeline: {30_60_90_days}
> Success metrics: {specific_measurable_goals}
> Support provided: {training_resources_manager_time}
>
> Write a 400-450 word formal PIP review that is direct but not punitive, legally compliant, and focused on specific behaviors and outcomes. Include exact success criteria, timeline, consequences of non-improvement, and available support. Maintain professional tone throughout while being absolutely clear about expectations.

**When to use it:** When informal coaching hasn't worked and you need to begin formal documentation before potential termination.

**Pro tip:** Have HR review this before the employee meeting. PIPs are legal documents - every word matters if this ends in termination.

> Accommodation options: {flexible_schedule / time_off / reduced_responsibilities / eap}

**When to use it:** When good employees are struggling due to personal issues and you need to document performance while offering appropriate support.

**Pro tip:** Never put details about their personal situation in writing unless they've formally requested accommodation. Focus on work impact and support offered.

> You are documenting a review conversation with an employee who disagrees with their performance rating.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Your rating: {your_assessment}
> Their self-rating: {their_assessment}
> Specific disagreement: {what_they_dispute}
> Evidence you have: {documentation_metrics_examples}
> Their counterarguments: {their_perspective}
> Areas of agreement: {common_ground}
> Next steps: {additional_review / stand_by_rating / compromise}
>
> Write a 300-350 word review summary that documents both perspectives professionally, acknowledges their concerns while supporting your assessment with specific evidence. Include any agreements reached and next steps for resolution. Maintain respectful tone while being clear about your position.

**When to use it:** After performance review meetings where employees strongly disagree with your assessment and you need to document the conversation.

**Pro tip:** Always end these reviews by acknowledging you heard their concerns, even if you don't change the rating. Feeling heard often matters more than the final score.

**Pro tip:** Lead with "right person, wrong role" messaging if true. People accept role changes much better than personal capability critiques.

## Team Leadership Reviews

> You are reviewing a team leader whose individual performance is strong but team results are inconsistent.
>
> Leader: {employee_name}
> Team size: {number_of_direct_reports}
> Individual contributions: {personal_achievements}
> Team performance: {metrics_outcomes_morale}
> Leadership style: {hands_on / delegating / micromanaging / absent}
> Team feedback: {what_direct_reports_say}
> Leadership experience: {new_to_leadership / experienced / been_in_role_years}
> Development areas: {specific_leadership_skills_needed}
>
> Write a 400-450 word leadership review that separates individual contributor success from management effectiveness. Address team results directly while providing specific leadership development guidance. Use examples that show the difference between doing the work and leading others to do the work.

**When to use it:** For managers who were promoted from individual contributor roles and are struggling with the leadership transition.

**Pro tip:** Many strong individual contributors fail at management because they keep doing the work instead of enabling others. Address this directly with concrete behavior changes.

## Remote and Hybrid Work Reviews

>

> Work location: {fully_remote / hybrid_schedule}
> Work quality: {specific_examples_of_excellence}
> Communication issues: {response_times / meeting_participation / updates}
> Collaboration challenges: {team_projects / cross_functional_work}
> Remote work tenure: {new_to_remote / experienced_remote_worker}
> Team integration: {connected / isolated / occasional_participant}
> Technology comfort: {strong / adequate / needs_support}
>
> Write a 300-350 word review that celebrates work quality while addressing remote work skills as distinct competencies. Provide specific guidance on virtual communication and collaboration expectations. Frame remote work skills as professional development, not personality issues.

**When to use it:** For remote workers who produce great individual work but struggle with the collaborative aspects of distributed teams.

**Pro tip:** Distinguish between communication preferences and communication requirements. Remote work demands more proactive communication than office work, regardless of personality.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What makes a good ChatGPT prompt for performance reviews?
A good prompt includes specific variables you can fill in (like employee name, achievements, and concerns), clear output requirements (word count, tone, format), and produces a finished draft you can edit lightly and use immediately. Avoid prompts that create templates or frameworks requiring additional work.

### How do I customize these prompts for different company cultures?
Adjust the tone variable in each prompt to match your culture (formal, supportive, direct) and modify the framework references if your company uses different performance models than SBI or STAR. The core structure works across different organizational styles.

### Can I use these prompts for self-evaluations and peer reviews?
These prompts are designed for manager-to-employee reviews. For self-evaluations, change "You are an HR manager writing" to "You are writing your own performance review" and adjust the perspective. For peer reviews, modify the relationship dynamic and focus on collaboration rather than management oversight.
