## Executive Summary Creation

> You are a Customer Success Manager writing an executive summary for a quarterly business review.
> 
> Client: {client_company_name}
> Relationship length: {months_or_years_as_customer}
> Primary stakeholder: {main_contact_name_and_title}
> Quarter performance: {exceeds_expectations / meets_expectations / below_expectations}
> Key wins this quarter:
> {three_to_four_wins_in_bullets}
> Main challenges faced:
> {two_challenges_with_context}
> Revenue impact: {dollar_figure_or_percentage}
> Contract status: {renewal_date_and_value}
> 
> Write a 250-300 word executive summary that opens with the biggest business impact, addresses challenges as opportunities, and closes with next quarter priorities. Use confident, data-driven language that positions our partnership as strategic.

**When to use it:** When you need the opening section of your QBR deck and want to lead with impact, not features.

**Pro tip:** If your client had a rough quarter, lead with usage growth or engagement metrics instead of pure ROI numbers.

## Performance Analysis and Metrics

> You are analyzing quarterly performance data for a customer success QBR presentation.
> 
> Client: {client_company_name}
> Baseline period: {previous_quarter_or_year}
> Current usage metrics: {key_performance_indicators}
> Goal achievement: {percentage_of_targets_met}
> User engagement: {login_frequency_or_feature_adoption}
> Business outcome: {revenue_growth_or_cost_savings}
> Benchmark comparison: {industry_average_or_peer_performance}
> Data confidence: {high / medium / low_data_quality}
> 
> Write a 400-450 word performance analysis that tells a story with the data. Structure as Problem-Solution-Impact-Proof. Include specific metrics but explain what they mean for business outcomes. Address any data limitations transparently. End with clear trajectory for next quarter.

**When to use it:** When you have strong data and need to build a compelling narrative around measurable success.

**Pro tip:** Lead with the metric that improved most dramatically—even if it's not the most important one, it creates positive momentum.

> You are creating a performance comparison analysis for a QBR with a competitive client.
> 
> Client: {client_company_name}
> Industry: {specific_industry_or_vertical}
> Company size: {employee_count_or_revenue_range}
> Our performance: {key_metrics_achieved}
> Industry benchmark: {average_performance_data}
> Top performer benchmark: {best_in_class_numbers}
> Competitive advantage: {unique_capabilities_or_results}
> Improvement opportunity: {biggest_gap_to_close}
> 
> Write a 350-400 word competitive performance analysis using the "Good-Better-Best" framework. Position their current performance as good, industry-leading as better, and our enhanced partnership as best. Include specific percentiles and rankings. Close with roadmap to top-tier performance.

**When to use it:** For competitive clients who respond to benchmarking and want to lead their industry.

**Pro tip:** If they're already top-performing, compare them to companies one size tier up instead of same-size peers.

> You are presenting quarterly metrics for a client who struggles with data interpretation.
> 
> Client: {client_company_name}
> Stakeholder background: {technical / business / executive}
> Key metric 1: {most_important_number}
> What it means: {business_impact_in_plain_language}
> Key metric 2: {second_priority_number}
> Success story: {specific_example_or_case_study}
> Trend direction: {improving / stable / declining}
> Next milestone: {specific_target_for_next_quarter}
> 
> Write a 250-300 word metrics explanation using simple, jargon-free language. Start each metric with "This means..." statements. Use analogies if helpful. Focus on business impact over technical details. Include one concrete success story that illustrates the numbers.

**When to use it:** When presenting to executives or stakeholders who want insights, not raw data.

**Pro tip:** Use percentage changes instead of absolute numbers when the baseline might not be familiar to them.

> 
> Client: {client_company_name}
> Expected outcome: {original_goal_or_prediction}
> Actual result: {what_really_happened}
> Variance: {percentage_difference}
> Root cause analysis: {why_results_differed}
> Learning captured: {insight_gained}
> Adjusted strategy: {how_approach_will_change}
> Confidence level: {high / medium / low_for_new_approach}
> 
> Write a 300-350 word performance variance analysis using the "Hypothesis-Test-Learn-Adapt" structure. Frame unexpected results as valuable learning, not failure. Include specific actions to capitalize on insights. Close with updated expectations and methodology.

**When to use it:** When actual results significantly differed from projections and you need to maintain credibility while adjusting course.

**Pro tip:** If results exceeded expectations, explore what you can replicate. If they fell short, focus on controllable factors going forward.

> You are creating a quarterly ROI calculation for budget justification discussions.
> 
> Client: {client_company_name}
> Investment amount: {annual_or_quarterly_contract_value}
> Time period: {months_of_usage_measured}
> Hard savings: {quantifiable_cost_reductions}
> Soft benefits: {efficiency_gains_or_time_saved}
> Revenue impact: {new_revenue_or_growth_attributed}
> Avoided costs: {problems_or_risks_prevented}
> Total quantified value: {dollar_amount_of_all_benefits}
> 
> Write a 350-400 word ROI analysis that builds a conservative business case. Structure as Investment-Returns-Methodology-Confidence. Show math clearly but don't over-engineer the calculation. Include both quantified and strategic value. End with ROI multiple and payback period.

**When to use it:** During budget planning season or when renewal decision makers need financial justification.

**Pro tip:** Include "avoided cost" scenarios like prevented downtime or compliance risks—they're often larger than direct savings.

## Stakeholder Communication

> You are drafting a QBR follow-up email to an executive sponsor who missed the meeting.
> 
> Executive: {executive_name_and_title}
> Relationship: {how_long_they_ve_been_sponsor}
> Meeting attendees: {who_represented_their_company}
> Key outcome: {most_important_decision_or_result}
> Action items: {specific_next_steps_agreed}
> Executive decision needed: {what_requires_their_input}
> Timeline: {when_decision_or_action_is_needed}
> Attachment: {qbr_deck_or_summary_document}
> 
> Write a 200-250 word executive follow-up email that gets to the point immediately. Lead with the business outcome, summarize key decisions, and include one clear ask. Use confident, peer-level language. Close with specific next contact timeline.

**When to use it:** When your main sponsor couldn't attend but needs to stay informed and potentially approve next steps.

**Pro tip:** Put the specific ask in bold or a separate line—executives scan emails quickly and need clear action items.
