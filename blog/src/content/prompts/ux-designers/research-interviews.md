## Discovery Interview Scripts

> You are a UX researcher preparing for user discovery interviews about a digital product redesign.
> 
> Product: {product_name}
> User type: {target_user_segment}
> Current pain points we know: {known_issues}
> Business goal: {primary_business_objective}
> Interview length: {30_45_60_minutes}
> User's experience level: {beginner_intermediate_expert}
> Tone: {conversational_formal_friendly}
> 
> Write a complete interview script with 12-15 open-ended questions. Start with warm-up questions about their background. Move to current behavior and pain points. End with future state and wish list questions. Include 2-3 follow-up probes for each main question. Format as a script the researcher can read directly from, 800-1000 words total.

**When to use it:** You have a user interview scheduled tomorrow and need a structured script that covers discovery without leading the participant.

**Pro tip:** Always include the user's experience level - beginners need more context-setting questions while experts can dive straight into nuanced workflow details.

> You are a UX researcher conducting interviews to understand why users abandon a specific workflow.
> 
> Workflow: {specific_user_workflow}
> Drop-off point: {where_users_typically_abandon}
> Product context: {mobile_app_web_platform_desktop}
> Users interviewed: {how_many_users_recruited}
> Interview format: {in_person_remote_moderated_unmoderated}
> Hypothesis: {your_best_guess_why_users_drop_off}
> Tone: {empathetic_neutral_investigative}
> 
> Create a 45-minute interview guide focused on uncovering abandonment triggers. Include task-based questions that walk through their last attempt. Add emotional state probes. Include questions about workarounds they've tried. Structure as: context questions (5 mins), workflow walkthrough (25 mins), alternatives exploration (10 mins), wrap-up (5 mins). 600-800 words.

**When to use it:** Your analytics show a specific drop-off point but you need to understand the human story behind the data.

**Pro tip:** Ask about their "last time" they tried the workflow rather than general behavior - specific instances reveal more honest details than hypothetical responses.

## Usability Testing Scripts

> You are a UX researcher facilitating a moderated usability test for a new feature release.
> 
> Feature: {feature_name_and_description}
> Test environment: {prototype_staging_live_product}
> User type: {new_existing_mixed_users}
> Task complexity: {simple_moderate_complex_workflow}
> Success metrics: {completion_time_error_rate_satisfaction}
> Testing method: {think_aloud_observation_task_based}
> Session length: {30_45_60_minutes}
> 
> Create a complete testing script with pre-task briefing, 4-6 realistic scenarios, and post-task questions. Include specific instructions for what to observe. Add prompts for when users get stuck. Structure the scenarios from simple to complex. Include emotional state check-ins. Format as a script the facilitator can follow exactly. 800-1000 words.

**When to use it:** You have usability sessions booked and need a structured script that captures both task completion and user sentiment.

**Pro tip:** Write scenarios as realistic goals the user would actually have, not feature tours - users behave differently when they have genuine motivation.

> You are a UX researcher testing the onboarding flow for new users who signed up but haven't completed setup.
> 
> Product: {product_name}
> Onboarding stage: {signup_to_first_value}
> Drop-off data: {where_most_users_abandon}
> New user type: {technical_non_technical_mixed}
> Onboarding length: {steps_or_time_estimate}
> Previous version results: {what_didnt_work_before}
> Test format: {live_prototype_click_through}
> 
> Design a 45-minute test session that identifies onboarding friction points. Include pre-task questions about their expectations. Create realistic scenarios for why they signed up. Add think-aloud instructions. Include recovery testing when they get confused. Structure as: expectation setting (5 mins), onboarding walkthrough (30 mins), reflection questions (10 mins). 600-800 words with specific observation prompts.

**When to use it:** Your trial-to-paid conversion is low and you suspect the onboarding experience is losing users before they see value.

**Pro tip:** Test with people who match your actual signup demographics, not internal team members - domain knowledge blindness is real in onboarding flows.

> Success measure: {speed_accuracy_preference_satisfaction}
> Session recording: {screen_recording_notes_both}
>

Structure as counterbalanced testing with qualitative follow-up. Add instructions for handling user frustration with either product. 700-900 words with bias-prevention guidelines.

**When to use it:** You need objective evidence of your product's strengths and weaknesses compared to the competition for roadmap prioritization.

**Pro tip:** Randomize which product users try first and use identical task language for both - small wording differences can skew results significantly.

## Stakeholder Interview Questions

> You are a UX researcher interviewing a product stakeholder to understand business requirements before starting user research.
> 
> Stakeholder role: {product_manager_ceo_sales_director}
> Project: {specific_project_or_initiative}
> Timeline pressure: {tight_standard_flexible_deadline}
> Success metrics: {known_unknown_contested}
> User research budget: {limited_standard_extensive}
> Stakeholder research experience: {research_savvy_new_to_ux_resistant}
> Political context: {smooth_project_contested_direction}
> 
> Write questions that align business goals with research approach. Include questions about assumptions, constraints, and success definitions. Ask about user access and internal data availability. Probe for unstated concerns or political considerations. Structure as collaborative planning session: current state (10 mins), desired outcomes (15 mins), research parameters (10 mins). 500-700 words with diplomatic phrasing.

**When to use it:** You're starting a new project and need to understand what the business actually needs from your research before designing the study.

**Pro tip:** Ask what would surprise them most in user feedback - stakeholder assumptions often reveal the most important things to test.

> You are a UX researcher presenting research findings to stakeholders who expected different results.
> 
> Research findings: {brief_summary_of_key_insights}
> Stakeholder expectations: {what_they_expected_to_hear}
> Business implications: {how_findings_affect_roadmap}
> Audience: {product_team_executives_mixed_group}
> Presentation format: {meeting_deck_report_workshop}
> Follow-up needed: {decisions_more_research_action_planning}
> Resistance level: {open_minded_skeptical_defensive}
> 
> Create a presentation script that introduces unexpected findings diplomatically. Include data storytelling that connects user insights to business impact. Add preemptive responses to likely objections. Structure as narrative: what we learned, why it matters, what it means for decisions. Include specific quotes and examples. End with recommended actions. 700-900 words with transition phrases and stakeholder management guidance.

**When to use it:** Your research findings contradict what stakeholders wanted to hear and you need to present results that will actually influence decisions.

**Pro tip:** Lead with a finding they agree with before introducing contradictory evidence - it establishes your credibility before challenging their assumptions.
