## Project Kickoff Briefs

> You are a UX designer writing a design brief for a new project kickoff meeting with a client.
> 
> Project: {project_name}
> Client: {client_company_name}
> Timeline: {project_duration_weeks} weeks
> Budget range: {budget_range}
> Key stakeholders: {stakeholder_names_and_roles}
> Main business objective: {primary_goal}
> Target users: {user_demographic_description}
> Current pain points: {three_specific_problems}
> Success metrics: {how_success_measured}
> 
> Write a 400-500 word project design brief that opens with the business context, defines the design challenge in one clear sentence, outlines scope boundaries, and closes with next steps for the first sprint. Use confident, solution-focused language that demonstrates expertise while staying accessible to non-designers.

**When to use it:** Right after your sales call when the client says yes and you need to document what you agreed to before memories fade.

**Pro tip:** Always include one constraint they didn't mention but you know will come up - like mobile responsiveness or accessibility requirements.

> You are a UX designer creating a brief for a website redesign project with tight budget constraints.
> 
> Client: {client_name}
> Current website: {existing_site_url}
> Industry: {business_sector}
> Budget: {total_budget_amount}
> Timeline: {weeks_to_completion}
> Must-keep elements: {features_to_preserve}
> Biggest complaint: {main_user_frustration}
> Conversion goal: {what_users_should_do}
> Competitor benchmark: {competitor_site_example}
> 
> Write a 300-350 word design brief that acknowledges budget realities upfront, prioritizes high-impact changes, and sets realistic expectations. Frame limitations as focused strategy, not corners cut. Include three specific deliverables and timeline milestones.

**When to use it:** When a small business client loves your work but has champagne taste and beer budget.

**Pro tip:** Lead with what you can accomplish rather than what you can't - clients remember the first frame you set.

**Pro tip:** Include estimated revenue impact in dollars, not just percentage improvements - executives think in money, not conversion rates.

> 
> App concept: {app_idea_description}
> Target users: {primary_user_group}
> Core feature: {main_functionality}
> Platform: {ios_android_both}
> Testing timeline: {weeks_until_user_testing}
> Prototype fidelity: {low_medium_high}
> User scenarios: {three_key_use_cases}
> Business hypothesis: {assumption_to_validate}
> Success criteria: {testing_goals}
> 
> Write a 280-320 word brief for a prototype project. Focus on what needs to be validated, not what needs to be built. Specify exactly which screens are required, what level of interactivity is needed, and how results will inform next decisions. Keep language hypothesis-driven and experiment-focused.

**When to use it:** When stakeholders want to build everything and you need to focus them on testing one core assumption first.

**Pro tip:** Always specify what you're NOT building in the prototype - clients assume if it's not mentioned, it's included.

## Scope Change Documentation

> You are a UX designer documenting a mid-project scope change request from a client.
> 
> Original scope: {initial_deliverables}
> Requested addition: {new_feature_or_change}
> Reason for change: {client_justification}
> Impact on timeline: {additional_weeks_needed}
> Budget implications: {extra_cost_amount}
> Current project phase: {discovery_design_testing_dev}
> Risk to launch date: {high_medium_low}
> Alternative solutions: {two_lighter_options}
> Decision deadline: {when_client_must_decide}
> 
> Write a 250-300 word scope change brief that acknowledges the request positively, explains implications clearly, and offers options. Present the full addition, a lighter version, and a post-launch option. End with a clear decision point and timeline for response.

**When to use it:** When the client says "just one more thing" halfway through the project and you need to document impact professionally.

**Pro tip:** Always offer a "Phase 2" option - it shows you're thinking long-term and often becomes the preferred choice.

> You are a UX designer explaining why a client's requested design change would hurt user experience.
> 
> Requested change: {specific_design_modification}
> Client's reasoning: {why_they_want_it}
> UX principle it violates: {usability_heuristic}
> User research that contradicts it: {data_or_findings}
> Potential negative impact: {user_experience_consequence}
> Your recommended alternative: {better_solution}
> Compromise position: {middle_ground_option}
> Reference example: {competitor_or_case_study}
> 
> Write a 200-250 word brief that respectfully pushes back on the request. Lead with understanding their goal, explain the UX concern with specific user impact, propose an alternative that meets their need differently. Use collaborative language that positions you as a partner, not an obstacle.

**When to use it:** When the client wants a carousel, popup, or other conversion-killing element because they saw it on a competitor's site.

**Pro tip:** Reference a brand they admire that does it the right way - social proof works better than UX theory.

> You are a UX designer creating a brief for emergency design changes due to user feedback.
> 
> Original launch date: {planned_go_live}
> Critical feedback: {main_user_complaint}
> Feedback source: {beta_users_support_tickets_reviews}
> Severity level: {blocks_workflow_confusing_annoying}
> Quick fix available: {yes_no_partial}
> Time to implement: {hours_or_days_needed}
> Delay consequences: {impact_of_postponing_launch}
> Stakeholder preference: {fix_now_or_ship_then_fix}
> Resource availability: {team_member_capacity}
> 
> Write a 180-220 word emergency brief that presents the situation, impact, and options clearly. Recommend either fixing before launch or shipping with a hotfix timeline. Include specific task assignments and timeline for resolution. Use urgent but calm language that shows control of the situation.

**When to use it:** When user testing reveals a critical issue 48 hours before launch and everyone's asking "what do we do now?"

**Pro tip:** Always have a rollback plan ready - mention it in the brief to show you're thinking about risk management.

> Key differences: {how_needs_vary}
> Design implications: {what_must_change}
> Research requirements: {user_interviews_needed}
> Timeline addition: {extra_weeks_required}
> Resource needs: {additional_team_members}
> Revenue potential: {business_opportunity_size}

**When to use it:** When initial success makes the client want to expand to enterprise customers, different countries, or new user types.

**Pro tip:** Propose a small pilot with the new segment before full expansion - it reduces risk and gives you real data to work with.

**Pro tip:** Celebrate the wins first - stakeholders need to hear what succeeded before they'll trust your recommendations for improvement.

## User Research Integration

> You are a UX designer creating a brief that incorporates new user research findings into an existing design.
> 
> Research method: {interviews_surveys_usability_testing}
> Key finding: {most_important_discovery}
> Users affected: {percentage_or_segment}
> Current design assumption: {what_we_thought_was_true}
> Reality discovered: {what_research_revealed}
> Design changes needed: {specific_modifications_required}
> Implementation complexity: {easy_moderate_major_rebuild}
> Timeline impact: {days_or_weeks_affected}
> Confidence level: {how_sure_we_are}
> 
> Write a 300-340 word brief that presents research findings and design implications. Explain what changed your understanding, what stays the same, and what needs to be modified. Use confident language that shows research expertise while acknowledging what you learned.

**When to use it:** When user interviews reveal that your beautiful design solves the wrong problem entirely.

**Pro tip:** Include one quote from research that captures the insight perfectly - stakeholders remember stories better than statistics.

**Pro tip:** Frame accessibility as expanding market reach - "15% more potential customers" resonates better than compliance requirements.
