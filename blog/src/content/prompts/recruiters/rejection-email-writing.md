## Post-Interview Rejections

> You are a recruiter writing a rejection email to a candidate who interviewed well but wasn't selected.
> 
> Candidate: {candidate_name}
> Position: {job_title}
> Interview stage: {final_round / second_round / panel_interview}
> Reason for rejection: {specific_reason_like_experience_gap_or_culture_fit}
> Positive feedback: {one_specific_strength_they_showed}
> Company tone: {warm / professional / direct}
> Future consideration: {yes / no / maybe_for_other_roles}
> 
> Write a 150 to 200 word rejection email that opens with appreciation for their time, includes one specific positive observation from the interview, explains the decision without being harsh, and closes with encouragement. Use a subject line format: "Update on your {job_title} application".

**When to use it:** When you've just finished final interviews and need to notify strong candidates who didn't make the cut.

**Pro tip:** Reference something specific they said in the interview - it shows you were listening and makes the rejection feel less automated.

> You are a recruiter rejecting a candidate who bombed their technical interview but seemed like a decent person.
> 
> Candidate: {candidate_name}
> Position: {job_title}
> Interview type: {technical / coding / skills_assessment}
> What went wrong: {specific_gap_like_javascript_knowledge_or_problem_solving}
> Years of experience: {experience_level}
> Tone: {encouraging / direct / developmental}
> Suggest improvement: {yes / no}
> Keep on file: {yes / no}
> 
> Write a 120 to 180 word rejection email that acknowledges their effort, gently addresses the skills gap without being brutal, and ends on a constructive note. If they're junior, be more encouraging. If they're senior, be more direct about expectations.

**When to use it:** After technical interviews where candidates clearly lacked required skills but you want to maintain a positive employer brand.

**Pro tip:** For junior candidates, suggest specific resources or skills to develop. For senior candidates, keep it shorter and more professional.

> You are a recruiter writing to a candidate who was overqualified and likely to leave quickly.
> 
> Candidate: {candidate_name}
> Position: {job_title}
> Their experience level: {much_more_senior_than_role_requires}
> Salary expectation: {above / below / at_budget}
> Interview impression: {engaged / seemed_bored / desperate}
> Reason for applying: {career_change / desperate / unclear}
> Company size: {startup / midsize / enterprise}
> 
> Write a 100 to 150 word rejection email that respectfully acknowledges their qualifications while explaining the mismatch. Frame it as protecting their career growth rather than rejecting them. Be diplomatic about overqualification without being condescending.

**When to use it:** When you've interviewed someone who's clearly too senior for the role and likely to be unhappy or leave quickly.

**Pro tip:** Mention you'll keep them in mind for more senior roles if you genuinely might - it softens the blow and keeps the door open.

**Pro tip:** Never mention specific personality traits in writing - stick to vague terms like "team dynamics" or "working style alignment."

## Volume Screening Rejections

> You are a recruiter sending rejection emails after resume screening for a high-volume role.
> 
> Position: {job_title}
> Number of applications: {application_volume}
> Key requirements they missed: {specific_requirement_like_years_experience_or_certification}
> Application quality: {clearly_unqualified / borderline / mass_applying}
> Company brand: {well_known / growing / startup}
> Tone: {brief / warm / professional}
> Talent pool interest: {yes / no}
> 
> Write a 60 to 90 word rejection email that's polite but efficient. Thank them briefly, mention high competition, and close professionally. Keep it short since you're sending hundreds. Include "we'll keep your resume on file for 12 months" if you mean it.

**When to use it:** When you've screened 200+ resumes and need to reject obvious non-fits quickly while maintaining professionalism.

**Pro tip:** Set up an email template in your ATS with this prompt output - you'll use it constantly for volume roles.

> You are a recruiter rejecting candidates who applied without reading the job description properly.
> 
> Position: {job_title}
> Location requirement: {specific_city_or_remote_policy}
> Salary range: {budget_range}
> Key requirement missed: {location / experience / certification / visa_status}
> Application effort: {generic_cover_letter / no_cover_letter / obvious_mass_application}
> Tone: {brief / slightly_frustrated / professional}
> 
> Write a 50 to 80 word rejection email that's polite but clear about the basic requirement they missed. Don't be rude, but don't encourage more unsuitable applications. Keep it factual and brief.

**When to use it:** When candidates clearly didn't read the job post and are wasting your time with obviously unsuitable applications.

**Pro tip:** Include the specific requirement they missed (location, visa, experience) so they understand why they were rejected.

**When to use it:** For specialized positions where candidates apply despite clearly lacking required qualifications.

**Pro tip:** For small gaps, mention they can reapply when they have more experience. For huge gaps, keep it shorter and more final.

## Feedback-Heavy Rejections

> You are a recruiter providing detailed feedback to a candidate who specifically requested it.
> 
> Candidate: {candidate_name}
> Position: {job_title}
> Interview performance: {specific_strengths_and_weaknesses}
> Decision factors: {what_other_candidate_had_that_they_lacked}
> Areas for improvement: {two_to_three_specific_development_areas}
> Positive attributes: {what_they_did_well}
> Future potential: {could_be_good_fit_later / probably_not_right_field}
> Tone: {constructive / encouraging / direct}
> 
> Write a 250 to 300 word rejection email that provides specific, actionable feedback. Structure it as: appreciation, decision explanation, specific strengths, areas for development, encouragement for future. Be honest but constructive.

**When to use it:** When candidates explicitly ask for interview feedback and you have time to provide meaningful development insights.

**Pro tip:** Only provide detailed feedback if you genuinely have time and insights to share - generic feedback is worse than no feedback.

**Pro tip:** Give specific suggestions like "try freelance projects" or "look for roles at smaller companies" rather than vague advice like "get more experience."

**When to use it:** When technically strong candidates don't get hired due to leadership, communication, or cultural fit concerns.

**Pro tip:** Connect the soft skill requirement to specific job duties so it doesn't sound like arbitrary personal judgment.

> 
> Write a 120 to 160 word rejection email that's honest about the compensation gap while maintaining the relationship. Don't reveal exact budget numbers but be clear the gap couldn't be bridged. Keep it professional and non-judgmental about their expectations.

**When to use it:** When compensation expectations are too far apart to make an offer, but you want to preserve the relationship.

**Pro tip:** Don't give specific budget numbers, but be clear whether it's a small gap that might close later or a fundamental mismatch.
