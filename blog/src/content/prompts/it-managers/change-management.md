## System Implementation Announcements

> You are an IT Manager announcing a new system implementation to end users.
> 
> System name: {system_name}
> Go-live date: {implementation_date}
> Affected departments: {departments_affected}
> Key benefits: {three_main_benefits}
> Training start date: {training_date}
> Your contact for questions: {support_contact}
> Tone: {reassuring / direct / enthusiastic}
> Downtime required: {yes_with_duration / no}
> 
> Write a 250 to 300 word announcement email. Lead with the benefit to their daily work. Include a clear timeline with three key dates they need to know. Address training availability. End with specific next steps and where to get help.

**When to use it:** When your project sponsor needs the go-live announcement drafted and you're three weeks out from implementation.

**Pro tip:** If the system replaces something people love, lead with continuity ("Your favorite reports will still be available") before highlighting improvements.

> You are an IT Manager writing a follow-up message about system implementation delays.
> 
> Original system: {system_name}
> Original go-live date: {original_date}
> New go-live date: {revised_date}
> Reason for delay: {technical_issue / vendor_delay / integration_complexity}
> What's been completed: {progress_made}
> Impact on users: {minimal / significant / training_reschedule}
> Compensation offered: {none / extended_support / additional_training}
> 
> Write a 200 to 250 word delay notification. Start with the new date upfront. Explain the reason without technical jargon. Highlight what progress has been made. End with revised timeline and what users should do now.

**When to use it:** When you're calling stakeholders at 4pm on Friday to tell them go-live is pushed back two weeks.

**Pro tip:** Always include what's already been completed successfully - it shows the project is progressing, just slower than planned.

> You are an IT Manager announcing emergency system maintenance affecting business operations.
> 
> System affected: {system_name}
> Maintenance window: {start_date_time} to {end_date_time}
> Business impact: {no_access / read_only / performance_issues}
> Reason: {security_patch / hardware_failure / performance_fix}
> Workaround available: {yes_describe / no}
> Emergency contact: {after_hours_support}
> Departments most affected: {critical_departments}
> 
> Write a 150 to 200 word urgent maintenance notice. Start with the maintenance window and impact. Explain why this can't wait for normal maintenance hours. Provide workaround steps if available. Include escalation path for critical issues during the window.

**When to use it:** When your monitoring alerts are going off and you need to communicate emergency maintenance starting in two hours.

**Pro tip:** Send this 2-4 hours before maintenance starts - enough time for people to prepare, not so much they forget and get surprised.

> You are an IT Manager writing a system retirement announcement to long-term users.
> 
> System being retired: {legacy_system_name}
> Final access date: {sunset_date}
> Replacement system: {new_system_name}
> Data migration status: {automatic / manual_export_required / completed}
> Years the old system has been in use: {system_age}
> Training available for new system: {training_details}
> Historical data access: {archived / migrated / export_required}
> 
> Write a 300 to 350 word retirement announcement with a nostalgic but forward-looking tone. Acknowledge the history and reliability of the old system. Clearly explain data preservation. Outline the migration timeline with specific action items for users. End with enthusiasm for the new system's capabilities.

**When to use it:** When you're sunsetting that 15-year-old system everyone depends on but complains about constantly.

**Pro tip:** Include a brief "what made the old system great" section - people need to feel heard before they'll embrace change.

> You are an IT Manager announcing successful system rollout completion to project stakeholders.
> 
> System name: {system_name}
> Rollout completion date: {completion_date}
> Users successfully migrated: {user_count}
> Departments now live: {departments}
> Key metrics achieved: {performance_improvements}
> Outstanding issues: {minor_items / none / being_addressed}
> Next phase: {optimization / expansion / maintenance_mode}
> Support availability: {ongoing_support_details}
> 
> Write a 275 to 325 word success announcement. Lead with the achievement and user adoption numbers. Highlight early wins and positive feedback. Address any remaining work transparently. Thank key contributors. Close with what users can expect in the coming weeks.

**When to use it:** When you've just moved the last department to the new system and want to declare victory before moving to the next project.

**Pro tip:** Include 2-3 specific user quotes or early success metrics - stakeholders want proof the change is working, not just your opinion.

## Training and Adoption Communications

> You are an IT Manager creating a training announcement for mandatory system training.
> 
> System requiring training: {system_name}
> Training format: {virtual / in_person / self_paced}
> Session duration: {training_length}
> Available dates: {training_schedule}
> Registration deadline: {deadline_date}
> Mandatory for: {affected_roles}
> Training content focus: {key_skills_covered}
> Manager approval required: {yes / no}
> 
> Write a 225 to 275 word training announcement. Start with why the training matters to their job performance. Include clear registration instructions. Address common concerns about time away from work. End with what they'll be able to do after training that they can't do now.

**When to use it:** When L&D asks you to drive registration for training sessions that are at 12% capacity two weeks before launch.

**Pro tip:** Include the business consequence of not attending - "Without training, you won't have system access after [date]" drives registration better than benefits.

> You are an IT Manager writing a training effectiveness follow-up to department heads.
> 
> System trained on: {system_name}
> Training completion rate: {completion_percentage}
> Departments with low completion: {lagging_departments}
> Common user questions post-training: {frequent_issues}
> Performance metrics since training: {adoption_data}
> Additional support needed: {extra_help_required}
> Manager actions requested: {specific_requests}
> Follow-up timeline: {next_steps_timing}
> 
> Write a 300 to 350 word training effectiveness report for department heads. Present completion data first. Highlight departments excelling at adoption. Address lagging areas without blame. Request specific manager support actions. Include timeline for follow-up assessments.

**When to use it:** Three weeks post-training when half the people still aren't using the system properly and department heads are asking why.

**Pro tip:** Frame low adoption as a support opportunity, not a compliance failure - you'll get more manager cooperation.

> You are an IT Manager announcing additional training resources for struggling users.
> 
> System: {system_name}
> Original training date: {initial_training}
> Additional resources: {office_hours / video_tutorials / peer_mentors}
> Common struggle areas: {specific_challenges}
> Resource availability: {when_and_how_to_access}
> Time commitment: {expected_duration}
> Success definition: {what_good_looks_like}
> No-judgment policy: {reassurance_message}
> 
> Write a 200 to 250 word supportive follow-up message. Normalize the need for additional help. Describe resources without making them feel remedial. Include success stories from others who used additional support. Make accessing help feel easy and stigma-free.

**When to use it:** When your help desk tickets show the same five people submitting the same basic questions every day.

**Pro tip:** Lead with "Many users have found additional practice helpful" rather than "Some users are struggling" - same message, less shame.

## Issue Resolution and Crisis Communications

> You are an IT Manager communicating about a significant system outage to affected users.
> 
> System down: {system_name}
> Outage start time: {outage_began}
> Estimated resolution: {expected_fix_time}
> Root cause: {technical_cause_in_plain_english}
> Business processes affected: {impacted_operations}
> Temporary workaround: {manual_process_if_available}
> Update frequency: {how_often_updates_provided}
> Escalation contact: {urgent_issues_contact}
> 
> Write a 200 to 250 word outage communication. Lead with current status and estimated fix time. Explain the cause without technical jargon. Provide specific workaround steps if available. Commit to update frequency. Acknowledge the business impact with empathy.

**When to use it:** When your primary system is down at 9am on Monday and 200 people are asking when they can do their jobs again.

**Pro tip:** Give time estimates in ranges ("2-4 hours") rather than specific times ("11:30am") - you'll miss specific times and lose credibility.

> You are an IT Manager providing an outage resolution and lessons learned update.
> 
> System restored: {system_name}
> Total outage duration: {downtime_length}
> Root cause identified: {what_went_wrong}
> Immediate fix applied: {resolution_action}
> Preventive measures implemented: {future_prevention}
> Data integrity status: {no_loss / minimal_impact / restoration_required}
> Business impact summary: {operations_affected}
> Timeline for full normal operations: {complete_recovery_time}
> 
> Write a 300 to 350 word resolution update. Start with "System restored" and current status. Explain root cause and fix clearly. Detail prevention measures to rebuild confidence. Address data concerns directly. Thank users for patience and workaround efforts.

**When to use it:** Two hours after you've restored service and everyone wants to know it won't happen again tomorrow.

**Pro tip:** Include what early warning signs you'll monitor going forward - it shows you're actively preventing recurrence, not just hoping.

> You are an IT Manager explaining a security incident response to non-technical stakeholders.
> 
> Incident type: {security_breach_type}
> Discovery date: {when_found}
> Systems affected: {impacted_systems}
> Data potentially compromised: {data_types}
> Immediate containment actions: {response_steps_taken}
> External authorities notified: {regulatory_reporting}
> User actions required: {password_resets / monitoring / none}
> Investigation timeline: {ongoing_work}
> 
> Write a 350 to 400 word security incident notification. Balance transparency with avoiding panic. Explain containment actions taken. Clearly state what users must do immediately. Address data protection concerns. Provide timeline for investigation completion and final report.

**When to use it:** When your CISO hands you the incident details at 6am and says "we need to tell everyone by 10am."

**Pro tip:** Lead with containment actions taken before describing the incident - people need to know you're in control before they'll listen to details.

> You are an IT Manager communicating about planned system recovery testing.
> 
> Systems being tested: {recovery_systems}
> Test date and duration: {test_schedule}
> Business operations affected: {impacted_processes}
> Reason for testing: {regulatory / best_practice / post_incident}
> Expected user experience: {normal / degraded / no_access}
> Success criteria: {what_constitutes_successful_test}
> Rollback plan: {if_test_fails}
> Communication during test: {how_updates_provided}
> 
> Write a 275 to 325 word disaster recovery test announcement. Explain why testing is necessary and valuable. Set clear expectations for system availability. Address user concerns about disruption. Highlight the business continuity benefits. Include communication plan during testing.

**When to use it:** When compliance requires DR testing and you need to prepare users for potential system weirdness during the test window.

**Pro tip:** Frame it as "ensuring we can protect your work" rather than "testing if our systems work" - same activity, better positioning.

> You are an IT Manager providing a post-incident review summary to leadership.
> 
> Incident summary: {what_happened}
> Business impact: {revenue_time_productivity_loss}
> Response timeline: {key_milestones_in_resolution}
> Root cause analysis: {underlying_issue_identified}
> Process improvements implemented: {what_changed}
> Technology investments needed: {recommendations}
> Team performance assessment: {what_went_well}
> Similar incident prevention: {future_risk_mitigation}
> 
> Write a 400 to 450 word executive summary. Present business impact first with specific metrics. Highlight effective response actions. Present root cause analysis and corrective actions clearly. Request specific resources or approvals needed. End with confidence-building statements about future preparedness.

**When to use it:** When the CEO wants to understand what happened, why it won't happen again, and what it's going to cost to prevent.

**Pro tip:** Include positive observations about team response and existing processes that worked - leadership needs to know what's working well, not just what broke.
