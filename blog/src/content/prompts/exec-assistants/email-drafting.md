## Meeting Coordination & Scheduling

> You are an executive assistant coordinating a high-priority meeting between multiple senior stakeholders.
>
> Meeting purpose: {meeting_purpose}
> Attendees: {attendee_list_with_titles}
> Executive's name: {executive_name}
> Proposed date/time: {proposed_datetime}
> Meeting duration: {duration}
> Location/platform: {location_or_platform}
> Urgency level: {urgent / standard / flexible}
> Key preparation items: {prep_items}
>
> Write a 150-200 word scheduling email that confirms availability, states the agenda clearly, and includes all logistics. Use a professional but efficient tone that respects everyone's time. Include a clear call-to-action for confirmation.

**When to use it:** When your executive needs to pull together department heads or external partners for a decision-making meeting within 48-72 hours.

**Pro tip:** Always include time zones if attendees span multiple locations. ChatGPT often forgets this detail unless you add {timezone} as a variable.

> You are rescheduling a previously confirmed meeting due to an urgent conflict.
>
> Original meeting: {original_meeting_details}
> Attendees: {attendee_names}
> Reason for change: {brief_reason}
> New proposed options: {two_to_three_alternative_times}
> Executive name: {executive_name}
> Meeting importance: {critical / important / routine}
> Lead time given: {same_day / next_day / week_ahead}
>
> Write a 100-150 word rescheduling email that apologizes efficiently, explains briefly, and offers clear alternatives. Maintain authority while showing respect for disrupted schedules. End with a deadline for responses.

**When to use it:** When your executive's calendar implodes and you need to move confirmed meetings without burning relationships.

**Pro tip:** Include the original meeting time in the subject line format: "Rescheduling: [Meeting Name] - Originally [Date]" for easy reference.

> You are following up on a meeting invitation that hasn't received responses from key attendees.
>
> Meeting details: {meeting_name_date_time}
> Non-responders: {list_of_names_and_roles}
> Meeting organizer: {executive_name}
> Days since original invite: {number_of_days}
> Meeting urgency: {high / medium / standard}
> Alternative if no response: {backup_plan}
> Response deadline: {specific_date_time}
>
> Write a 120-180 word follow-up email that creates gentle urgency without being pushy. Reference the original invitation, restate key details, and give a clear deadline for responses. Include what happens if they don't respond.

**When to use it:** When half your invitees have confirmed but critical stakeholders are radio silent 24-48 hours before the meeting.

**Pro tip:** Mention the confirmed attendees by name early in the email. Social proof works even in follow-ups.

> You are sending a pre-meeting briefing email to ensure attendees come prepared.
>
> Meeting name: {meeting_name}
> Date and time: {meeting_datetime}
> Attendees: {attendee_list}
> Key decisions needed: {main_decisions}
> Documents to review: {document_list}
> Data/metrics to bring: {required_data}
> Executive hosting: {executive_name}
> Meeting duration: {duration}
>
> Write a 200-250 word pre-meeting email sent 24-48 hours ahead that outlines expectations, required preparation, and agenda flow. Use bullet points for clarity. Include attachments list and emphasize time efficiency.

**When to use it:** Before quarterly reviews, budget meetings, or strategy sessions where unprepared attendees kill productivity.

**Pro tip:** Send this exactly 36 hours before the meeting. Too early and people forget; too late and they can't prepare properly.

## Client and Stakeholder Communication

> You are communicating a significant delay or setback to an important client on behalf of your executive.
>
> Client name: {client_name}
> Project/deliverable: {project_details}
> Original deadline: {original_date}
> New timeline: {revised_timeline}
> Reason for delay: {brief_explanation}
> Mitigation steps: {actions_being_taken}
> Executive name: {executive_name}
> Compensation offered: {discount_credit_or_none}
> Next update schedule: {when_next_communication}
>
> Write a 200-280 word email that owns the problem, explains the situation clearly, and maintains client confidence. Lead with accountability, not excuses. Include specific recovery actions and next steps.

**When to use it:** When your executive is in back-to-back meetings but a client needs immediate communication about missed deadlines or budget overruns.

**Pro tip:** Always include a specific date for the next update, even if you don't have full resolution yet. Clients hate communication black holes.

> Information needed: {specific_requirements}
> Why it's needed: {brief_justification}
> Current project status: {where_things_stand}

> Executive name: {executive_name}

**When to use it:** When projects stall because clients haven't provided contracts, approvals, content, or technical specifications you're waiting on.

**Pro tip:** Include examples of the format you need. A sample filename or document structure saves multiple back-and-forth emails.

## Internal Team Updates and Announcements

> You are announcing an organizational change that affects multiple departments.
>
> Change description: {what_is_changing}
> Effective date: {when_change_starts}
> Departments affected: {which_teams}
> Reason for change: {brief_business_justification}
> New processes: {how_things_will_work}
> Training provided: {support_available}
> Executive sponsor: {executive_name}
> Questions contact: {who_to_ask}
> Timeline for full implementation: {rollout_schedule}
>
> Write a 250-300 word internal announcement that explains the change clearly, addresses likely concerns preemptively, and provides clear next steps. Use a tone that's informative but reassuring.

**When to use it:** When your executive needs to communicate restructuring, new policies, system changes, or process updates to the broader organization.

**Pro tip:** Send these announcements on Tuesday or Wednesday mornings. Monday emails get buried and Friday announcements get forgotten over weekends.

## Project Status and Timeline Updates

> You are updating stakeholders on a project that is significantly behind schedule.
>
> Project name: {project_identifier}
> Original timeline: {initial_deadline}
> Current status: {percentage_complete}
> Main blockers: {specific_obstacles}
> Revised timeline: {new_realistic_deadline}
> Recovery plan: {steps_to_accelerate}
> Resource needs: {additional_support_required}
> Executive sponsor: {executive_name}
> Next milestone: {immediate_next_deliverable}
> Stakeholders affected: {who_needs_to_know}
>
> Write a 250-300 word project update that honestly assesses the situation, takes ownership of delays, and presents a credible recovery plan. Include specific actions and accountability measures.

**When to use it:** When major initiatives are running late and stakeholders need realistic timelines rather than optimistic promises that won't be met.
