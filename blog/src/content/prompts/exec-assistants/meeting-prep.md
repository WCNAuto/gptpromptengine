## Pre-Meeting Executive Briefings

> You are an executive assistant preparing a comprehensive briefing for your executive before a high-stakes client meeting.
> 
> Executive: {executive_name}
> Client company: {client_company_name}
> Meeting purpose: {primary_objective}
> Key attendees: {attendee_names_and_titles}
> Previous meeting outcome: {last_interaction_summary}
> Current business relationship status: {relationship_status}
> Sensitive topics to avoid: {topics_to_avoid}
> Desired meeting outcome: {success_metrics}
> Meeting duration: {time_length}
> 
> Write a 400-500 word executive briefing document. Start with a two-sentence meeting overview. Include a "Key People" section with attendee backgrounds. Add a "Discussion Points" section with 3-4 strategic talking points. Include a "Potential Challenges" section flagging risks. Close with "Success Indicators" defining what a good outcome looks like.

**When to use it:** The night before any client meeting where your executive needs context on relationships, history, and strategic positioning.

**Pro tip:** Always include the last interaction date and outcome—executives often can't remember if the previous meeting was two weeks or two months ago.

> 
> Executive: {executive_name}
> Company performance: {exceeds_targets / meets_targets / below_targets}
> Key metrics this quarter: {three_key_numbers}
> Major wins: {two_to_three_achievements}
> Problem areas: {main_challenges}
> Board members attending: {board_member_names}
> Expected tough questions: {anticipated_concerns}
> Supporting data available: {reports_and_documents}
> Meeting tone needed: {confident / defensive / transparent}
> 
> Write a 350-400 word board meeting brief. Open with performance summary in two sentences. Include "Key Numbers to Reference" section with metrics formatted for easy recall. Add "Anticipated Questions & Responses" with 3-4 tough questions and suggested talking points. Close with "Supporting Materials" listing documents to reference.

**When to use it:** Before quarterly board meetings where your executive will face detailed questioning about company performance.

**Pro tip:** Include specific dollar figures and percentages in an easy-to-scan format—your executive might need to reference exact numbers mid-meeting.

**Pro tip:** Always include the actual dollar amounts or percentage terms your executive can reference—vague "market rate" language doesn't help in real negotiations.

## Client Meeting Preparation

> You are an executive assistant creating detailed talking points for a client retention meeting with a major account threatening to leave.
> 
> Client company: {client_name}
> Account value: {annual_revenue_amount}
> Relationship duration: {years_as_client}
> Primary complaint: {main_issue_causing_departure}
> Client contacts attending: {their_team_names}
> Our team attending: {our_attendees}
> Previous service failures: {past_problems}
> Competitors they're considering: {alternative_vendors}
> Retention budget available: {concession_limits}
> 
> Write 500-600 word client retention talking points. Open with relationship value acknowledgment. Include "Issue Resolution Plan" addressing their specific complaints with concrete solutions. Add "Value Reinforcement" section highlighting unique benefits they'd lose. Include "Retention Offers" with specific concessions available. Close with "Next Steps" commitment timeline.

**When to use it:** When a major client has expressed intent to leave and your executive needs a structured approach to save the relationship.

**Pro tip:** Include competitor comparison points—clients often mention alternatives to test your response, and your executive needs specific differentiation talking points ready.

Add "Investment Requirements" detailing capital, resources, and timeline needs. Include "Risk Assessment" covering market, operational, and financial risks. **Pro tip:** Include specific competitor revenue figures and market share data—strategic discussions need concrete benchmarks for realistic planning.
