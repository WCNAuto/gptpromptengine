## Client Boundary Setting

> You are a Virtual Assistant writing a professional boundary-setting email to a client who consistently sends urgent requests outside agreed hours.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Your working hours: {working_hours}
> Recent after-hours request: {specific_example}
> Relationship length: {months_or_years_working_together}
> Client's urgency level: {high / medium / reasonable}
> Your tone preference: {firm_but_warm / strictly_professional / collaborative}
> Emergency contact alternative: {phone_number / other_team_member / none}
> 
> Write a 200-300 word email that acknowledges their request, restates your availability, and provides a clear path forward for true emergencies. Start with appreciation for their business, explain your working hours as a business decision, and end with reassurance about response timing during business hours.

**When to use it:** When a client texts you at 11pm expecting an immediate response, or sends "URGENT" emails on weekends for non-urgent tasks.

**Pro tip:** Send this on a Tuesday morning when emotions aren't running high. Never send boundary emails in response to the triggering incident—wait 24 hours.

> You are a Virtual Assistant explaining to a client why their requested task is outside your agreed scope of work.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Task they requested: {specific_task_description}
> Your contracted services: {list_of_agreed_services}
> Complexity level: {simple_addition / major_scope_change / requires_new_skills}
> Timeline they want: {deadline_they_mentioned}
> Your relationship: {new_client / long_term_client / difficult_client}
> Suggested alternative: {refer_specialist / add_to_contract / decline_gracefully}
> 
> Write a 150-250 word email that politely declines the out-of-scope request while protecting the relationship. Reference your original agreement, explain why this falls outside it, and offer one concrete alternative solution. Use a consultative tone, not defensive.

**When to use it:** When clients ask you to "quickly" design a website, write legal documents, or handle tasks requiring specialized expertise you don't have.

**Pro tip:** Always offer an alternative, even if it's just referring them to another professional. Never leave clients hanging with just a "no."

> You are a Virtual Assistant ending a working relationship with a problematic client professionally.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Reason for ending relationship: {scope_creep / payment_issues / communication_problems / unrealistic_expectations}
> Notice period: {two_weeks / one_month / immediate}
> Outstanding work: {what_needs_to_be_completed}
> Handover requirements: {passwords / files / contact_lists}
> Final invoice amount: {amount_owed_to_you}
> Tone: {cordial / strictly_business / firm}
> 
> Write a 200-250 word professional termination email. Keep it brief, avoid explaining all the reasons why, focus on logistics and handover. Thank them for the opportunity, state your last working day clearly, and outline handover steps. No emotion, just business.

**When to use it:** When a client relationship has become unworkable and you've decided it's better for your business to end it cleanly.

**Pro tip:** Never send this email when you're angry. Draft it, save it, and review it 24 hours later. Focus entirely on logistics, not grievances.

## Scheduling and Coordination

> You are a Virtual Assistant rescheduling a client's important meeting due to a conflict that just arose.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Original meeting details: {date_time_participants}
> Reason for rescheduling: {client_emergency / venue_issue / key_participant_conflict}
> Urgency level: {same_day / within_24_hours / flexible}
> Alternative time options: {two_or_three_specific_alternatives}
> Meeting importance: {board_meeting / client_presentation / team_checkin}
> External participants: {number_of_external_people_affected}
> 
> Write a 150-200 word email to the meeting participants explaining the rescheduling. Apologize for short notice, briefly explain the necessity, offer specific alternative times, and request quick responses. Include a backup plan if none of the alternatives work. Professional and apologetic but not over-explanatory.

**When to use it:** When your client gets sick the morning of a big presentation, or when the venue cancels last minute.

**Pro tip:** Always offer at least two alternative time slots. Call the most important participants directly after sending the email to ensure they received it.

> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Trip purpose: {conference / client_meetings / site_visits}
> Cities and dates: {specific_itinerary_with_dates}
> Flight confirmation numbers: {list_confirmation_numbers}

> Meeting contacts: {key_people_they're_meeting}

> 
> Write a 300-400 word comprehensive trip confirmation email. Organize by city/date, include all confirmation numbers, provide contact information for each venue, and highlight any special arrangements. Include a "what to do if something goes wrong" section with your contact details.

**When to use it:** The week before a complex business trip, especially when it involves multiple cities or international travel.

**Pro tip:** Send this twice—once a week before travel and once 24 hours before departure. Include screenshots of mobile boarding passes when possible.

**When to use it:** Before your client attends important external meetings where preparation can make or break the outcome.

## Client Communication

> You are a Virtual Assistant delivering difficult news to a client about a project delay.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Project name: {specific_project_or_deliverable}
> Original deadline: {when_it_was_supposed_to_be_done}
> New realistic deadline: {revised_completion_date}
> Reason for delay: {vendor_issue / scope_change / unforeseen_complexity / resource_constraint}
> Impact on client: {how_this_affects_their_plans}
> Recovery plan: {specific_steps_to_get_back_on_track}
> Who else needs to know: {stakeholders_to_inform}
> 
> Write a 200-300 word email delivering the bad news directly but professionally. Lead with the delay announcement, briefly explain why, focus on the solution and revised timeline, and end with immediate next steps. Take responsibility without making excuses. Include a specific date when you'll provide the next update.

**When to use it:** As soon as you know a deadline will be missed, especially when the client has external commitments depending on your deliverable.

**Pro tip:** Always call first if the delay affects something time-sensitive, then follow up with this email for documentation. Never bury the bad news in the middle of a longer email.

> You are a Virtual Assistant explaining a complex technical issue to a non-technical client.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Technical problem: {specific_issue_in_plain_language}
> Impact on their business: {what_they_can't_do_right_now}
> Cause of the problem: {simplified_explanation}
> Solution being implemented: {what_you're_doing_to_fix_it}
> Timeline for resolution: {realistic_timeframe}
> Temporary workaround: {alternative_process_if_available}
> Cost implications: {none / minimal / significant}
> 
> Write a 250-350 word email that explains the technical issue in business terms. Avoid jargon, focus on business impact and solutions, and provide a clear timeline. Structure it as: What happened, Why it matters to them, What you're doing about it, When it will be resolved. Reassuring but honest tone.

**When to use it:** When your client's website crashes, their CRM stops syncing, or any technical problem affects their operations.

**Pro tip:** Use analogies from their industry to explain technical concepts. Always lead with the business impact, not the technical details.
