## Quarterly Business Review Presentations

> You are a Product Manager presenting quarterly roadmap results to executive leadership.
> 
> Product: {product_name}
> Quarter: {Q1/Q2/Q3/Q4} {year}
> Key metrics achieved: {three_metrics_with_numbers}
> Major releases shipped: {two_to_three_releases}
> Blockers encountered: {one_to_two_blockers}
> Next quarter priorities: {three_priorities}
> Budget impact: {over/under/on_budget}
> Team headcount: {current_team_size}
> 
> Write a 500-word executive summary for slides 2-4 of your QBR deck. Open with the strongest metric win. Structure as: Results Delivered, Lessons Learned, Next Quarter Focus. Use bullet points for key achievements. Close with one specific ask from leadership. Write in confident, data-driven tone suitable for C-level audience.

**When to use it:** The week before your QBR when you need executive slides that lead with wins and end with clear asks.

**Pro tip:** Put your biggest number first, even if it wasn't your primary goal. Executives scan for impact metrics in the opening sentences.

> You are a Product Manager explaining a delayed roadmap milestone to your VP of Product.
> 
> Product: {product_name}
> Original delivery date: {original_date}
> New delivery date: {revised_date}
> Delay reason: {technical/resource/market/dependency}
> Impact on other features: {cascading_effects}
> Mitigation steps taken: {actions_already_completed}
> Customer communication: {how_customers_were_informed}
> Team morale: {high/stable/concerned}
> 
> Write a 300-word delay explanation for your weekly leadership update. Lead with the new timeline and business impact. Explain the root cause in one paragraph without blame. Focus on forward-looking solutions and revised success metrics. Tone should be accountable and solution-focused.

**When to use it:** When you're writing the dreaded delay email and need to sound responsible without making excuses.

**Pro tip:** Always include what you learned that will prevent similar delays. Leadership wants to see pattern recognition, not just problem reporting.

> You are a Product Manager presenting roadmap trade-offs to a cross-functional leadership team.
> 
> Decision needed: {specific_feature_or_initiative}
> Option A: {first_approach_summary}
> Option A impact: {customer/revenue/technical_impact}
> Option B: {second_approach_summary}  
> Option B impact: {customer/revenue/technical_impact}
> Engineering effort: {Option_A_weeks} weeks vs {Option_B_weeks} weeks
> Customer research: {key_finding_from_user_research}
> Competitive landscape: {what_competitors_are_doing}
> Recommendation: {A_or_B}
> 
> Write a 400-word trade-off analysis for a leadership decision meeting. Present both options objectively in the first half. Include customer impact, engineering cost, and market timing for each. End with your clear recommendation and the specific risks of not deciding this week. Structure as: Context, Option Analysis, Recommendation, Next Steps.

**When to use it:** When you need leadership buy-in for a strategic direction and have to present competing approaches fairly.

**Pro tip:** Acknowledge the legitimate merits of the option you're not recommending. Leaders trust PMs who can argue both sides before landing on one.

**When to use it:** When market conditions force a roadmap change and you need to rally leadership around the new direction.

**Pro tip:** Lead with external market factors, not internal learnings. Pivots sound more strategic when driven by market opportunity than internal mistakes.

## Customer-Facing Roadmap Communications

> You are a Product Manager writing a public roadmap update for existing customers.
> 
> Product: {product_name}
> Update type: {quarterly/major_release/annual}
> Recently shipped: {two_to_three_completed_features}
> Currently building: {features_in_development}
> Coming next quarter: {upcoming_feature_preview}
> Customer-requested features: {most_requested_items_status}
> Performance improvements: {speed/reliability/scale_improvements}
> Platform updates: {infrastructure_changes_affecting_users}
> Beta opportunities: {features_customers_can_test_early}
> 
> Write a 400-word customer newsletter roadmap section. Lead with recently shipped value. Tease upcoming features without overpromising delivery dates. Address the top customer-requested feature explicitly. Maintain excitement while setting realistic expectations. Include one beta testing call-to-action. Tone should be partnership-focused and transparent.

**When to use it:** Monthly or quarterly customer communications when you need to show progress without creating unrealistic expectations.

**Pro tip:** Always mention the most-requested customer feature, even if it's not ready. Customers want to know you're listening, not just building your vision.

> You are a Product Manager responding to customer concerns about a deprecated feature.
> 
> Product: {product_name}
> Deprecated feature: {feature_being_removed}
> Deprecation date: {when_feature_goes_away}
> Replacement solution: {new_approach_or_feature}
> Migration path: {specific_steps_customers_take}
> Customer impact: {how_workflows_change}
> Support available: {migration_assistance_offered}
> Business reason: {why_deprecation_necessary}
> Timeline: {how_long_customers_have_to_adapt}
> 
> Write a 350-word deprecation communication for affected customers. Acknowledge the disruption upfront. Explain the business reasoning briefly without being defensive. Focus on the migration path and support resources available. Emphasize improved capabilities in the replacement solution. End with specific timeline and next steps for customers. Tone should be empathetic but confident in the strategic decision.

**When to use it:** When you need to communicate unpopular product decisions while maintaining customer trust and minimizing churn.

**Pro tip:** Lead with what customers gain from the change, not what they're losing. Frame deprecations as evolution, not removal.

> Integration requirements: {technical_setup_needed}
> Early access: {beta_or_preview_availability}
> Competitive differentiation: {unique_advantage_vs_alternatives}

Include availability and pricing transparency. End with clear call-to-action for interested customers. Write in excited but credible tone that builds urgency without overselling.

**When to use it:** Major product launches when you need marketing copy that balances excitement with realistic expectations.

**Pro tip:** Include one specific customer quote or use case in your announcement. Social proof makes new capabilities feel proven, not experimental.

> 
> Product: {product_name}
> Meeting attendees: {customer_segments_represented}
> Current focus: {top_development_priority}
> Customer feedback themes: {recurring_requests_from_CAB}
> Competing priorities: {features_not_being_built_now}
> Resource constraints: {team_or_technical_limitations}
> Market pressures: {competitive_or_timing_factors}
> Timeline expectations: {realistic_delivery_estimates}

> 
> Write a 500-word roadmap presentation for your customer advisory board. Explain current priorities and trade-offs transparently. Address why certain requested features aren't being built immediately. Ask for specific input on prioritization criteria and use case validation. Show how customer feedback influences roadmap decisions. End with concrete questions for the group. Tone should be collaborative and seeking genuine partnership.

**When to use it:** Customer advisory board meetings where you need to balance transparency with strategic confidentiality.

**Pro tip:** Bring real trade-off decisions to your CAB, not just progress updates. They provide better input when asked to help solve actual prioritization dilemmas.

> 
> Product: {product_name}
> Competitive threat: {specific_competitor_and_capability}
> Our current gap: {functionality_we_lack}
> Roadmap response: {features_being_built_to_compete}
> Timeline: {when_competitive_features_available}
> Interim positioning: {how_to_sell_around_the_gap}
> Unique advantages: {areas_where_we_still_lead}
> Customer retention risk: {accounts_potentially_at_risk}
> Win-back strategy: {approach_for_lost_prospects}
> 
> Write a 400-word competitive roadmap update for your sales team. Acknowledge the competitive gap directly. Provide specific timeline for closing the gap. Give sales team interim messaging and positioning guidance. Highlight areas where you still differentiate. Include talking points for at-risk customer conversations. Tone should be realistic and strategic, not defensive.

**When to use it:** When competitors launch features that create immediate sales headwinds and your team needs response guidance.

**Pro tip:** Give your sales team permission to acknowledge competitive gaps while providing stronger differentiation points. Credibility beats blind positioning.

## Internal Stakeholder Updates

> You are a Product Manager updating engineering leadership on roadmap technical requirements.
> 
> Product: {product_name}
> Upcoming features: {next_quarter_development_priorities}
> Technical complexity: {architecture_or_scaling_challenges}
> Infrastructure needs: {platform_investments_required}
> Team capacity: {current_engineering_bandwidth}
> External dependencies: {third_party_integrations_or_vendors}
> Performance requirements: {speed_scale_or_reliability_targets}
> Security considerations: {compliance_or_security_requirements}
> Timeline pressure: {business_deadline_constraints}
> 
> Write a 350-word technical roadmap brief for engineering leadership. Focus on architectural implications and resource requirements. Highlight potential technical risks and mitigation approaches. Include business context for timeline constraints. Ask for specific technical input on implementation approach. Tone should be collaborative and respectful of engineering complexity. Structure as: Business Context, Technical Requirements, Timeline Constraints, Input Needed.

**When to use it:** Sprint planning or quarterly planning meetings when engineering needs context for technical decision-making.

**Pro tip:** Always include the business reasoning for timeline constraints. Engineers make better trade-off decisions when they understand customer or market pressures.
