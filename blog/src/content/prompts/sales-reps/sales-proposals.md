## Initial Proposal Creation

> You are a sales professional writing a comprehensive business proposal for a new prospect.
> 
> Company: {prospect_company_name}
> Contact: {decision_maker_name} at {prospect_company_name}
> Industry: {prospect_industry}
> Pain points discovered: {three_specific_pain_points}
> Your solution: {core_product_or_service}
> Proposed timeline: {project_duration}
> Budget range: {dollar_amount_or_range}
> Key differentiator: {main_competitive_advantage}
> Next step: {meeting_demo_trial_or_contract}
> 
> Write a 800-1200 word proposal following this structure: Executive Summary (2 paragraphs), Problem Analysis (3 specific pain points with business impact), Proposed Solution (features tied directly to their pain points), Implementation Timeline, Investment & ROI, Next Steps. Use a consultative tone that demonstrates deep understanding of their business challenges.

**When to use it:** When you're responding to an inbound lead or following up after a discovery call where you've identified clear pain points and budget authority.

**Pro tip:** Include specific metrics from their industry in the Problem Analysis section. "Companies in {industry} typically lose 23% revenue when X happens" hits harder than generic statements.

> You are responding to a formal RFP with strict requirements and evaluation criteria.
> 
> RFP title: {rfp_document_title}
> Issuing organization: {client_organization}
> Key requirements: {five_must_have_requirements}
> Evaluation criteria: {scoring_factors_like_price_experience_approach}
> Your company: {your_company_name}
> Relevant experience: {similar_projects_or_clients}
> Team members: {key_people_and_roles}
> Proposed approach: {methodology_or_process}
> Total investment: {dollar_amount}
> 
> Write a 1000-1500 word RFP response structured as: Executive Summary, Understanding of Requirements, Proposed Approach & Methodology, Team Qualifications, Timeline & Deliverables, Investment, References. Address each evaluation criterion explicitly. Use formal business tone with specific examples that demonstrate capability.

**When to use it:** When responding to government RFPs or large enterprise procurement processes with formal scoring matrices.

**Pro tip:** Mirror their exact language from the RFP requirements. If they say "vendor must provide scalable solutions," use "scalable solutions" in your response, not "flexible offerings."

> You are writing a follow-up proposal after an initial rejection or "not right now" response.
> 
> Prospect: {company_and_contact_name}
> Original proposal date: {date_of_first_proposal}
> Reason for initial rejection: {budget_timing_features_or_other}
> What's changed: {new_circumstances_or_information}
> Modified solution: {adjusted_offering_or_approach}
> New timeline: {revised_implementation_schedule}
> Revised investment: {updated_pricing}
> Urgency factor: {deadline_or_business_driver}
> Relationship status: {warm_cold_or_existing_contact}
> 
> Write a 400-600 word re-engagement proposal. Open by acknowledging the previous conversation and what's changed. Present the modified solution as a response to their original concerns. Include a clear call-to-action with urgency. Use a confident but not pushy tone that respects their original position while making the case for reconsideration.

**When to use it:** When a qualified prospect said no 3-6 months ago, but their situation or your offering has evolved enough to warrant another approach.

**Pro tip:** Reference something specific from your original conversation to prove you remember their situation. "You mentioned the Q4 budget freeze" shows you were listening, not just following up on everyone.

## Pricing and Investment Proposals

> You are presenting a custom pricing proposal after initial price objections.
> 
> Client: {company_name}
> Original quoted price: {initial_price_quoted}
> Their budget: {stated_or_implied_budget_limit}
> Modified scope: {adjusted_deliverables_or_timeline}
> New price: {revised_price_point}
> What's removed: {specific_items_cut_from_original_scope}
> Payment terms: {payment_schedule_or_structure}
> Value maintained: {key_benefits_still_included}
> Upgrade path: {how_they_can_add_back_removed_items}
> 
> Write a 300-500 word pricing revision proposal. Open by acknowledging their budget parameters. Present the modified solution as a strategic fit, not a compromise. Detail what's included and excluded clearly. Close with payment terms and upgrade options. Use collaborative tone that frames this as problem-solving, not concession.

**When to use it:** After a prospect says "we love it but it's over budget" and you have flexibility to adjust scope or terms.

**Pro tip:** Always show the full original price crossed out, then your concession. This anchors them to the higher number and makes your revision feel like a win for them.

> You are justifying premium pricing against a lower-cost competitor's bid.
> 
> Your company: {your_company_name}
> Competitor: {competing_company_name}
> Your price: {your_quoted_amount}
> Their price: {competitor_quoted_amount}
> Price difference: {dollar_or_percentage_gap}
> Your advantages: {three_key_differentiators}
> Hidden costs in their bid: {what_competitor_likely_missed_or_charges_extra}
> Client's risk tolerance: {high_medium_or_low}
> Project importance: {business_critical_or_routine}
> Decision criteria: {what_matters_most_to_this_client}
> 
> Write a 500-700 word price justification proposal. Structure as: Investment Comparison, Value Differential Analysis, Hidden Cost Revelation, Risk Assessment, Total Cost of Ownership. Acknowledge the price difference directly, then systematically justify it through superior value and lower total cost. Use confident, fact-based tone that educates rather than defends.

**When to use it:** When you're 15-30% higher than the competition but offer demonstrably superior value or lower risk.

**Pro tip:** Create a comparison table showing "Apparent Cost vs Total Cost" where you add back all the extras they'll charge for that you include.

**When to use it:** When a qualified prospect wants to move forward but needs to spread payments across multiple budget periods or quarters.

**Pro tip:** Always tie payments to deliverables, never just calendar dates. "Upon completion of Phase 1 testing" is better than "30 days after contract signing."

## Objection Handling and Responses

> You are responding to a "we need to think about it" objection after presenting your proposal.
> 
> Prospect: {company_and_contact_name}
> Proposal presented: {date_of_presentation}
> Stated objection: {specific_reason_for_hesitation}
> Real concern: {underlying_issue_you_suspect}
> Decision makers: {who_else_is_involved_in_decision}
> Timeline pressure: {any_deadlines_or_urgency_factors}
> Next step offered: {meeting_call_or_demo_you_want}
> Competitor threat: {other_vendors_they_might_be_considering}
> Value props: {three_strongest_benefits_for_them}
> 
> Write a 300-400 word follow-up proposal that addresses their hesitation. Acknowledge their need to deliberate, then provide additional information that makes the decision easier. Include a specific next step with urgency but no pressure. Offer to address any unstated concerns. Use helpful tone that positions you as an advisor, not just a vendor.

**When to use it:** 24-48 hours after a prospect says they need time to think, especially when you sense there's an unstated concern.

**Pro tip:** Offer to speak with other decision makers directly. "I'm happy to present to your CFO directly" shows confidence and speeds up their internal process.

> You are addressing a direct price objection with value reinforcement.
> 
> Client: {company_name}
> Objection: {their_specific_price_concern}
> Your price: {quoted_amount}
> Their expectation: {what_they_thought_it_would_cost}
> Cost of inaction: {what_happens_if_they_do_nothing}
> ROI timeline: {how_fast_they_see_payback}
> Competitive context: {how_your_price_compares_to_alternatives}
> Payment flexibility: {options_you_can_offer}
> Risk factors: {what_could_go_wrong_with_cheaper_options}
> 
> Write a 400-500 word price objection response. Structure as: Acknowledgment, Value Reframe, Cost of Inaction, ROI Demonstration, Risk Comparison. Never apologize for your price or immediately offer discounts. Instead, reinforce value and create urgency around the cost of delay. Use confident educational tone that teaches them how to think about the investment correctly.

**When to use it:** When a qualified prospect's first reaction to your price is "that's more than we expected" or "we can't afford that."

**Pro tip:** Ask "compared to what?" when they say it's expensive. Often they're comparing to something irrelevant, and this question reframes the conversation.

## Technical and RFP Responses

> You are responding to a detailed technical RFP with specific functionality requirements.
> 
> RFP number: {official_rfp_identifier}
> Issuing organization: {client_organization}
> Technical requirements: {five_key_functional_specifications}
> Compliance standards: {regulatory_or_security_requirements}
> Integration needs: {systems_that_must_connect}
> Performance criteria: {speed_capacity_or_availability_requirements}
> Your solution architecture: {how_your_system_meets_their_needs}
> Implementation approach: {technical_rollout_methodology}
> Support structure: {ongoing_maintenance_and_support}
> 
> Write a 1000-1500 word technical response structured as: Executive Summary, Requirements Analysis, Technical Architecture, Implementation Methodology, Compliance & Security, Performance Specifications, Support & Maintenance. Address each requirement explicitly with specific technical details. Use precise technical language while remaining accessible to both technical and business stakeholders.

**When to use it:** When responding to government or enterprise RFPs that require detailed technical specifications and compliance documentation.

**Pro tip:** Create a requirements traceability matrix that shows exactly where in your response you address each RFP requirement. Procurement teams love this level of organization.
