## Initial Outreach Emails

> You are a startup founder writing a cold email to a VC partner.
>
> Your details:
> Company: {company_name}
> Founder: {founder_name}
> Industry: {industry_sector}
> Stage: {pre_seed / seed / series_a}
> Traction metric: {key_metric_and_number}
> Previous experience: {founder_background}
> VC name: {vc_partner_name}
> VC firm: {vc_firm_name}
> Connection point: {portfolio_company / shared_contact / conference / none}
> Funding goal: {amount_seeking}
>
> Write a 150-200 word cold email. Lead with the connection point or strongest traction metric. Include one specific question about their portfolio or thesis. End with a clear ask for a 15-minute call. Use a confident but respectful tone.

**When to use it:** You've researched a VC who invests in your stage and sector, and need to make first contact without a warm intro.

**Pro tip:** Reference a specific portfolio company that complements (doesn't compete with) your startup. VCs notice when you've done your homework.

>

> Company: {company_name}
> Technical breakthrough: {core_technology}
> Problem solving: {market_problem}
> Technical background: {founder_credentials}
> Early validation: {proof_point}
> VC name: {vc_partner_name}
> Their investment thesis: {vc_focus_area}
> Patents/IP status: {ip_situation}
> Team size: {current_team_count}
> Runway needed: {months_of_runway}
>
> Write a 180-220 word email that leads with the technical innovation. Explain the market problem in business terms, not technical jargon. Include one question about their experience with similar technologies. Request a technical deep-dive meeting.

**When to use it:** You're building something technically complex and need to reach VCs who understand deep tech without overwhelming them with jargon.

**Pro tip:** Lead with the business impact, then mention the technical moat. VCs invest in markets first, technology second.

**When to use it:** You've had a previous exit or built significant value before, and want to leverage that credibility for your new venture.

**Pro tip:** Mention one specific mistake from your previous company and how you're avoiding it this time. VCs value founders who learn.

## Follow-Up Sequences

> You are following up with a VC who hasn't responded to your initial email sent 10 days ago.
>
> Original context:
> Company: {company_name}
> Original ask: {what_you_requested}
> New development: {recent_milestone_or_customer}
> VC name: {vc_partner_name}
> Their focus area: {vc_investment_thesis}
> Mutual connection: {shared_contact_if_any}
> Timing: {urgency_factor}
> Social proof: {recent_press_or_validation}
>
> Write a 100-130 word follow-up email. Reference your original email briefly. Lead with the new development. Include one new piece of social proof or validation. Acknowledge they're busy and suggest a specific alternative like a brief call or coffee. Keep it shorter than your original email.

**When to use it:** Your first email went unanswered, but you've hit a meaningful milestone or gained validation that changes your story.

**Pro tip:** Wait exactly 10-14 days between emails. VCs expect persistence but respect patience. Never apologize for following up.

> You are following up after a VC meeting to provide requested information.
>
> Meeting context:
> VC name: {vc_partner_name}
> Meeting date: {meeting_date}
> Information requested: {specific_data_asked_for}
> Attached document: {deck_financials_or_data}
> Follow-up question: {question_they_raised}
> Timeline mentioned: {their_decision_timeline}
> Next step discussed: {agreed_next_action}
> Additional context: {relevant_update_since_meeting}
>
> Write a 120-150 word follow-up email that delivers exactly what they asked for. Reference a specific point from your conversation to show you were listening. Include the requested attachment. Confirm the timeline and next steps you discussed. Add one brief update if relevant.

**When to use it:** You've had an initial VC meeting and they asked for specific follow-up materials or information.

**Pro tip:** Send this within 24 hours of your meeting while you're fresh in their memory. Speed signals organization and respect for their time.

**When to use it:** VCs passed on your current round but expressed interest in staying updated on your progress for potential future investment.

**Pro tip:** Include one honest challenge in every update. VCs appreciate transparency and may offer valuable advice when you're struggling.

**When to use it:** You're raising a later round and want to re-engage VCs who passed on your earlier stage due to specific concerns you've since addressed.

**Pro tip:** Reference their exact previous concern and show concrete proof you've solved it. VCs appreciate founders who listen to feedback and execute.

> You are sending a final follow-up to a VC who has been unresponsive for 3 weeks.
>
> Context:
> VC name: {vc_partner_name}
> Previous contact: {when_you_last_heard}
> Round status: {funding_round_progress}
> Time pressure: {deadline_or_urgency}
> Alternative ask: {smaller_request_or_intro}
> Door open: {reason_to_reconnect_later}
> Company: {company_name}
> Graceful exit: {positive_note_to_end_on}
>
> Write an 80-100 word final follow-up that gracefully closes the loop. Acknowledge they're probably not interested right now. Briefly mention your round progress. Ask for a simple introduction if they know someone more relevant. Leave the door open for future stages. Keep it professional and brief.

**When to use it:** You've followed up twice with no response and need to move on while maintaining goodwill for the future.

**Pro tip:** Always end unresponsive sequences gracefully. The VC who ignores you at seed might lead your Series A. Fundraising is a long game.

## Subject Lines and Hooks

> You are writing compelling subject lines for VC cold outreach emails.
>
> Company context:
> Company: {company_name}
> Traction metric: {impressive_number_and_metric}
> Market category: {industry_or_vertical}
> Founder background: {notable_credential}
> Connection: {mutual_contact_or_portfolio_connection}
> Urgency: {timeline_factor}
> Unique angle: {differentiation_point}
>
> Write 8 different subject lines under 60 characters each. Mix approaches: metric-led, connection-based, curiosity-driven, and direct. Avoid spam words like "opportunity" or "exciting." Include at least 2 that reference specific numbers or names. Make them specific to this company, not generic.

**When to use it:** You need multiple subject line options to A/B test or want to personalize subject lines for different VC personalities.

**Pro tip:** VCs get 200+ pitches per week. Subject lines with specific metrics or mutual connections get opened first. Test "Company X - 400% growth, Series A" format.

> You are crafting opening hooks for VC pitch emails that grab attention in the first sentence.
>
> Hook elements:
> Problem statement: {market_pain_point}
> Solution: {company_approach}
> Traction proof: {customer_or_revenue_proof}
> Founder story: {personal_connection_to_problem}
> Market timing: {why_now_factor}
> Unique insight: {contrarian_or_non_obvious_truth}
> VC connection: {portfolio_or_thesis_alignment}
>
> Write 6 different opening sentences under 25 words each. Mix approaches: problem-first, traction-first, story-first, insight-first. Each should make a busy VC want to read the second sentence. Avoid cliches like "we're disrupting" or "huge market opportunity."

**When to use it:** You have 3 seconds to capture a VC's attention before they delete your email. The opening sentence determines if they keep reading.

**Pro tip:** Start with your strongest proof point, not background context. "We're processing $2M monthly transactions" beats "We started Company X to solve problem Y."

> Write 3 different email signature options under 4 lines each. Include different combinations of credibility markers. One minimal, one metric-heavy, one story-focused. Each should feel professional, not salesy. Include appropriate contact information for busy VCs.

**When to use it:** You want your email signature to reinforce your credibility and make it easy for interested VCs to learn more or schedule time.

**Pro tip:** Include your Calendly link in outreach signatures. VCs who want to meet prefer scheduling immediately rather than email ping-pong about timing.

> You are writing compelling one-line company descriptions for VC pitches.
>
> Company details:
> Company name: {company_name}
> Target customer: {specific_customer_type}
> Core product: {what_you_build}
> Key benefit: {primary_value_proposition}
> Market category: {industry_vertical}
> Business model: {how_you_make_money}
> Competitive advantage: {unique_moat_or_approach}
> Scale achieved: {current_customer_or_revenue_scale}
>
> Write 5 different one-line descriptions under 15 words each. Use active voice. Avoid jargon or buzzwords. Each should clearly explain who you serve and how. One should be customer-focused, one product-focused, one outcome-focused, one market-focused, one scale-focused.

**When to use it:** You need a crisp company description for email introductions, signature lines, or the first sentence of pitch emails.

**Pro tip:** Test your one-liner on non-industry friends. If they can't repeat back what you do and who you serve, simplify it further.
