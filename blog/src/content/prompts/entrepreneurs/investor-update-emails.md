## Monthly Performance Updates

> You are a startup founder writing a monthly investor update email.
> 
> Company: {company_name}
> Month/Quarter: {time_period}
> Revenue this period: {revenue_figure}
> Revenue growth: {growth_percentage}
> Key metrics: {metric_1}: {value_1}, {metric_2}: {value_2}, {metric_3}: {value_3}
> Major wins: {three_wins_bullet_format}
> Current challenges: {two_challenges}
> Team updates: {hiring_or_departures}
> Cash runway: {months_remaining}
> Next month priorities: {three_priorities}
> 
> Write a 400 to 500 word investor update email. Start with a one-sentence summary of the month. Present metrics in a clean format. Frame challenges as problems you're solving, not failures. Close with specific asks if you need investor help.

**When to use it:** Every month-end when you're sending regular updates to your investor group and need to present mixed results professionally.

**Pro tip:** Always lead with your strongest metric, even if it's not revenue. Investor attention drops after the first paragraph.

> Team size: {current_headcount}
> Burn rate: {monthly_burn}
> Runway: {months_cash_remaining}

> 
> Write a 350 to 450 word update email that celebrates growth while staying credible. Open with the headline numbers. Include one forward-looking statement about market opportunity. Keep the tone confident but not overconfident.

**When to use it:** After a strong quarter when you want to reinforce investor confidence and potentially set up conversations about next fundraising.

**Pro tip:** Include one small concern or challenge even in good updates. Pure positivity makes sophisticated investors suspicious.

> Manufacturing issue: {specific_problem}
> Impact on delivery: {delay_timeline}
> Units produced this month: {actual_vs_target}
> Resolution plan: {three_step_action_plan}
> Additional costs: {cost_impact}

> Supply chain changes: {vendor_or_process_changes}
> Revised timeline: {new_delivery_date}
> Cash impact: {runway_change}
> 
> Write a 300 to 400 word update that addresses the manufacturing setback directly. Lead with accountability, not excuses. Detail your resolution plan with specific dates. End with reassurance about long-term trajectory.

**When to use it:** When manufacturing delays or quality issues force you to reset expectations with investors.

**Pro tip:** Give investors the bad news first, then the plan. Never bury manufacturing problems in the middle of updates.

## Challenge and Recovery Updates

> You are a startup founder addressing a significant customer churn event.
> 
> Company: {company_name}
> Churn event: {what_happened}
> Customers lost: {number_or_percentage}
> Revenue impact: {monthly_or_annual_revenue_lost}
> Root cause analysis: {underlying_problem_identified}
> Immediate actions taken: {crisis_response_steps}
> Process improvements: {systemic_changes_implemented}
> Customer retention plan: {proactive_measures_for_remaining_customers}
> Recovery timeline: {when_you_expect_stabilization}
> Learning integration: {how_this_prevents_future_churn}
> 
> Write a 350 to 400 word email that takes ownership of the churn event and demonstrates learning. Lead with accountability. Detail your systematic response. Show how this makes you stronger long-term.

**When to use it:** When you lose multiple customers due to product issues, service problems, or competitive pressure and need to address investor concerns.

**Pro tip:** Frame customer churn as expensive but valuable learning. Investors respect founders who extract maximum insight from setbacks.
