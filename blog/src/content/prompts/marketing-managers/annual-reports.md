## Executive Summary Sections

> You are a Marketing Manager writing the executive summary for your annual marketing report.
> 
> Company: {company_name}
> Industry: {industry}
> Report year: {year}
> Primary achievement: {biggest_marketing_win}
> Key metrics: {three_main_kpis_with_numbers}
> Biggest challenge overcome: {major_challenge_solved}
> Next year's priority: {top_strategic_focus}
> Audience: {board / leadership_team / investors}
> Tone: {confident / balanced / ambitious}
> 
> Write a 250 to 300 word executive summary that opens with the primary achievement, presents key metrics in context, acknowledges the challenge as a learning moment, and closes with next year's strategic direction. Use active voice and include percentage changes where relevant.

**When to use it:** When you need to distill 50 pages of marketing performance into the opening section leadership will actually read.

**Pro tip:** Put your strongest metric first, even if it's not revenue. A 400% social engagement increase sounds more impressive than "revenue grew 8%."

> You are writing an executive summary for a marketing annual report after a challenging year with mixed results.
> 
> Company: {company_name}
> Revenue change: {percentage_up_or_down}
> Market conditions: {recession / competitive_pressure / supply_chain_issues}
> What worked: {successful_initiative_with_results}
> What didn't: {failed_initiative_honestly_described}
> Pivot made: {strategic_change_implemented}
> Team changes: {hiring / restructuring / budget_cuts}
> Outlook: {optimistic / cautious / aggressive}
> Lessons learned: {key_insight_gained}
> 
> Write a 300 to 350 word balanced executive summary that acknowledges difficulties without making excuses, highlights adaptation and resilience, and demonstrates strategic learning. Frame setbacks as market navigation rather than failures.

**When to use it:** When your annual report needs to address a tough year honestly while maintaining stakeholder confidence.

**Pro tip:** Use the phrase "despite market headwinds" exactly once. More than that and you sound like you're making excuses.

> 
> Company: {company_name}
> Revenue growth attributed to marketing: {percentage_or_dollar_amount}
> New market entered: {geographic_region_or_customer_segment}
> Campaign that broke through: {campaign_name_and_channel}
> Attribution method used: {first_touch / multi_touch / lift_studies}
> Team size change: {how_team_grew}
> Investment increase: {budget_change_percentage}
> Industry recognition: {awards_or_press_coverage}
> Competitive advantage gained: {what_you_now_lead_in}
> 
> Write a 275 to 325 word executive summary that quantifies marketing's business impact, explains your attribution methodology briefly, and positions the marketing team as a growth engine. Include one forward-looking statement about sustaining momentum.

**When to use it:** When you have strong attribution data showing marketing drove real business results and need to cement that narrative.

**Pro tip:** Include your attribution methodology in one sentence. It makes your growth claims more credible to finance and operations teams.

> Legacy systems replaced: {old_tools_removed}
> New tech stack: {martech_platforms_implemented}

> Efficiency gain: {time_saved_or_cost_reduced}

## Performance Analysis Sections

> You are analyzing marketing channel performance for an annual report, comparing this year's results across all major channels.
> 
> Paid search spend: {dollar_amount} generating {leads_or_revenue}
> Social media spend: {dollar_amount} generating {leads_or_revenue}
> Content marketing: {hours_invested} generating {leads_or_revenue}
> Email marketing: {sends_and_frequency} generating {leads_or_revenue}
> Events and sponsorships: {dollar_amount} generating {leads_or_revenue}
> Underperforming channel: {worst_performing_channel}
> Breakout channel: {best_performing_channel}
> Attribution window: {days_or_weeks}
> 
> Write a 400 to 450 word channel performance analysis that ranks channels by efficiency, explains the underperformer's context, celebrates the breakout channel's success, and recommends budget reallocation for next year. Include ROAS or cost-per-acquisition for each channel.

**When to use it:** When you need to present channel performance data that will inform next year's budget allocation discussions.

**Pro tip:** Always include the attribution window. A 7-day attribution makes paid search look better; 90-day attribution favors content and email.

> You are a Marketing Manager analyzing customer acquisition performance across different segments for your annual report.
> 
> Enterprise segment: {number_acquired} at {average_cost} each
> Mid-market segment: {number_acquired} at {average_cost} each  
> SMB segment: {number_acquired} at {average_cost} each
> Geographic breakdown: {region_performance_differences}
> Seasonal patterns: {monthly_or_quarterly_trends}
> Highest LTV segment: {which_segment_retains_best}
> Fastest growing segment: {which_segment_accelerating}
> Channel preference by segment: {how_each_segment_prefers_to_buy}
> 
> Write a 375 to 425 word customer acquisition analysis that compares segment economics, identifies growth opportunities, explains seasonal patterns, and recommends segment prioritization for next year based on LTV-to-CAC ratios.

**When to use it:** When leadership needs to understand which customer segments to prioritize and where to focus acquisition spending.

**Pro tip:** Calculate payback period for each segment, not just CAC. A $500 CAC with 3-month payback beats $200 CAC with 18-month payback.

> 
> Total leads generated: {number_of_marketing_qualified_leads}

> Sales cycle length: {days_from_lead_to_close}
> Lead source quality ranking: {best_to_worst_converting_sources}

> 
> Write a 360 to 410 word lead quality analysis that examines conversion rates at each funnel stage, identifies the highest-quality lead sources, explains sales cycle variations, and recommends improvements to lead generation strategy based on conversion data.

**Pro tip:** Break out conversion rates by lead source. Email leads might convert at 12% while trade show leads convert at 45%, completely changing your channel strategy.

## Strategic Initiative Reviews

> You are a Marketing Manager reviewing a major product launch from a marketing perspective for your annual report.
> 
> Product launched: {product_name_and_category}
> Launch date: {month_and_quarter}
> Target market: {customer_segment_or_geography}
> Go-to-market strategy: {channel_and_messaging_approach}
> Pre-launch awareness: {buzz_building_activities}
> Launch week results: {immediate_response_metrics}
> Adoption rate: {customer_uptake_speed}
> Revenue contribution: {sales_generated_to_date}
> Biggest challenge: {unexpected_obstacle_encountered}
> Key learning: {insight_for_future_launches}
> 
> Write a 425 to 475 word product launch review that analyzes marketing's execution, measures success against objectives, identifies what worked and what didn't, and distills learnings for future launches. Include both quantitative results and strategic insights.

**When to use it:** When you need to document a major product launch for stakeholders and extract learnings for the marketing playbook.

**Pro tip:** Include the one thing you'd do differently if launching again. It shows strategic thinking and helps other teams avoid the same mistake.

>

**Pro tip:** Calculate the lifetime value of customers saved, not just first-year revenue. Retention programs often look break-even until you account for multi-year customer value.
