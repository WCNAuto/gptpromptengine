## Search Ad Copy Creation

> You are a PPC specialist writing Google Search ads for a product launch campaign.
> 
> Product: {product_name}
> Target audience: {primary_demographic}
> Main benefit: {key_value_proposition}
> Competitor advantage: {how_you_beat_competitors}
> Price point: {pricing_tier: budget / mid-range / premium}
> Landing page URL: {destination_url}
> Brand voice: {tone: professional / friendly / urgent / technical}
> 
> Write 5 Google Search ad variants. Each ad needs: 3 headlines (30 characters max each), 2 descriptions (90 characters max each). Focus on the main benefit in headline 1, competitor advantage in headline 2, and urgency or CTA in headline 3. Include the product name in at least 2 headlines. End each description with a clear call-to-action.

**When to use it:** When your campaign needs fresh ad copy for a new product or service launch within 48 hours.

**Pro tip:** Test the character count by pasting into Google Ads Editor before uploading - ChatGPT sometimes miscounts spaces and punctuation.

> 
> Write 3 comparison-focused ad variants that highlight your advantage without directly naming the competitor. Headlines should focus on your differentiator, solution, and social proof. ## Landing Page Headline Testing

> You are writing landing page headlines for a Google Ads campaign A/B test.
> 
> Campaign focus: {primary_keyword_or_theme}
> Target audience: {specific_customer_segment}
> Main value proposition: {core_benefit}
> Page conversion goal: {form_signup_purchase_or_call}
> Current headline: {existing_headline_to_beat}
> Traffic source: {google_ads_campaign_type}
> Industry context: {business_sector}
> Emotional trigger: {fear_greed_curiosity_or_urgency}
> 
> Create 6 headline variations for split testing. Include one benefit-focused, one problem-focused, one urgency-focused, one social proof-focused, one curiosity-focused, and one direct response headline. Each headline should be 60 characters or less for mobile optimization. Make them different enough to produce statistically significant results.

**When to use it:** When your current landing pages have below 8% conversion rates and you need new headline angles to test this week.

**Pro tip:** Run tests with at least 100 conversions per variant before declaring a winner - smaller sample sizes often show false positives in headline tests.

> 
> Ad group: {ad_group_name}
> Top performing ad: {current_best_performing_ad}
> Landing page mismatch: {disconnect_between_ad_and_page}
> Bounce rate: {current_bounce_rate_percentage}
> Time on page: {average_seconds_on_page}
> Conversion rate: {current_conversion_percentage}
> User feedback: {common_complaint_or_confusion}
> Device split: {mobile_vs_desktop_traffic_percentage}
> 
> Write a new landing page headline and subheadline that perfectly match the ad copy and fix the identified mismatch. The headline should echo the ad's main promise, the subheadline should address the user feedback issue. Keep headline under 50 characters for mobile, subheadline under 120 characters. Focus on message match over creativity.

**When to use it:** When Google Ads data shows high CTR but low conversion rates, indicating a disconnect between ad promise and landing page delivery.

**Pro tip:** Use the exact same power words from your top-performing ad in the headline - "instant," "guaranteed," "proven" work in both contexts.

## Campaign Brief Generation

> You are creating a Google Ads campaign brief for a new product launch.
> 
> Product launch date: {specific_launch_date}
> Product name: {new_product_or_service_name}
> Target launch revenue: {first_month_revenue_goal}
> Budget allocation: {total_paid_search_budget}
> Primary audience: {main_customer_demographic}
> Secondary audience: {backup_target_audience}
> Competitive landscape: {number_and_type_of_competitors}
> Unique selling proposition: {key_differentiator}
> Launch timeline: {campaign_duration}
> 
> Write a 400-word Google Ads campaign brief that includes campaign structure recommendations, suggested ad groups, bidding strategy, and success metrics. Format as a client-ready document with clear sections: Objectives, Audience Strategy, Campaign Architecture, Budget Distribution, and Success Metrics. Include specific KPI targets and timeline milestones.

**When to use it:** When presenting a new campaign strategy to leadership or clients and need a professional brief that justifies budget and timeline decisions.

**Pro tip:** Include a "Week 1 vs Week 4" performance expectation table - it sets realistic expectations about ramp-up time and prevents panic over initial metrics.
