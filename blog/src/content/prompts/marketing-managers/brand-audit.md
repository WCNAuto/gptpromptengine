## Executive Summary Reports

> You are a marketing manager writing an executive summary for a comprehensive brand audit.
> 
> Brand name: {brand_name}
> Industry: {industry_sector}
> Audit timeframe: {start_month} to {end_month} {year}
> Key finding: {primary_discovery}
> Brand health score: {score_out_of_100}
> Biggest opportunity: {main_opportunity}
> Critical weakness: {major_weakness}
> Budget impact: {financial_implication}
> Stakeholder audience: {c_suite / board / investors}
> 
> Write a 400 to 500 word executive summary that opens with the brand health score and primary finding. Structure using three sections: Current State, Key Opportunities, and Recommended Actions. Include specific metrics where provided. Close with a single, actionable next step that requires executive decision-making within 30 days.

**When to use it:** When the C-suite requests a brand audit overview before your full presentation next week.

**Pro tip:** Lead with the brand health score in your opening sentence. Executives scan for numbers first, context second.

> You are a brand manager presenting audit findings to skeptical stakeholders who question marketing spend.
> 
> Brand: {brand_name}
> Previous year revenue: {revenue_figure}
> Brand awareness change: {percentage_change}
> Competitor gaining ground: {competitor_name}
> Marketing budget under review: {budget_amount}
> Stakeholder concern: {specific_worry}
> Timeframe for decision: {weeks_until_budget_decision}
> Success metric they care about: {revenue / market_share / customer_acquisition}
> Recommended investment: {dollar_amount_needed}
> 
> Write a 350 to 400 word executive summary that acknowledges their concern in the opening sentence. Present findings using a problem-cost-solution structure. Quantify the risk of inaction using competitor data. End with a specific budget request tied directly to their success metric.

**When to use it:** When finance or leadership is pushing back on brand investment and you need data-driven justification.

**Pro tip:** Always include the cost of competitor advantage in your risk calculation. Decision-makers respond to competitive threats more than growth opportunities.

## Competitive Analysis Sections

> You are a marketing analyst writing the competitive landscape section of a brand audit.
> 
> Your brand: {brand_name}
> Market category: {industry_sector}
> Top competitor: {main_rival}
> Emerging threat: {new_competitor}
> Market share data: {your_percentage_vs_theirs}
> Pricing position: {premium / mid / value}
> Unique advantage: {your_differentiator}
> Vulnerability: {where_competitors_win}
> Customer overlap: {shared_audience_percentage}
> Time period analyzed: {analysis_timeframe}
> 
> Write a 500 to 600 word competitive analysis that opens with market share context. Structure as landscape-positioning-threats-opportunities. Include specific competitor tactics in the positioning section. Identify where competitors are gaining ground in threats. Close with three competitive response recommendations ranked by impact potential.

**When to use it:** When stakeholders need to understand competitive threats and your brand's relative market position.

**Pro tip:** Focus on competitor customer acquisition tactics, not just their messaging. Stakeholders care more about what's working than what's being said.

> You are a brand strategist analyzing a direct competitor who launched a successful campaign against your market position.
> 
> Your brand: {brand_name}
> Attacking competitor: {competitor_name}
> Their campaign: {campaign_description}
> Campaign launch date: {start_date}
> Market impact: {share_lost_or_metrics_affected}
> Their messaging: {key_competitor_claims}
> Customer response: {market_reaction}
> Your current response: {what_youve_done}
> Budget available: {response_budget}
> Timeline pressure: {deadline_for_response}
> 
> Write a 350 to 400 word competitive response analysis using a threat-assessment-counter-strategy structure. Quantify the competitive impact in the threat section. Analyze why their approach resonated in assessment. Provide specific counter-tactics with timeline in the strategy section. Focus on immediate actions that can be deployed within 30 days.

**When to use it:** When a competitor's campaign is directly impacting your brand performance and you need a response strategy.

**Pro tip:** Analyze their customer promise, not their creative execution. Successful competitive campaigns usually identify an unmet need, not just better advertising.

## Brand Performance Analytics

> You are a marketing analyst presenting brand awareness tracking results from a quarterly study.
> 
> Brand name: {brand_name}
> Study period: {quarter_and_year}
> Awareness type measured: {aided / unaided / top_of_mind}
> Current awareness level: {percentage}
> Previous period comparison: {percentage_change}
> Category average: {benchmark_percentage}
> Demographic breakdown: {age_group_or_segment_performance}
> Geographic variation: {regional_differences}
> Investment correlation: {media_spend_relationship}
> Methodology: {survey_size_and_approach}
> 
> Write a 450 to 500 word brand awareness analysis using current state-trends-implications structure. Present awareness metrics with category benchmarking in section one. Identify demographic and geographic patterns in trends section. Translate findings into budget and strategy recommendations in implications section. Include specific actions to improve awareness in underperforming segments.

**When to use it:** When quarterly brand tracking results arrive and stakeholders need to understand awareness performance trends.

**Pro tip:** Always benchmark against category averages, not just your historical performance. Stakeholders need competitive context to make investment decisions.
