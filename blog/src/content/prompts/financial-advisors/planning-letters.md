## Portfolio Performance Updates

> You are a financial advisor writing a quarterly portfolio performance letter to a long-term client.
>
> Client: {client_name}
> Portfolio value: {current_value}
> Performance this quarter: {percentage_gain_or_loss}
> Market benchmark comparison: {outperformed_or_underperformed_by_percentage}
> Major holdings that drove performance: {top_three_holdings_and_performance}
> Economic context: {one_major_market_event_this_quarter}
> Client risk tolerance: {conservative / moderate / aggressive}
> Relationship length: {years_as_client}
>
> Write a 400 to 500 word letter that opens with the performance numbers in context, explains what drove the results using specific holdings, addresses the broader market environment, and closes with next steps. Use a confident but not boastful tone. Include one forward-looking insight about positioning for next quarter.

**When to use it:** Every quarter when client statements go out and you need personal commentary beyond the numbers.

**Pro tip:** Always lead with absolute dollar performance before percentages. Clients connect more with "your portfolio grew by $12,000" than "gained 3.2%".

> You are a financial advisor writing to a client whose portfolio significantly underperformed this quarter.
>
> Client: {client_name}
> Portfolio loss: {percentage_declined}
> Benchmark comparison: {how_much_worse_than_market}
> Main cause of underperformance: {specific_sector_or_holding}
> Client's original time horizon: {years_until_retirement_or_goal}
> Previous strong quarters: {two_examples_of_past_outperformance}
> Tone preference: {transparent / reassuring / data_focused}
> Action being taken: {specific_rebalancing_or_strategy_change}
>
> Write a 350 to 450 word letter using the situation-behavior-impact framework. Acknowledge the disappointment directly, explain the specific causes without making excuses, reference their long-term track record, and outline concrete steps being taken. End with a clear timeline for review.

**When to use it:** After any quarter where a client's portfolio trails benchmarks by more than 2%.

**Pro tip:** Reference specific past wins by dollar amount and date. Clients forget good quarters but remember every loss in detail.

> You are a financial advisor writing to a new client about their first quarter's performance after transferring assets.
>
> Client: {client_name}
> Assets transferred: {dollar_amount}
> Transfer completion date: {date}
> First quarter performance: {percentage_return}
> Previous advisor's last year performance: {comparison_percentage}
> Key changes made to allocation: {two_major_adjustments}
> Client's main financial goal: {retirement / education / legacy}
> Timeline to goal: {years}
> Risk adjustments made: {more_conservative / more_aggressive / maintained}
>
> Write a 300 to 400 word letter that welcomes them to the first full quarter, explains what you've adjusted and why, shows early results, and reinforces the long-term strategy. Use an educational tone that builds confidence in the transition.

**When to use it:** Three months after onboarding a significant asset transfer from another firm.

**Pro tip:** Include a one-line reminder of why they switched advisors. Reinforces their decision during the vulnerable early relationship period.

> You are a financial advisor writing an annual performance summary letter highlighting a client's best year in five years.
>
> Client: {client_name}
> Annual return: {percentage_gained}
> Dollar gain: {total_dollar_increase}
> Benchmark outperformance: {percentage_above_market}
> Best performing holding: {investment_name_and_return}
> Strategic decision that paid off: {specific_timing_or_allocation_choice}
> Original investment timeline: {years_remaining_to_goal}
> Progress toward goal: {percentage_of_target_reached}
> Market environment: {bull_market / volatile / recovering}
>
> Write a 450 to 550 word letter that celebrates the results while crediting their long-term discipline. Open with the headline numbers, detail what went right strategically, acknowledge their patience during previous tough periods, and close with how this positions them for their ultimate goal. Use an appreciative but professional tone.

**When to use it:** After exceptional annual performance when you want to reinforce the advisory relationship.

**Pro tip:** Include the specific date they started working with you and their portfolio value then versus now. Anniversary effect builds emotional connection.

> You are a financial advisor writing to a client about rebalancing their portfolio after a major market rally concentrated gains in one sector.
>
> Client: {client_name}
> Overweighted sector: {technology / healthcare / energy / etc}
> Current allocation percentage: {percentage_of_portfolio}
> Target allocation: {ideal_percentage}
> Profit to be taken: {dollar_amount}
> Reallocation target: {where_profits_will_move}
> Tax implications: {minimal / moderate / significant}
> Client's risk profile: {growth_focused / balanced / income_focused}
> Market timing: {bull_market_peak / mid_rally / uncertain}
>
> Write a 350 to 400 word letter explaining why you're recommending taking profits from strength to rebalance. Use specific allocation percentages, explain the risk of concentration, describe where the money will move, and address tax efficiency. Close with timeline for execution.

**When to use it:** When any single sector grows beyond 20% of a client's portfolio due to market movements.

**Pro tip:** Frame rebalancing as "taking profits from your winners" rather than "reducing your best performers." Same action, better psychology.

## Retirement Planning Communications

> You are a financial advisor writing to a client who is five years from retirement about adjusting their investment strategy.
>
> Client: {client_name}
> Current age: {age}
> Planned retirement age: {retirement_age}
> Current portfolio value: {total_assets}
> Retirement income need: {monthly_dollars_required}
> Current asset allocation: {percentage_stocks_bonds_cash}
> Recommended new allocation: {suggested_percentages}
> Social Security estimate: {monthly_amount}
> Pension or other income: {monthly_amount_or_none}
> Healthcare cost planning: {estimated_annual_amount}
>
> Write a 500 to 600 word letter explaining the shift toward income generation and capital preservation. Use the three-bucket approach (immediate needs, medium-term growth, long-term protection). Include specific timeline for changes and explain how this positions them for retirement success.

**When to use it:** During the annual review for any client within five years of their target retirement date.

**Pro tip:** Show the math on their retirement paycheck from all sources. Clients need to see the monthly income number, not just total assets.

> You are a financial advisor writing to a client who wants to retire two years earlier than originally planned.
>
> Client: {client_name}
> Original retirement age: {planned_age}
> New desired retirement age: {earlier_age}
> Current portfolio value: {total_assets}
> Shortfall created: {dollar_amount_gap}
> Monthly expenses in retirement: {estimated_amount}
> Flexibility on expenses: {willing_to_cut / fixed_lifestyle}
> Spouse's income: {amount_and_duration_or_none}
> Healthcare coverage plan: {bridge_insurance_or_spouse}
> Risk tolerance for catch-up: {conservative / moderate / aggressive}
>
> Write a 450 to 500 word letter that analyzes whether early retirement is feasible. Present three scenarios: maintaining current plan, modest acceleration, and aggressive catch-up. Use specific numbers for each path and recommend the most realistic option. End with next steps.

**When to use it:** When established clients request major changes to retirement timeline during market upswings.

**Pro tip:** Always show the "stay on current path" scenario first. Clients often forget how solid their original plan was before getting early retirement fever.

>
> Client: {client_name}
> Retirement start date: {month_and_year}
> Portfolio value at retirement: {starting_amount}
> First year withdrawal rate: {percentage}

> Market performance impact: {portfolio_gain_or_loss}
> Social Security status: {started / delayed / spouse_claiming}
> Major expenses encountered: {healthcare / travel / home / none}
> Emotional adjustment: {loving_retirement / struggling_with_change}
> Withdrawal strategy: {systematic / bucket / bond_ladder}
>
> Write a 400 to 450 word letter reviewing their first year of retirement. Celebrate what's working, address any spending surprises, show how the portfolio is responding to withdrawals, and adjust the strategy if needed. Use a supportive tone that reinforces their security.

**When to use it:** Exactly 12 months after a client's retirement date during their first annual review.

**Pro tip:** Address the emotional side first. New retirees worry constantly about spending their savings, even when the math works perfectly.

>
> Client: {client_name}
> Age: {current_age}
> Medicare eligibility date: {month_and_year}
> Current health insurance: {employer / COBRA / marketplace}
> Health status: {excellent / good / managing_conditions}
> Spouse Medicare status: {same_timing / different / already_enrolled}
> Estimated Medicare costs: {monthly_premium_estimate}
> Medigap insurance need: {recommended / minimal / comprehensive}
> HSA balance: {current_amount}
> Healthcare budget allocation: {annual_dollar_target}
>
> Write a 350 to 450 word letter explaining how Medicare enrollment affects their financial plan. Cover Medicare costs, supplement insurance options, HSA strategy changes, and budget adjustments needed. Include enrollment timeline and action steps.

**When to use it:** Six months before any client turns 65 or loses employer health coverage.

**Pro tip:** Emphasize HSA triple tax advantage for retirees who have balances. Many clients don't realize they can use HSA funds for Medicare premiums.

> Other taxable income: {pensions_social_security_etc}

> Withdrawal strategy: {monthly / quarterly / annual}
>

**When to use it:** Two years before any client reaches age 73 during annual planning meetings.

**Pro tip:** Run QCD numbers for any client who donates to charity. Tax-free charitable distributions often beat taking RMDs and deducting donations.

## Investment Strategy Letters

> You are a financial advisor writing to a client about adding international diversification to their portfolio.
>
> Client: {client_name}
> Current US allocation: {percentage_domestic}
> Recommended international allocation: {target_percentage}
> Time horizon: {years_to_goal}
> Risk tolerance: {conservative / moderate / aggressive}
> Currency hedge preference: {hedged / unhedged / mixed}
> Emerging markets comfort: {willing / developed_only / small_allocation}
> Tax account type: {taxable / IRA / mix}
> Investment vehicle: {ETFs / mutual_funds / individual_stocks}
> Recent international performance: {underperformed_or_outperformed_US}
>
> Write a 400 to 450 word letter explaining the benefits of international diversification. Address home country bias, currency effects, and correlation benefits. Use historical examples of when international outperformed US markets. Close with specific implementation timeline.

**When to use it:** When client portfolios become overly concentrated in US investments during domestic market outperformance.

**Pro tip:** Show rolling 10-year periods where international beat US markets. Clients assume US always wins because of recent performance.

> You are a financial advisor writing to a conservative client about adding a small growth allocation to their income-focused portfolio.
>
> Client: {client_name}
> Current allocation: {percentage_bonds_dividend_stocks_cash}
> Proposed growth allocation: {small_percentage_to_add}
> Age: {client_age}
> Risk comfort level: {very_conservative / moderately_conservative}
> Income needs: {current_or_future}
> Inflation concerns: {worried / not_concerned}
> Time horizon: {years_until_needing_principal}
> Growth vehicle: {broad_index / dividend_growth / balanced}
> Market timing: {bull_market / bear_market / uncertain}
>
> Write a 350 to 400 word letter explaining why a small growth position makes sense for long-term purchasing power protection. Address their income security first, then explain inflation risk to conservative portfolios. Use gentle language that respects their risk aversion.

**When to use it:** During inflationary periods when conservative clients need growth exposure but resist volatility.

**Pro tip:** Frame growth allocation as "purchasing power protection" rather than "growth investment." Same strategy, less scary language.

> You are a financial advisor writing to a client about tax-loss harvesting opportunities in their taxable account.
>
> Client: {client_name}
> Taxable account value: {current_amount}
> Unrealized losses available: {total_loss_amount}
> Tax bracket: {current_marginal_rate}
> Other capital gains this year: {amount_or_none}
> Positions to harvest: {specific_holdings_at_losses}
> Replacement investments: {substantially_different_options}
> Wash sale risk: {retirement_accounts_with_similar_holdings}
> Tax savings estimate: {dollar_amount}
> Year-end timeline: {months_remaining}
>
> Write a 300 to 400 word letter explaining the tax-loss harvesting opportunity. Explain how losses offset gains and ordinary income, detail which positions you recommend selling, show replacement options, and quantify tax savings. Include timing considerations for year-end.

**When to use it:** During market downturns when client taxable accounts have significant unrealized losses.

**Pro tip:** Harvest losses in November, not December. Gives you flexibility if clients have unexpected capital gains from other sources.
