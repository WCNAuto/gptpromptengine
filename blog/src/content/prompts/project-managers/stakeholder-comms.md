## Status Updates and Progress Reports

> You are a Project Manager sending a weekly status update to stakeholders.
> 
> Project: {project_name}
> Week ending: {date}
> Overall status: {on_track / at_risk / behind_schedule}
> Key accomplishments this week:
> {three_to_four_bullet_points}
> Upcoming milestones:
> {two_to_three_milestones_with_dates}
> Blockers or risks: {current_blockers_or_none}
> Budget status: {percentage_spent} spent of {total_budget}
> Stakeholder action needed: {specific_action_or_none}
> Tone: {professional / casual / urgent}
> 
> Write a 250 to 350 word status update email. Lead with overall project health using a clear red/yellow/green indicator. Group accomplishments by workstream. Flag any risks that need stakeholder attention. End with a clear next steps section.

**When to use it:** Every Friday afternoon when you're rushing to get status updates out before the weekend.

**Pro tip:** Always include a percentage completion estimate in your subject line - "Project Alpha - 65% Complete - Week of June 8th" gets opened faster than generic status updates.

> You are a Project Manager announcing a significant milestone completion to the project steering committee.
> 
> Project: {project_name}
> Milestone completed: {milestone_description}
> Completion date: {actual_completion_date}
> Original target: {planned_completion_date}
> Key deliverables: {three_to_five_deliverable_bullets}
> Team members to recognize: {team_member_names_and_contributions}
> Impact on timeline: {ahead_schedule / on_schedule / minor_delay}
> Next major milestone: {next_milestone_name} on {target_date}
> Lessons learned: {one_to_two_key_insights}
> 
> Write a 200 to 300 word milestone completion email. Open with clear celebration of the achievement. Acknowledge team contributions by name. Connect this win to overall project success. Close with momentum toward the next milestone.

**When to use it:** Within 24 hours of completing any major milestone worth over $50K or 500 hours of effort.

**Pro tip:** Include a brief "what this means" section for non-technical stakeholders - many executives won't understand why "API integration complete" matters to the business outcome.

> You are a Project Manager providing a monthly executive dashboard update to C-level stakeholders.
> 
> Project portfolio: {number_of_active_projects}
> Reporting period: {month_year}
> Green projects: {number_and_names}
> Yellow projects: {number_names_and_brief_risk}
> Red projects: {number_names_and_critical_issues}
> Budget variance: {over_under_budget_percentage}
> Resource utilization: {team_capacity_percentage}
> Upcoming decisions needed: {executive_decisions_required}
> Business impact this month: {quantified_value_delivered}
> 
> Write a 400 to 500 word executive summary email. Use a dashboard format with clear metrics. Highlight business value delivered, not just project tasks. Flag decisions that need C-level attention. Keep technical details to one sentence maximum per project.

**When to use it:** Last business day of each month when preparing board-level project reporting.

**Pro tip:** Lead with business impact numbers (revenue, cost savings, customer satisfaction) rather than project completion percentages - executives care about business outcomes first.

> 
> Project: {project_name}
> Issue identified: {critical_problem_description}
> Impact if unresolved: {business_consequence}
> Root cause: {underlying_reason}
> Recovery plan: {three_to_four_step_action_plan}
> Additional resources needed: {budget_people_or_time}
> Revised timeline: {new_completion_date}
> Risk mitigation: {steps_to_prevent_recurrence}
> Stakeholder decisions required: {urgent_approvals_needed}

> 
> Write a 350 to 450 word crisis communication email. Lead with the problem and business impact. Present a clear recovery plan with specific actions and owners. Request immediate decisions needed. Maintain confidence while being transparent about challenges.

**When to use it:** When a project goes from green to red status and you need stakeholder buy-in for recovery actions within 48 hours.

**Pro tip:** Always include a "lessons learned" section even in crisis emails - stakeholders want to know you're thinking about prevention, not just firefighting.

> 
> Project: {project_name}
> Planned go-live date: {launch_date}
> Readiness status: {ready / needs_attention / not_ready}
> Technical testing results: {pass_rate_and_critical_bugs}
> User acceptance testing: {completion_percentage_and_feedback}
> Training completion: {percentage_of_users_trained}

**Pro tip:** Include user impact estimates ("affects 250 daily users for first 3 days") so stakeholders can make informed decisions about launch timing versus business disruption.

## Budget and Resource Communications

> You are a Project Manager requesting budget reallocation due to scope changes.
> 
> Project: {project_name}
> Current budget: {approved_amount}
> Requested adjustment: {additional_amount_needed}
> Reason for change: {scope_change_description}
> Business justification: {why_change_is_necessary}
> Impact of not approving: {consequence_of_no_additional_budget}
> Alternative options considered: {other_approaches_evaluated}
> Timeline impact: {effect_on_project_schedule}
> Approval needed by: {decision_deadline}
> Budget source suggestions: {recommended_funding_sources}
> 
> Write a 350 to 450 word budget request email. Lead with business justification, not project needs. Present options with trade-offs clearly. Include ROI or cost-benefit analysis. Make the approval process as easy as possible with specific next steps.

**When to use it:** When scope changes require budget increases above your discretionary authority limit.

**Pro tip:** Always present three options: minimum viable, recommended, and ideal scenarios with different budget levels - gives stakeholders control over the decision.

> You are a Project Manager announcing resource constraints that will impact project delivery.
> 
> Project: {project_name}
> Resource gap: {skill_set_or_team_member_unavailable}
> Impact on timeline: {delay_in_weeks_or_months}
> Critical path effects: {which_milestones_affected}
> Attempted solutions: {internal_options_already_tried}
> Recommended approach: {preferred_solution_contractor_hire_etc}
> Cost implications: {budget_impact_of_solution}
> Alternative timeline: {revised_project_schedule}
> Stakeholder decision needed: {approval_required}
> Urgency: {how_long_before_impact_becomes_critical}
> 
> Write a 300 to 400 word resource constraint email. Present the problem with timeline impact first. Show you've tried internal solutions. Recommend the best path forward with cost-benefit analysis. Give stakeholders clear decision options.

**When to use it:** When resource unavailability will delay your project by more than two weeks.

**Pro tip:** Include market rates for contract resources and lead times for hiring - helps stakeholders understand the urgency and cost trade-offs of different solutions.

**When to use it:** Monthly financial reporting to CFO, finance team, or budget-conscious steering committee members.

**Pro tip:** Include spend velocity analysis - "at current burn rate, budget exhaustion in X weeks" - helps finance teams plan cash flow and interventions.

**Pro tip:** Include a "cost of delay" calculation showing lost revenue or increased costs per week of delay - often makes expensive contractors look like bargains.

## Risk Management and Issue Escalation

> You are a Project Manager escalating a critical risk that needs immediate stakeholder attention.
> 
> Project: {project_name}
> Risk identified: {specific_risk_description}
> Probability: {high_medium_low}
> Impact if occurs: {business_consequence_and_cost}
> Timeline to impact: {when_risk_becomes_reality}
> Current mitigation efforts: {what_you_are_already_doing}
> Additional mitigation needed: {resources_or_decisions_required}
> Stakeholder action required: {specific_help_needed}
> Alternative scenarios: {other_approaches_to_consider}
> Decision needed by: {deadline_for_stakeholder_response}
> 
> Write a 300 to 400 word risk escalation email. Lead with potential business impact in quantified terms. Show you're actively managing what you can control. Request specific stakeholder actions with clear deadlines. Include escalation rationale.

**When to use it:** When a project risk moves to "high probability" or when mitigation requires stakeholder-level decisions or resources.

**Pro tip:** Always include three scenarios: do nothing, minimum intervention, and full mitigation with different cost and probability outcomes.

**Pro tip:** Coordinate with legal and InfoSec teams before sending - they may need to control external communications for liability or investigation reasons.
