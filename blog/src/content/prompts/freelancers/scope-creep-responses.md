## Initial Scope Creep Pushback

> You are a freelancer responding to a client who has just requested work outside your original scope.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Original project: {original_project_description}
> New request: {specific_scope_creep_request}
> Project status: {not_started / in_progress / nearly_complete}
> Your relationship: {new_client / repeat_client / difficult_client}
> Your tone preference: {friendly_but_firm / professional / collaborative}
> Timeline impact: {adds_hours / delays_delivery / requires_new_skills}
> 
> Write a 150-200 word email response that acknowledges their request positively, explains why it falls outside scope, and offers two clear next steps: adding it as paid additional work or saving it for a future project. Be specific about impact without being defensive.

**When to use it:** The moment you read a client request that makes you think "that's not what we agreed to."

**Pro tip:** Send this within 2 hours of receiving the request. Delayed responses make clients think you're already working on it for free.

> You are a freelancer whose client is asking for "just a quick addition" to completed work.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Completed deliverable: {what_you_already_delivered}
> Their "quick" request: {specific_additional_request}
> Your estimate of actual time: {realistic_hours_needed}
> Project budget: {original_budget_amount}
> Payment status: {paid_in_full / final_payment_pending / payment_overdue}
> Their exact words: "{clients_exact_quote}"
> 
> Write a 100-150 word response that politely corrects the "quick" assumption, provides your actual time estimate, and gives them a specific price for the additional work. Include one line that reinforces the value of what you already delivered.

**When to use it:** When clients minimize extra work with words like "quick," "simple," or "just."

**Pro tip:** Always quote more time than you think it needs. "Quick" requests usually have hidden complexity.

## Boundary Reinforcement

> You are a freelancer whose client keeps adding "small tweaks" without acknowledging they're scope creep.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Number of tweaks so far: {count_of_small_requests}
> Original project scope: {original_deliverables}
> Time spent on tweaks: {hours_spent_on_additions}
> Your hourly rate: {rate_per_hour}
> Pattern you've noticed: {daily / after_each_delivery / during_feedback}
> Project progress: {behind / on_track / nearly_done}
> Your patience level: {first_warning / second_warning / final_warning}
> 
> Write a 180-250 word email that kindly but firmly addresses the pattern of small additions. Calculate the cumulative impact of tweaks, restate your original scope, and implement a clear process for any future requests outside scope. Be professional but decisive.

**When to use it:** When small requests are adding up to significant unpaid work.

**Pro tip:** Track every small request in a document. They add up faster than clients realize.

**When to use it:** When clients try to renegotiate your rates by citing mysterious competitors.

**Pro tip:** If they got a better deal elsewhere, they should take it. Don't compete with fictional quotes.

## Scope Expansion Offers

> You are a freelancer turning scope creep into a paid upsell opportunity.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Their additional request: {specific_scope_expansion}
> Original project value: {current_project_budget}
> Estimated time for addition: {hours_needed}
> Your hourly rate: {rate_per_hour}
> Complexity level: {simple_addition / moderate / complex}
> Their business need: {why_they_want_this_addition}
> Timeline preference: {add_to_current / separate_project / future_phase}
> 
> Write a 180-250 word response that reframes their scope creep as a smart business expansion, provides clear pricing for the additional work, and gives them options for timing. Position yourself as their trusted partner who can deliver the expanded vision with proper planning and budget.

**When to use it:** When scope creep represents a genuine opportunity to help the client and grow the project.

**Pro tip:** Always quote expansion work separately, even if you plan to do it immediately. Clear pricing prevents confusion.
