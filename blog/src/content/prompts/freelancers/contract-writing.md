## Payment Terms and Late Fees

> You are a freelance contract lawyer writing a payment clause for a creative services agreement.
> 
> Project type: {web_design / copywriting / consulting / development / marketing}
> Total project value: {dollar_amount}
> Payment schedule: {50_25_25 / net_30 / milestone_based / upfront}
> Late fee structure: {percentage_per_month / flat_daily_rate / compounding}
> Currency: {USD / EUR / GBP / CAD}
> Client industry: {startup / enterprise / nonprofit / agency}
> Rush job: {yes / no}
> 
> Write a 150-200 word payment terms clause that specifies invoice timing, payment deadlines, and late fee consequences. Include language that protects against scope creep affecting payment schedules. Use professional but firm tone that doesn't sound threatening.

**When to use it:** When a client wants to discuss payment terms and you need specific language that protects your cash flow without killing the deal.

**Pro tip:** If the client is a startup, add "payment terms survive company acquisition or ownership changes" to prevent new management from resetting your payment schedule.

>

> Client size: {small_business / mid_market / enterprise / startup}
> Dispute history: {first_time_client / repeat_client / referred_client}
> Payment amount: {under_5k / 5k_to_25k / over_25k}
> Timeline pressure: {flexible / moderate / urgent}
> 
> Write a 100-150 word clause covering what happens when a client disputes an invoice. Include a mandatory 10-day discussion period before any payment holds, requirement for written dispute reasons, and consequences for frivolous disputes. Keep the tone collaborative but protective.

**When to use it:** When you're working with a new client who seems hesitant about payment terms or has a history of challenging invoices.

**Pro tip:** Add "disputed work continues unless both parties agree to pause" so clients can't stop paying you while claiming they need to "review everything."

## Scope and Revision Management

> You are writing a scope change management clause for a freelance web development project.
> 
> Project type: {website_build / app_development / ecommerce_site / redesign / maintenance}
> Original scope complexity: {simple / moderate / complex / enterprise}
> Change request frequency: {rare / occasional / frequent / constant}
> Client decision makers: {single_contact / small_team / large_committee}
> Timeline flexibility: {fixed_deadline / flexible / ongoing}
> Budget sensitivity: {fixed_budget / flexible_budget / cost_plus}
> Integration requirements: {none / third_party_apis / legacy_systems}
> 
> Write a 160-220 word scope change clause that defines what constitutes a change, requires written approval for modifications, and establishes billing for additional work. Include language about timeline impacts and minimum billing increments. Keep tone professional but firm about change control.

**When to use it:** Before starting any project where the client has said "this should be straightforward" but you suspect hidden complexity or changing requirements.

**Pro tip:** Always include "verbal requests are not binding until confirmed in writing" because clients will try to sneak in changes during casual conversations.

> Timeline: {rush / standard / flexible}
> Revision complexity: {minor_tweaks / major_changes / complete_overhauls}
> Additional revision rate: {hourly_rate / flat_fee / percentage}
> Design approval process: {single_stakeholder / committee / legal_review}
> 
> Write a 120-160 word revision clause that clearly defines what counts as one revision round, distinguishes between minor tweaks and major changes, and sets expectations for additional revision billing. Include timeline impacts of excessive revisions.

**When to use it:** Any creative project where client feedback will come from multiple stakeholders or where you suspect the client doesn't have a clear vision yet.

**Pro tip:** Define "minor revision" as changes that take under 30 minutes - this prevents clients from calling a complete color scheme overhaul a "quick tweak."

## Intellectual Property and Usage Rights

> You are writing a copyright ownership clause for freelance graphic design work.
> 
> Design type: {logo / website / marketing_materials / packaging / illustrations}
> Usage scope: {single_company / multi_brand / resale_rights / exclusive_worldwide}
> Industry: {technology / healthcare / finance / retail / nonprofit}
> Competition concerns: {direct_competitors / industry_wide / no_restrictions}
> Derivative works: {allowed / restricted / prohibited}
> Attribution requirements: {portfolio_use / public_credit / no_attribution}
> Transfer timing: {upon_payment / upon_delivery / upon_launch}
> 
> Write a 150-200 word copyright clause that clearly transfers rights upon final payment while reserving your portfolio and promotional rights. Address derivative works and specify any usage restrictions. Keep language clear enough for non-legal clients.

**When to use it:** Any creative project where the client will own the final work and you need to clarify what rights transfer when and what you retain.

**Pro tip:** Always reserve "portfolio use and case study rights" even in full copyright transfers - you need proof of your work for future marketing.
