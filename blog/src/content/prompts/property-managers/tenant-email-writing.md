## Lease Violations and Compliance Issues

> You are a property manager addressing a lease violation with a tenant.
> 
> Tenant name: {tenant_name}
> Property address: {property_address}
> Violation type: {violation_type}
> Date violation occurred: {violation_date}
> Previous warnings given: {yes_or_no}
> Cure period required: {cure_period_days}
> Consequences if not resolved: {consequences}
> Tone: {firm_but_professional / stern / diplomatic}
> 
> Write a 200 to 300 word lease violation notice email. Open with the specific violation and date. Reference the lease clause being violated. Provide clear steps to remedy the situation within the cure period. Close with consequences if the violation continues. Use a firm but respectful tone throughout.

**When to use it:** When you discover a lease violation and need to document it formally while giving the tenant a chance to correct the issue.

**Pro tip:** Always reference specific lease clause numbers in the {consequences} variable - it shows you're following proper legal procedure and makes the notice more enforceable.

## Rent Collection and Late Payments

> You are a property manager sending a late rent reminder to a tenant with a history of timely payments.
> 
> Tenant name: {tenant_name}
> Unit address: {unit_address}
> Rent amount due: {rent_amount}
> Days late: {days_late}
> Late fees accrued: {late_fee_amount}
> Payment history: {generally_good / excellent / first_time_late}
> Preferred payment method: {payment_method}
> Grace period remaining: {grace_days_left}
> Contact for payment arrangements: {contact_info}
> 
> Write a 150 to 200 word friendly but clear late rent email. Acknowledge their good payment history. State the exact amount owed including late fees. Provide simple payment instructions and contact information for questions. Maintain a helpful tone while being clear about expectations.

**When to use it:** When a typically reliable tenant is a few days late and you want to maintain the relationship while collecting payment.

**Pro tip:** Include your online payment portal link directly in the email - good tenants often pay immediately when reminded, and you want to make it as easy as possible.

> You are a property manager sending a pay-or-quit notice to a tenant with chronic late payments.
> 
> Tenant name: {tenant_name}
> Property address: {property_address}
> Total amount owed: {total_amount_owed}
> Breakdown of charges: {rent_plus_fees_breakdown}
> Days to pay or vacate: {legal_notice_period}
> Late payment history: {number_of_late_payments_this_year}
> Payment deadline date: {specific_deadline_date}
> Acceptable payment methods: {payment_methods}
> Legal action consequences: {eviction_process_timeline}
> 
> Write a 200 to 250 word formal pay-or-quit notice email. Use official legal language. Break down all amounts owed clearly. State the exact deadline and consequences of non-payment. Reference state law notice requirements. Include only business hours contact information.

**When to use it:** When you need to serve formal legal notice for non-payment, usually after multiple late payments or significant amounts owed.

**Pro tip:** Send this via email AND certified mail to ensure proper legal service - courts require proof of delivery for eviction proceedings.

## Property Access and Inspections

> You are a property manager scheduling a routine annual inspection.
> 
> Tenant name: {tenant_name}
> Property address: {property_address}
> Inspection type: {annual / move_out / maintenance / compliance}
> Proposed inspection dates: {three_date_options}
> Time windows available: {time_options}
> Legal notice period being provided: {notice_days}
> Areas to be inspected: {inspection_scope}
> Estimated duration: {inspection_duration}
> Inspector name: {inspector_name}
> 
> Write a 200 to 250 word inspection notice email. Reference legal right to inspect with proper notice. Provide multiple date and time options. Explain what will be inspected and approximately how long it will take. Request tenant confirmation of preferred time. Include contact information for questions or scheduling conflicts.

**When to use it:** When conducting routine inspections required by your lease agreement or local regulations.

**Pro tip:** Always provide 3 different date options spanning at least a week - this shows you're trying to accommodate their schedule and makes it harder for tenants to claim the timing is unreasonable.
