## New Client Onboarding

> You are a real estate agent writing a welcome email to a new buyer client.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Property type they're seeking: {property_type}
> Budget range: {budget_range}
> Timeline: {timeline}
> Their main concerns: {top_two_concerns}
> Your experience with similar clients: {relevant_experience}
> Next meeting date: {next_meeting_date}
> 
> Write a 250 to 300 word welcome email that acknowledges their specific concerns, demonstrates your relevant experience, and outlines the next three steps in their home buying journey. End with a clear call to action about the upcoming meeting.

**When to use it:** Within 2 hours of signing a buyer representation agreement, when you need to set professional expectations immediately.

**Pro tip:** Always mention a specific recent success story that matches their property type and budget range - it builds credibility faster than generic testimonials.

> Property address: {property_address}

> Your commission rate: {commission_rate}
> 
> Write a 300 to 350 word follow-up email that directly addresses their main concern, reinforces the unique value of their property, and presents your listing strategy as the solution to their timeline needs. Include your commission structure transparently and end with a specific next step.

**When to use it:** Same day after a listing presentation, when you're competing against other agents and need to stand out.

**Pro tip:** Reference something specific from their home that isn't just curb appeal - mention the kitchen renovation date, the school district rating, or the lot size advantage.

> You are a real estate agent writing to a new seller client about pricing strategy.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Property address: {property_address}
> Their desired price: {desired_price}
> Market analysis price: {market_price}
> Days on market average: {average_dom}
> Recent comparable sale: {recent_comp_address_and_price}
> Market trend: {rising/stable/declining}
> Their motivation level: {high/medium/low}
> 
> Write a 200 to 250 word email explaining the pricing gap between their expectations and market reality. Use the recent comparable sale as your primary evidence. Recommend a specific listing price and explain how it serves their motivation level. Tone should be direct but supportive.

**When to use it:** When there's a significant gap between seller expectations and market reality, and you need to reset expectations diplomatically.

**Pro tip:** Always lead with the comparable sale that's closest in square footage and features, not just the most recent - it carries more persuasive weight.

**Pro tip:** Use exact calendar dates instead of "in 5 business days" - first-time buyers need concrete deadlines to feel in control.

## Transaction Management

> You are a real estate agent writing an inspection contingency deadline reminder.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Property address: {property_address}
> Inspection date: {inspection_date}
> Contingency deadline: {deadline_date}
> Inspector name: {inspector_name}
> Major issues found: {major_issues}
> Estimated repair costs: {repair_estimate}
> Seller's likely response: {cooperative/resistant/unknown}
> 
> Write a 300 to 350 word email summarizing inspection findings, explaining their three options (negotiate, accept, withdraw), and recommending a specific course of action based on repair costs and seller personality. Include exact deadline timing and next steps.

**When to use it:** Day after inspection, when clients are processing findings and need clear decision framework before the deadline.

**Pro tip:** Give repair cost ranges, not exact numbers - contractors' final quotes often vary, and you don't want clients feeling misled if estimates change.

> You are a real estate agent writing about appraisal results that came in low.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Property address: {property_address}
> Contract price: {contract_price}
> Appraised value: {appraised_value}
> Shortfall amount: {shortfall}
> Comparable sales used: {comp_addresses}
> Client's down payment: {down_payment_percent}
> Seller's situation: {motivated/firm/unknown}
> 
> Write a 350 to 400 word email explaining the appraisal gap, its impact on their financing, and presenting four specific options to move forward. Assess each option's likelihood of success based on the seller's situation. End with your recommended strategy and timeline for decision.

**When to use it:** Same day you receive low appraisal results, before clients panic or make emotional decisions about walking away.

**Pro tip:** Explain that appraisals protect buyers from overpaying - frame the low appraisal as the system working, not as a deal killer.

> Original closing date: {original_date}
> New closing date: {new_date}
> Reason for delay: {delay_reason}
> Who caused the delay: {buyer/seller/lender/title_company}

> Write a 200 to 250 word email that clearly states the delay, explains the reason without blame, addresses their moving plan concerns, and outlines specific steps to prevent further delays. Include your contact availability for questions during this stressful time.

**When to use it:** Immediately when you learn about closing delays, before clients hear about it from other parties and lose confidence in your management.

**Pro tip:** Offer specific solutions for their moving logistics - recommend storage companies, temporary housing options, or truck rental rescheduling services.

## Market Updates and Communication

> You are a real estate agent writing a market update to active buyer clients.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Their target area: {search_area}
> Their budget range: {budget_range}
> Current inventory levels: {inventory_status}
> Average days on market: {average_dom}
> Recent price trend: {price_trend}
> New listings this week: {new_listing_count}
> Interest rate change: {rate_direction}
> 
> Write a 300 to 350 word market update email that connects current conditions to their specific search parameters. Recommend strategy adjustments based on market trends and include 2-3 specific new listings they should consider viewing.

**When to use it:** Weekly for active buyers in competitive markets, when market conditions are shifting and strategy needs adjustment.

**Pro tip:** Include specific street names or subdivisions in their target area where activity is highest - generic market stats don't help them make viewing decisions.

**Pro tip:** Quote the exact feedback phrases from showings - "overpriced for the updates" hits differently than your paraphrase, and sellers need to hear buyer language.

## Problem Resolution and Difficult Situations

> You are a real estate agent writing to clients about a seller disclosure issue discovered after contract.
> 
> Client name: {client_name}
> Property address: {property_address}
> Issue discovered: {disclosure_issue}
> When it was found: {discovery_timing}
> Impact on property value: {value_impact}
> Legal implications: {legal_severity}
> Seller's knowledge: {knew/didnt_know}
> Repair vs. replacement costs: {cost_estimate}
> 
> Write a 350 to 400 word email explaining the disclosure issue clearly, outlining their legal options, recommending a specific course of action based on cost and legal implications, and timeline for decision-making. Tone should be serious but reassuring about your advocacy.

**When to use it:** When inspection or title work reveals issues that weren't properly disclosed, requiring immediate legal and financial decision-making.

**Pro tip:** Recommend they speak with their attorney before making demands to the seller - disclosure violations can have legal consequences beyond just repair costs.
