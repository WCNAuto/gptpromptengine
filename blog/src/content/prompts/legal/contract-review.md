## Pre-Signature Risk Assessment

> You are a senior legal counsel conducting a final risk assessment before contract execution.
> 
> Contract type: {contract_type}
> Counterparty: {counterparty_name}
> Deal value: {deal_value}
> Key commercial terms: {three_main_terms}
> Industry: {industry_sector}
> Timeline pressure: {urgent/standard/flexible}
> Internal stakeholder: {requesting_department}
> 
> Write a 400-word risk assessment memo for the business team. Structure it as: Executive Summary (go/no-go recommendation), Top 3 Risks (with specific contract language that creates each risk), Mitigation Steps (actionable items with owners), and Sign-off Requirements. Use bullet points for risks and mitigations. Keep language business-friendly but legally precise.

**When to use it:** When the business team is pushing for signature and you need to document risks clearly before execution.

**Pro tip:** Include specific clause references in your risk descriptions—it makes follow-up conversations faster when stakeholders want to discuss changes.

> 
> Software: {software_name}
> Vendor: {vendor_name}
> Annual value: {contract_value}
> Rejected redlines: {three_main_rejected_items}
> Business urgency: {high/medium/low}
> Alternative vendors: {yes_viable/no_viable/unknown}

> 
> Draft a 300-word analysis for your business client explaining the risks of proceeding with the vendor's terms. Start with a clear recommendation (proceed/negotiate further/walk away). List the three biggest risks from the rejected redlines using plain English. End with two specific negotiation tactics to try before signing. Format as an email they can forward to their manager.

**When to use it:** When you're caught between vendor pushback and business pressure to close a deal quickly.

**Pro tip:** Always suggest specific alternative language, not just "we should negotiate this"—it shows you've thought through practical solutions.

> You are conducting due diligence on a target company's commercial contracts as part of an acquisition.
> 
> Target company: {company_name}
> Acquisition value: {deal_size}
> Contract category: {customer_contracts/supplier_agreements/licensing_deals}
> Sample size reviewed: {number_of_contracts}
> Key findings: {three_main_issues_found}
> Deal timeline: {weeks_to_closing}
> Buyer risk tolerance: {conservative/moderate/aggressive}
> 
> Create a 500-word due diligence summary for the acquisition team. Structure as: Overview (what you reviewed), Material Issues (three findings with quantified impact where possible), Recommended Actions (specific steps pre-closing and post-closing), and Red Flags (deal-breaker items if any). Use numbered lists for actions and findings. Include a confidence level (high/medium/low) for your assessment.

**When to use it:** When you need to summarize contract review findings for deal teams who aren't lawyers but need to understand legal risks.

**Pro tip:** Always quantify financial exposure when possible—"could cost $50K annually" hits harder than "creates ongoing risk."

## Post-Execution Compliance Monitoring

> You are setting up ongoing compliance monitoring for a data processing agreement under new privacy regulations.
> 
> Vendor: {vendor_name}
> Data types processed: {personal_data_categories}
> Processing locations: {geographic_locations}
> Contract execution date: {signature_date}
> Compliance audit schedule: {quarterly/biannual/annual}
> Internal compliance owner: {department_and_role}
> Regulatory environment: {gdpr_ccpa_other_specify}
> 
> Draft a 400-word compliance monitoring checklist for the privacy team. Structure as: Monthly Check Items (3-4 operational items), Quarterly Review Items (compliance documentation and reporting), Annual Assessment Items (full audit requirements), and Escalation Triggers (specific events requiring legal review). Make each item actionable with clear yes/no criteria. Format as a document the compliance team can follow without legal training.

**When to use it:** When you need non-lawyers to monitor contract compliance without constant legal oversight.

**Pro tip:** Build escalation triggers around specific events, not subjective judgments—"vendor changes data location" not "vendor acts suspiciously."

## Industry-Specific Contract Analysis

> You are reviewing a clinical trial agreement for a pharmaceutical development partnership.
> 
> Sponsor company: {pharmaceutical_sponsor}
> Research institution: {clinical_site_name}
> Trial phase: {phase_1_2_3_or_4}
> Patient population: {target_enrollment_number}
> Primary endpoint: {main_efficacy_measure}
> Trial duration: {expected_length_in_months}
> Regulatory pathway: {fda_ema_or_other}
> Intellectual property ownership: {sponsor_site_or_shared}
> 
> Create a 500-word risk assessment focusing on regulatory and IP issues. Open with regulatory compliance requirements and sponsor obligations. Address intellectual property ownership and publication rights in the middle section. Close with liability allocation and insurance coverage adequacy. Use numbered sections for each major risk area. Include specific regulatory citations where relevant. Write for the technology transfer office who manages these agreements.

**When to use it:** When reviewing clinical trial agreements where regulatory compliance and IP ownership are critical success factors.

**Pro tip:** Publication rights often conflict with patent prosecution timelines—build in specific review periods that work for both research and commercial goals.

## Emergency Contract Modifications

> You are drafting an emergency amendment to suspend performance obligations during a force majeure event.
> 
> Contract parties: {your_company} and {counterparty_name}
> Original contract: {brief_contract_description}
> Force majeure event: {specific_event_or_circumstances}
> Affected obligations: {which_performance_requirements_impacted}
> Expected duration: {estimated_length_of_suspension}
> Critical obligations continuing: {what_remains_in_effect}
> Notice requirements: {contractual_notice_procedures}
> 
> Write a 300-word contract amendment suspending affected obligations. Structure as: Recitals (brief background and force majeure event), Suspended Obligations (specific list), Continuing Obligations (what remains active), Duration and Review (timeline and restart conditions), and Signatures. Use formal contract language but keep terms clear. Include a specific restart mechanism triggered by defined conditions.

**When to use it:** When unexpected events require immediate suspension of contract performance and you need formal documentation.

**Pro tip:** Always specify which obligations continue during suspension—payment terms, confidentiality, and insurance requirements typically survive force majeure events.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the difference between contract review checklists and contract analysis prompts for legal teams?
Contract review checklists are static templates lawyers fill out manually. These AI prompts generate finished risk assessments, compliance memos, and legal analyses based on specific contract situations. You input variables about your actual contract and get a complete document ready for business stakeholders.

### How do I customize these AI prompts for different contract types beyond the examples shown?
Replace the scenario details and variables with your specific contract elements. Keep the output format and structure—the 400-word risk memo format works whether you're reviewing employment agreements or distribution contracts. Change the industry-specific risks but maintain the executive summary, key risks, and action items structure.

### Can these contract review prompts help with regulatory compliance requirements?
Yes, several prompts specifically address regulatory compliance monitoring and documentation. The data processing agreement prompt handles privacy regulations, while the clinical trial prompt covers FDA requirements. Customize the regulatory environment variable to match your specific compliance obligations like GDPR, CCPA, or industry-specific regulations.
