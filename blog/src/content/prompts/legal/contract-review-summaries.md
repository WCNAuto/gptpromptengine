## Executive Contract Summaries

> You are a senior associate preparing an executive summary of a commercial contract for leadership review.
> 
> Contract type: {contract_type}
> Parties: {party_1} and {party_2}
> Contract value: {total_value}
> Term length: {contract_duration}
> Key obligations: {primary_obligations_each_party}
> Notable risks: {top_three_risks}
> Renewal terms: {renewal_mechanism}
> Termination rights: {termination_triggers}
> 
> Write a 400-500 word executive summary structured as: Deal Overview (2 sentences), Financial Terms, Key Obligations by Party, Risk Assessment, and Recommendation. Use business language, not legal jargon. Include specific dollar amounts and dates where provided.

**When to use it:** When the C-suite asks for a "quick overview" of a major deal and you have 20 minutes before their board meeting.

**Pro tip:** Always include the specific termination date in the first paragraph. Executives forget contract end dates and this prevents awkward renewal scrambles.

> You are a contracts manager creating a one-page summary for a service agreement under expedited review.
> 
> Service provider: {vendor_name}
> Services: {core_services_description}
> Monthly fee: {monthly_cost}
> Performance metrics: {key_performance_indicators}
> Data security requirements: {security_standards}
> Liability cap: {liability_limit}
> Indemnification scope: {indemnity_coverage}
> Contract start: {effective_date}
> 
> Create a 250-300 word summary using bullet points under four headers: Service Scope, Commercial Terms, Performance Standards, and Risk Allocation. Highlight any unusual terms that deviate from standard practice.

**When to use it:** When procurement needs a fast decision on a vendor agreement and the full contract is 40+ pages.

**Pro tip:** Flag any liability caps below $1M in red text. Most executives assume standard coverage and miss artificially low limits.

**Pro tip:** Convert royalty percentages to estimated dollar amounts using current revenue projections. Product managers think in revenue impact, not percentage points.

## Risk Assessment Reviews

> You are a senior associate conducting risk analysis on a software development contract with aggressive delivery timelines.
> 
> Vendor: {software_development_company}
> Project scope: {deliverables_and_specifications}
> Delivery timeline: {project_milestones_and_deadlines}
> Acceptance criteria: {testing_and_approval_process}
> Delay penalties: {liquidated_damages_structure}
> IP ownership: {code_ownership_terms}
> Support obligations: {post_delivery_maintenance}
> Change order process: {scope_modification_mechanism}
> 
> Write a 400-450 word risk assessment focused on delivery and performance risks. Structure as: Schedule Risk Analysis, Quality Assurance Concerns, Financial Exposure, IP Protection Issues, and Mitigation Recommendations. Rate each risk category as High/Medium/Low.

**When to use it:** When IT is pushing to sign a development contract with a vendor you've never used before and timeline is unrealistic.

**Pro tip:** Always red-flag any acceptance criteria that requires "substantial completion" instead of specific functional requirements. Vague acceptance terms create payment disputes.

**Pro tip:** Research whether the destination country requires local legal entity formation for distribution activities. Many agreements are unenforceable without proper local business registration.

**Pro tip:** Check if force majeure clauses exclude "economic hardship" or supplier financial problems. Standard force majeure may not cover vendor bankruptcy or cash flow crises.

## Client Communication Drafts

> You are a partner explaining complex contract terms to a business client who needs to understand key provisions before signing.
> 
> Client: {client_company_name}
> Contract type: {agreement_type}
> Complex provisions: {difficult_terms_to_explain}
> Business impact: {how_terms_affect_operations}
> Client concerns: {specific_issues_client_raised}
> Risk tolerance: {client_risk_appetite}
> Industry context: {relevant_market_factors}
> Negotiation status: {current_negotiation_position}
> 
> Write a 500-600 word client explanation email that translates legal terms into business language. Structure as: Executive Summary of Key Terms, Business Impact Analysis, Risk Assessment in Plain English, Strategic Recommendations, and Next Steps. Use analogies and examples to clarify complex concepts.

**When to use it:** When the client says "just tell me what this means for my business" and you need to explain derivative liability or cross-default provisions.

**Pro tip:** Use industry-specific analogies that match the client's business. Manufacturing clients understand supply chain metaphors better than financial services comparisons.

> Negotiation stage: {current_status}
> Key open issues: {unresolved_terms}
> Business timeline: {client_deadline_pressure}
> Vendor position: {their_key_demands}
> Our position: {company_requirements}
> Risk assessment: {deal_breaker_issues}
> Alternative options: {backup_vendor_or_strategy}
> 
> Create a 350-400 word negotiation status update structured as: Current Position Summary, Outstanding Issues Analysis, Timeline Impact Assessment, Strategic Options, and Recommended Action Plan. Focus on business decisions needed from stakeholders.

**When to use it:** When negotiations are stalled and the business team needs to decide whether to compromise or walk away from the deal.

**Pro tip:** Always include the cost of delay in your timeline analysis. Business teams often underestimate how contract delays affect project launches and revenue targets.

>

> Client position: {how_client_interprets_terms}

> Financial impact: {dollar_amount_at_stake}
> Relationship importance: {strategic_value_of_relationship}
> Litigation risk: {probability_of_formal_dispute}
> 
> Write a 450-500 word legal interpretation memo structured as: Contract Language Analysis, Interpretation Arguments, Precedent and Industry Practice, Risk-Benefit Assessment, and Strategic Recommendation. Include probability assessments for different outcomes.

**When to use it:** When a contract dispute is escalating and the client needs to decide whether to compromise, litigate, or seek alternative resolution.

**Pro tip:** Include the estimated cost and timeline of litigation versus settlement. Most business disputes resolve when clients see the true cost of being "right" in court.
