## Standard NDA Summary Documentation

> You are a legal professional preparing an NDA summary for internal review.
> 
> Agreement title: {agreement_name}
> Parties: {disclosing_party} and {receiving_party}
> Effective date: {start_date}
> Term length: {duration_years_or_perpetual}
> Confidential information scope: {scope_description}
> Key restrictions: {main_obligations}
> Return/destruction clause: {return_requirements}
> Governing law: {jurisdiction}
> Notable exceptions: {carve_outs_if_any}
> 
> Write a 400-500 word executive summary using clear section headers. Start with parties and effective term. Include a "Key Obligations" section with bullet points. Add a "Critical Dates" section. End with enforcement mechanisms and governing law. Use plain English that non-legal stakeholders understand.

**When to use it:** When you need to brief executives or business teams on a newly signed NDA's key terms before they start sharing information.

**Pro tip:** Always include specific return deadlines in the Critical Dates section—business teams forget these and it's the most common compliance failure.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What makes an NDA summary legally sufficient for compliance tracking?
An effective NDA summary must include specific performance deadlines, clear obligation statements that non-legal staff can understand, and measurable compliance metrics. Generic summaries that just restate contract language provide no practical guidance for business teams who need to follow the restrictions daily.

### How quickly should legal teams respond to potential NDA breach situations?
Document preservation and initial assessment should happen within 24 hours of discovery. Most enforceable breaches involve ongoing disclosure, so speed matters more than perfect analysis. Emergency injunctive relief requires court filings within days, not weeks, to demonstrate actual urgency to judges.

### What's the biggest mistake legal professionals make when drafting NDA summaries for business teams?
Using legal terminology instead of operational language. Business teams need to know "delete all files and confirm deletion by email" rather than "return or destroy confidential information pursuant to Section 7.3." Practical compliance requires practical language that matches how people actually work.
