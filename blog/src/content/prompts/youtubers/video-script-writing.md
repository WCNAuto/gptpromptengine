## Tutorial and How-To Scripts

> You are a YouTube script writer creating a step-by-step tutorial video.
> 
> Topic: {tutorial_topic}
> Target audience: {beginner / intermediate / advanced}
> Video length: {5_to_8_minutes / 10_to_15_minutes / 20_plus_minutes}
> Your expertise level: {years_experience_in_this_field}
> Common mistakes people make: {three_to_four_mistakes_in_bullets}
> Tools or materials needed: {list_of_specific_tools}
> Your channel tone: {friendly_teacher / no_nonsense_expert / casual_buddy}
> Call to action: {subscribe / download_free_resource / buy_course}
> 
> Write a 1,200 to 1,500 word script that opens with a hook about the biggest mistake people make with this topic. Structure as: hook, overview of steps, detailed walkthrough with timestamps, common troubleshooting, and strong CTA. Include natural pause points where you'll demonstrate on screen.

**When to use it:** You're filming a tutorial tomorrow and need a complete script that keeps viewers engaged through each step.

**Pro tip:** List your common mistakes from real comments or DMs. The script will feel more authentic when it addresses actual viewer struggles.

**When to use it:** You're creating foundational content for new subscribers who need the absolute basics explained clearly.

**Pro tip:** Record yourself explaining this to a friend first. Use that natural language in your {teaching_style} variable for a more conversational script.

## Product Review and Recommendation Scripts

> You are writing an honest product review script after using something for weeks or months.
> 
> Product: {specific_product_name}
> How long you've used it: {actual_time_period}
> What you used it for: {specific_use_cases}
> Price you paid: {actual_cost}
> Main benefits you experienced: {specific_results_or_advantages}
> Biggest drawbacks: {honest_negatives}
> Who should buy this: {ideal_customer_profile}
> Who should skip it: {who_its_wrong_for}
> Your affiliate status: {yes_i_earn_commission / no_affiliate_relationship}
> 
> Write a 700 to 900 word script that opens with your bottom-line recommendation first. Structure as: verdict, context of how you used it, what works well, what doesn't, price value analysis, final recommendation. Be conversational but thorough. Include specific examples of results.

**When to use it:** You've genuinely used a product long enough to give an informed review and want to build trust with honest assessment.

**Pro tip:** Lead with your conclusion, not a slow build-up. Viewers want to know if you recommend it within the first 30 seconds.

> Write an 800 to 1,000 word comparison script. Open with "if you're torn between X and Y, here's how to choose." Create clear categories: price, features, ease of use, results. Give specific scenarios for when each wins. End with decision framework they can apply.

**When to use it:** Your audience frequently asks about choosing between popular options in your niche.

**Pro tip:** Create a simple visual comparison chart while writing. Your script will be clearer when you know exactly what you're comparing.

## Educational and Explainer Scripts

> You are explaining a complex concept in your field that beginners find confusing.
> 
> Complex concept: {specific_topic_people_struggle_with}
> Why it matters: {practical_importance_in_real_world}
> Common confusion: {what_people_get_wrong_about_it}
> Your simple analogy: {everyday_comparison_that_explains_it}
> Real example: {specific_case_study_or_scenario}
> Key components: {main_parts_they_need_to_understand}
> How it applies to them: {why_your_audience_should_care}
> Next step: {what_to_learn_after_grasping_this}
> 
> Write a 600 to 800 word explainer script that opens with "everyone gets confused by X, but it's actually simple." Use your analogy early and reference it throughout. Avoid jargon. Include a concrete example they can relate to. End with how understanding this changes their approach.

**When to use it:** Comments show viewers struggling with fundamental concepts that block their progress.

**Pro tip:** Test your analogy on someone outside your field first. If they don't get it, find a simpler comparison.

> 
> What happened: {specific_recent_development}
> When it happened: {timeframe}
> Why it matters: {impact_on_your_audience}
> Who this affects: {specific_groups_impacted}
> What changes for viewers: {practical_implications}
> Your take on it: {your_expert_opinion}
> What to do about it: {actionable_response}
> Timeline for impact: {when_theyll_feel_effects}
> 
> Write a 500 to 700 word news analysis script starting with "here's what [recent event] means for you." Explain the development in simple terms, then focus on practical impact for your audience. Avoid speculation - stick to known facts and likely outcomes. End with specific steps they can take to adapt.

**When to use it:** Something significant happened in your industry and your audience needs someone to explain what it means for them practically.

**Pro tip:** Focus more on "what this means for you" than on the news itself. Your audience wants implications, not just information.
