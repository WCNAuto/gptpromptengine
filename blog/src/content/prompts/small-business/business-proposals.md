## RFP Response Proposals

> You are a small business owner responding to an RFP for {project_type} services.
> 
> Client: {client_company_name}
> Project scope: {three_to_four_key_requirements}
> Your business: {your_company_name}, specializing in {your_specialty_area}
> Timeline: {project_duration}
> Budget range: {budget_estimate}
> Key differentiator: {what_makes_you_different}
> Past relevant work: {similar_project_example}
> Team size: {number_of_people_working}
> 
> Write a 600-800 word RFP response proposal. Structure as: Executive Summary, Understanding of Requirements, Proposed Approach, Timeline & Deliverables, Why Choose Us, Investment. Use confident but not overselling tone. Include one specific example of how you solved a similar challenge.

**When to use it:** When you have 2 hours to respond to a formal RFP that just hit your inbox and you need to stand out from 10+ competitors.

**Pro tip:** Always mention the client's company name in the executive summary - many RFP evaluators scan for this to see if proposals are generic or customized.

> 
> Prospect: {business_name}, {business_type}
> Their challenge: {specific_problem_they_mentioned}
> Your solution: {how_you_solve_it}
> Proposed timeline: {start_date} to {completion_date}
> Investment: {total_cost} paid in {payment_structure}
> Guarantee offered: {specific_guarantee}
> Next step: {what_happens_next}
> Meeting context: {where_you_met_them}
> 
> Write a 400-500 word follow-up proposal email. Lead with their specific pain point, explain your 3-step solution process, include the guarantee prominently, and end with one clear next step. Professional but personal tone since you've already met.

**When to use it:** After a networking event or referral meeting where someone expressed interest but you need a written proposal to move forward.

**Pro tip:** Reference something specific from your conversation ("As you mentioned about the inventory issues last Tuesday") to prove this isn't a template.

> 
> Agency: {government_department}
> Project: {official_project_name}
> Compliance requirements: {specific_regulations_or_standards}
> Your certifications: {relevant_licenses_or_certifications}
> Subcontractors: {any_partners_involved}
> Local hiring commitment: {percentage_local_staff}
> Timeline: {project_start} to {project_end}
> Total bid: {dollar_amount}
> 
> Write a 700-900 word government RFP response. Structure as: Company Qualifications, Technical Approach, Compliance & Certifications, Local Economic Impact, Project Timeline, Cost Breakdown. Use formal tone with specific regulation numbers where relevant. Emphasize local business benefits and compliance track record.

**When to use it:** Bidding on city, county, or state contracts where compliance documentation and local preference points matter as much as price.

**Pro tip:** Government evaluators love specific numbers - include exact percentages for local hiring, minority-owned subcontractors, or on-time completion rates from past projects.

> 
> Client: {company_name}
> Project: {project_description}
> Your edge: {main_competitive_advantage}
> Price position: {higher/lower/similar} than competitors because {reason}
> Risk mitigation: {biggest_project_risk} and {how_you_handle_it}

> Delivery method: {how_you_work_differently}

> Write a 500-600 word competitive proposal. Open with your unique angle, explain why your approach reduces their risk, address price positioning directly, and close with a specific success guarantee. Confident tone that acknowledges competition without being defensive.

**When to use it:** When the client has told you they're considering multiple vendors and price isn't the only factor.

**Pro tip:** If you're not the cheapest, lead with risk reduction - most clients fear a failed project more than paying extra for certainty.

## Service Package Proposals

> You are pitching a monthly retainer service for {service_type} to a growing business.
> 
> Client: {business_name}
> Current challenge: {what_they_handle_internally_now}
> Monthly retainer: {dollar_amount}
> Hours included: {number_of_hours}
> Core services: {three_main_deliverables}
> Additional hours rate: {overage_cost}
> Contract length: {minimum_commitment}
> Reporting: {how_you_communicate_results}
> Start date: {proposed_beginning}
> 
> Write a 400-500 word retainer proposal. Explain the value of consistent partnership over project-based work, detail what they get each month, address how overages work, and position this as scaling support for their growth. Professional but relationship-focused tone.

**When to use it:** Converting a one-off project client into ongoing monthly revenue, or pitching retainer work to a referral.

**Pro tip:** Include one "bonus" service that costs you little but feels valuable - like priority response times or monthly strategy calls.

**Pro tip:** Always charge a rush fee for compressed timelines - it covers your opportunity cost and makes clients take deadlines seriously.

## Product Sales Proposals

> You are proposing {product_type} for a business that needs {specific_functionality}.
> 
> Business: {company_name}
> Current situation: {what_they_use_now}
> Product recommended: {your_product_solution}
> Key benefits: {three_main_advantages}
> Implementation timeline: {how_long_to_deploy}
> Training provided: {how_you_get_them_started}
> Total investment: {product_cost_plus_services}
> Ongoing support: {what_happens_after_purchase}
> ROI projection: {expected_return_or_savings}
> 
> Write a 450-550 word product proposal. Lead with their current pain, explain why this product fits, detail the implementation process, project clear ROI, and include training/support to reduce risk. Solution-focused, confidence-building tone.

**When to use it:** Selling software, equipment, or systems where implementation and training matter as much as the product features.

**Pro tip:** Always include implementation time in your timeline - clients fear buying something that sits unused for months.

**Pro tip:** Break custom work into "core system" and "custom features" so they understand what drives the cost.

**When to use it:** When you've identified a business that serves your market differently and could create more value together.

**Pro tip:** Propose a specific small project first to test compatibility before suggesting bigger partnership arrangements.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the difference between an AI proposal prompt and a proposal template?
AI prompts generate custom proposals based on your specific situation, while templates require you to fill in blanks manually. With these prompts, you input your variables and get a fully written proposal draft that matches your exact scenario and tone requirements.

### How do I modify these prompts for different industries or business sizes?
Change the variables to match your situation - swap "retail store" for "manufacturing plant" or "$5,000" for "$50,000" depending on your scale. The prompt structure works across industries; only the specifics need adjustment for your market.

### Can I use these prompts with different AI tools besides ChatGPT and Claude?
Yes, these prompts work with any AI writing tool that accepts detailed instructions. You might need minor formatting adjustments for different platforms, but the core structure - scenario, variables, constraints, and output format - translates across all major AI writing tools.
