## Client-Facing Investment Summaries

> You are an investment advisor writing a quarterly portfolio summary for a high-net-worth client.
> 
> Client: {client_name}
> Portfolio value: {current_value}
> Period performance: {percentage_return}
> Benchmark comparison: {vs_benchmark}
> Top 3 performing positions: {top_performers_with_returns}
> Underperformers: {bottom_two_positions}
> Market conditions: {market_summary_one_sentence}
> Client risk tolerance: {conservative / moderate / aggressive}
> Next quarter outlook: {bullish / neutral / cautious}
> 
> Write a 400-500 word client portfolio summary. Open with performance highlights in the first paragraph. Include a brief market context section. Address underperforming positions with specific reasons and actions taken. Close with forward-looking strategy for next quarter. Use confident but measured tone appropriate for wealthy investors.

**When to use it:** Friday afternoons when quarterly client reports are due and you need to summarize portfolio performance across multiple accounts quickly.

**Pro tip:** Always lead with absolute dollar gains/losses before percentages—clients care more about "$50,000 gain" than "3.2% return."

**When to use it:** Month-end reporting cycles when you need to explain fund performance and positioning changes to pension funds, endowments, or fund-of-funds investors.

**Pro tip:** Institutional investors want attribution analysis—always specify which sectors or positions drove performance, not just overall returns.

> Portfolio impact: {estimated_percentage_impact}
> Client portfolio type: {growth / income / balanced}
> Immediate actions taken: {trades_or_hedges_implemented}

> Recovery timeline estimate: {timeframe_expectation}
> Client communication tone needed: {reassuring / factual / urgent}

> 
> Write a 250-300 word crisis communication to clients. Open by acknowledging the market event and immediate impact. Include one historical parallel for context. Explain specific actions taken to protect portfolios. Close with clear guidance on what clients should or shouldn't do right now. Maintain calm authority without downplaying legitimate concerns.

**When to use it:** During market crashes, geopolitical events, or sudden volatility when clients are calling worried and you need to send consistent messaging quickly.

**Pro tip:** Include specific historical recovery timeframes when possible—"Similar events in 2018 and 2020 saw recovery within 3-6 months" calms clients more than vague reassurance.

## Due Diligence and Research Reports

> You are an equity analyst writing a buy recommendation for the investment committee.
> 
> Company: {company_name}
> Ticker: {stock_ticker}
> Current price: {current_share_price}
> Target price: {twelve_month_target}
> Market cap: {market_capitalization}
> Key investment thesis: {primary_bullish_argument}
> Financial highlights: {revenue_growth_and_margins}
> Competitive advantage: {moat_description}
> Risks: {top_two_risk_factors}
> Valuation method: {dcf / comps / sum_of_parts}
> 
> Write a 500-600 word investment committee memo recommending this stock. Start with clear buy recommendation and target price. Include a compelling thesis paragraph focusing on the strongest bull case. Add a financial performance section with key metrics. Address the main risks honestly. End with valuation justification and position size recommendation. Use confident analytical tone appropriate for internal decision-makers.

**When to use it:** When presenting new stock ideas to investment committees or portfolio managers who need complete analysis and recommendation in standard format.

**Pro tip:** Lead with the strongest part of your thesis—if it's a turnaround story, start there. If it's secular growth, lead with that. Bury weaker supporting arguments.
