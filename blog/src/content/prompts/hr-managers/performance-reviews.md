## Year-End Performance Reviews

> You are an HR manager writing a year-end performance review for a high performer being considered for promotion.
> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}, {tenure} at the company
> Rating: {exceeds_expectations / meets_expectations / below_expectations}
> Key wins this year: {three_specific_accomplishments}
> Growth areas: {two_development_opportunities}
> Promotion consideration: {yes / no / under_review}
> Company tone: {supportive / direct / formal}
> 
> Write a 400-450 word performance review using the SBI model (Situation, Behaviour, Impact). Open with specific recognition of their biggest win. Address growth areas as development opportunities, not deficiencies. Close with two forward-looking goals that align with potential promotion responsibilities.

When to use it: Thursday before your Friday review deadline when you need a complete draft for your star performer.

Pro tip: If promotion is "under_review", focus the closing goals on skills they'd need in the next role. Makes the development path obvious to both employee and leadership.

> You are writing a performance review for an employee who meets expectations but lacks initiative.
> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Department: {department_name}
> Years with company: {tenure_years}
> Core responsibilities: {primary_job_duties}
> Areas where they excel: {two_strengths}
> Initiative concerns: {specific_examples_of_passive_behavior}
> Manager relationship: {good / neutral / strained}
> 
> Write a 300-350 word review that acknowledges their solid foundation while addressing the initiative gap. Use specific examples from {specific_examples_of_passive_behavior}. Frame the development need around "taking ownership" rather than "lacking initiative." Include one concrete action step they can start next quarter.

When to use it: When you need to document performance concerns without demotivating a reliable but passive employee.

Pro tip: Replace "lacks initiative" language with "opportunity to drive projects" - it shifts from deficit to growth mindset while still documenting the issue.

## Difficult Performance Conversations

> You are preparing talking points for a performance conversation about attendance issues.
> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Attendance pattern: {specific_days_hours_frequency}
> Impact on team: {coverage_issues_or_workload_shifts}
> Previous conversations: {dates_and_outcomes_of_prior_discussions}
> Personal circumstances: {known_factors_if_shared}
> Policy implications: {warnings_given_or_next_steps}
> Desired outcome: {behavior_change / documentation / final_warning}
> 
> Create a 200-250 word script for a direct but supportive conversation about attendance. Open with specific data from {attendance_pattern}. Acknowledge any personal circumstances without excusing the behavior. Close with clear expectations and timeline. Include language for documenting the conversation in their file.

When to use it: Monday morning when you need to address Friday's no-show and you're tired of having the same conversation.

Pro tip: Start with impact on others, not policy violations. "The team had to cover your Tuesday meetings" lands harder than "you violated policy 4.2."

Pro tip: Use the customer's exact words in quotes - it makes the feedback concrete and harder to dismiss as "just your opinion."

## Promotion and Development Reviews

> You are writing a promotion recommendation for a high-potential employee.
> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Current role: {current_job_title}
> Target role: {promotion_position_desired}
> Time in current role: {months_or_years}
> Promotion-ready evidence: {leadership_examples_expanded_scope_results}
> Skills gap to address: {what_they_need_to_develop}
> Internal competition: {strong / moderate / none}
> Timeline preference: {immediate / next_quarter / next_year}
> 
> Write a 400-450 word promotion recommendation that builds a compelling case for advancement. Lead with their biggest leadership example from {promotion_ready_evidence}. Address the skills gap from {skills_gap_to_address} as development opportunities in the new role, not disqualifiers. Include specific metrics or outcomes that demonstrate readiness. Close with recommended timeline and support needed.

When to use it: When you need to advocate upward for someone's promotion and want to present the strongest possible case.

Pro tip: Include a comparison to others who've been promoted into similar roles - it gives leadership context for the decision.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the difference between performance review prompts and regular HR templates?
These prompts generate complete, customized performance reviews ready for final editing. Traditional templates give you blank forms to fill out yourself. The AI does the writing - you just provide the employee details and context.

### Can I modify these prompts for different performance management systems?
Yes, adjust the constraints section to match your company's rating scales, word limits, or required frameworks. The core prompt structure works with any performance management platform.

### How do I handle sensitive performance issues using AI-generated content?
Use these prompts for initial drafts, then review carefully for tone and legal compliance. For serious performance issues or potential terminations, have HR or legal review the final version before delivery.
