## Workforce Planning and Headcount Justification

> You are an HR Manager writing a headcount justification memo for a senior leadership team.
>
> Role being requested: {job_title}
> Department: {department_name}
> Business case trigger: {trigger: e.g. backlog growth / new product line / attrition spike}
> Current team size: {current_headcount}
> Revenue or output impact of the gap: {business_impact_in_one_sentence}
> Urgency: {urgency: immediate / next quarter / annual plan}
> Tone: {tone: direct / formal / persuasive}
>
> Write a 300 to 380 word headcount justification memo. Open with a one-sentence summary of the risk if the role stays unfilled. Present the business case in three numbered points using quantified language where possible. Close with a specific hiring timeline and a recommended approval date. Do not use HR jargon. Write as if the reader has 90 seconds.

**When to use it:** Monday morning before your leadership sync when a department head is pushing for a new hire and you need something on paper before the meeting.

**Pro tip:** If your {business_impact_in_one_sentence} is vague (e.g. "the team is overwhelmed"), ChatGPT will produce a vague memo. Spend 30 seconds quantifying it first — even an estimate like "delaying 3 client onboardings per month" sharpens the whole output.

> You are an HR Manager drafting a workforce gap analysis brief for a quarterly board update.
>
> Company size: {company_size}
> Industry: {industry}
> Roles currently vacant: {open_roles_list}
> Roles forecasted to open in 90 days: {forecasted_openings}
> Hardest skill to hire right now: {critical_skill_shortage}
> Current average time-to-fill: {avg_days_to_fill} days
>
> Write a 250 to 320 word workforce gap analysis brief. Structure it as: Current State (2 to 3 sentences), Risk (what happens if gaps persist), Action Required (three concrete steps with owners). Use plain prose, not bullet soup. Close with one recommended decision the board should make this quarter.

**When to use it:** Before a quarterly business review when you need to show leadership the recruitment picture without building a slide deck from scratch.

**Pro tip:** Paste the finished brief into the speaker notes of a blank slide. It reads well as talking points and doubles as a leave-behind without any extra formatting work.

> You are an HR Manager writing an internal memo explaining a new headcount freeze to hiring managers.
>
> Freeze scope: {freeze_scope: company-wide / specific departments}
> Departments affected: {affected_departments}
> Freeze duration: {freeze_duration}
> Exceptions allowed: {exceptions: yes — critical roles only / no exceptions}
> Reason to share publicly: {official_reason}
> Tone: {tone: empathetic / matter-of-fact / formal}
>
> Write a 200 to 260 word internal memo. Open with the decision clearly stated in the first sentence — no burying the lead. Explain the business reason in two sentences. List what managers can and cannot do during the freeze in two short bullet groups. Close with one named contact for exception requests and a review date.

**When to use it:** The afternoon a freeze decision is made, before managers start sending you Slack messages asking if their open role is still live.

**Pro tip:** Add the exception contact name and review date as your actual variables before running the prompt — if ChatGPT invents a placeholder name, hiring managers will catch it immediately and your credibility takes a hit.

> You are an HR Manager writing talking points for a conversation with a department head who wants to hire three people this quarter but only one is budgeted.
>
> Department head name: {manager_name}
> Roles requested: {three_roles_listed}
> Approved role: {approved_role}
> Reason the other two are not approved: {budget_reason}
> Alternative you can offer: {alternative: contract / delayed start / restructured scope}
> Your relationship with this manager: {relationship: new / established / tense}
>
> Write a set of 5 to 7 concise talking points for a 15-minute in-person conversation. Each talking point is one to two sentences, written as spoken language — not formal prose. Start with acknowledgement, move to the constraint, then pivot to the alternative offer. End with one specific next step and a date.

**When to use it:** Twenty minutes before a difficult conversation with a manager who will push back on budget limits.

**Pro tip:** Run the prompt twice with different {relationship} values. The "tense" version will surface more careful phrasing that's worth keeping even when the relationship is fine.

> You are an HR Manager writing a one-page workforce planning summary to onboard a new CHRO who is joining in two weeks.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Current open roles: {number_of_open_roles}
> Roles on hold: {roles_on_hold}
> Biggest hiring bottleneck right now: {main_bottleneck}
> Top three roles by priority: {priority_roles_list}
> One thing the new CHRO should know immediately: {critical_context}
>
> Write a 350 to 430 word briefing document. Use three sections: Where We Are (current state in plain numbers), Where It's Stuck (the bottleneck and why), What Needs a Decision (the two or three things only a CHRO can unblock). Write in a tone that is direct and collegial — not deferential. Do not pad. The CHRO will read this on their first day.

**When to use it:** When a new CHRO is starting and you need to brief them without booking an hour on their calendar.

**Pro tip:** Include one uncomfortable truth in {critical_context} — something like "the VP of Engineering rejects 80% of candidates before interview." A sanitised briefing document protects no one and costs you credibility with a new leader.

## Job Advertising and Candidate Attraction

> You are an HR Manager writing a job advertisement for a specialist role that has been hard to fill.
>
> Job title: {job_title}
> Department: {department}
> What the person will actually do in week one: {week_one_reality}
> Three non-negotiable requirements: {must_haves}
> Two things that are nice to have but not required: {nice_to_haves}
> What makes this role genuinely interesting: {honest_sell}
> Salary range: {salary_range}
> Remote/hybrid/on-site: {work_arrangement}
>
> Write a 280 to 340 word job advertisement in plain English. Do not use corporate jargon or superlatives like "rockstar" or "dynamic". Open with what the person will own, not a paragraph about the company. List must-haves as three bullet points. Close with the salary range and one sentence on the interview process so candidates know what they're walking into.

**When to use it:** When a role has been posted for three weeks with low-quality applicants and you need a rewrite before the next push.

**Pro tip:** If the job has been live and underperforming, add this sentence to the prompt: "The previous version of this ad used [paste old ad summary here] — avoid repeating those phrases." ChatGPT will differentiate the tone without you having to diagnose what went wrong.

> You are an HR Manager writing a LinkedIn post to attract passive candidates for a senior individual contributor role.
>
> Role: {job_title}
> The problem this person will solve: {problem_statement}
> One specific project they'll lead in the first 90 days: {first_project}
> What kind of person thrives here: {culture_fit_in_one_line}
> What kind of person struggles here: {honest_downside}
> Application link or contact: {call_to_action}
>
> Write a LinkedIn post of 180 to 220 words. No hashtag lists. No "we're hiring!" as the first line. Open with the problem the role solves — make it sound like a real challenge, not a job description. Write in first person, as if the HR Manager is speaking directly. Include one line that names who this role is NOT for — it will attract the right people by filtering the wrong ones. End with a low-friction call to action.

**When to use it:** When you need a LinkedIn post that actually gets engagement from people who aren't actively job hunting.

**Pro tip:** The {honest_downside} line is where most HR managers get vague. "Fast-paced environment" filters nobody. Specifics like "you'll have no dedicated design support" or "the roadmap changes frequently" filter the right people and make the post more credible.

> You are an HR Manager writing a targeted outreach message to a passive candidate on LinkedIn.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Their current role: {candidate_current_role}
> Specific thing about their profile that prompted the message: {specific_observation}
> Role you are reaching out about: {open_role}
> One genuine reason this role fits their background: {fit_reason}
> Your name and title: {your_name_and_title}
>
> Write a LinkedIn InMail of 90 to 120 words. Do not open with "I hope this message finds you well." Do not describe the company at length. Lead with the specific observation about their profile. State the role in one sentence. Give one concrete reason their background fits. End with a low-pressure question — not "are you interested?" but something that invites a response without requiring a commitment. Sign off with your name and title.

**When to use it:** When your recruiter has a list of 10 passive candidates to contact this week and the standard template is getting no replies.

**Pro tip:** Run the prompt once per candidate, changing only {specific_observation} and {fit_reason}. The message reads bespoke even though 80% of it is consistent — that's the balance between personalisation and throughput.

> You are an HR Manager writing a job advertisement for an entry-level role at a company competing against larger employers for early-career talent.
>
> Job title: {job_title}
> What the person will learn in year one: {learning_outcomes}
> Two senior people they will work directly with: {mentors_or_leads}
> Day-to-day reality in one sentence: {daily_reality}
> Starting salary: {starting_salary}
> One thing larger companies can't offer that you can: {differentiator}
>
> Write a 240 to 300 word job ad targeting candidates who are six to eighteen months into their career and evaluating multiple offers. Lead with what they will learn, not what they will do. Include one sentence about who they'll learn from. Be honest about the role's limitations. Close with a one-line pitch on why a smaller company is the better move at this stage of their career.

**When to use it:** When you're hiring for a graduate or associate role and your job post is losing to companies with bigger brand names and slightly higher starting salaries.

**Pro tip:** Add the actual names of the two mentors or leads — not just "senior team members." Named people make the ad feel like an actual commitment, not a marketing claim. Confirm with those people first.

> You are an HR Manager writing a short email to a warm candidate who applied three months ago for a role that was paused, and you now have a similar opening.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Original role they applied for: {original_role}
> Reason for pause (shareable): {pause_reason}
> New role you're reaching out about: {new_role}
> Key difference between the two roles: {role_difference}
> Next step you want them to take: {next_step}
>
> Write an email of 130 to 170 words. Acknowledge the gap and the original application in the opening sentence — don't pretend the time away didn't happen. Explain the new role in two sentences and name one specific overlap with their original application. State the next step clearly: a link, a call, or a reply. Tone should be warm and efficient — not overly apologetic.

**When to use it:** When you're reopening a search and have a pipeline of previous applicants who went cold because of a freeze, not because of fit.

**Pro tip:** If it has been more than four months, add one sentence acknowledging that their situation may have changed and they're under no obligation. It feels respectful and it removes the awkwardness that kills re-engagement emails.

## Interview Process and Candidate Experience

> You are an HR Manager writing a structured interview guide for a hiring panel assessing a mid-level candidate.
>
> Role being interviewed for: {job_title}
> Interview format: {format: panel / one-on-one / sequential}
> Number of interviewers: {interviewer_count}
> Three competencies to assess: {competency_1}, {competency_2}, {competency_3}
> One red flag to watch for based on previous hires who failed: {historical_red_flag}
> Interview length: {interview_length_minutes} minutes
>
> Write a structured interview guide as a document the panel can print and use on the day. For each of the three competencies, write two behavioural questions using the STAR framework format (not labelled STAR, just written to elicit Situation, Task, Action, Result). Include one follow-up probe per question. Add a 50-word interviewer brief at the top: what this role actually needs and what to listen for. Total length 400 to 480 words.

**When to use it:** When a panel interview is coming up and your interviewers have not assessed this role before and need a shared rubric fast.

**Pro tip:** The {historical_red_flag} variable is the most valuable input in this prompt. If you don't have one from a previous hire, ask the hiring manager what went wrong last time someone left this role within 12 months. That answer shapes better questions than any competency framework.

> You are an HR Manager writing a candidate briefing email to send the day before a final-round interview.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Interview date and time: {interview_datetime}
> Location or video link: {location_or_link}
> Number of interviewers and their names/titles: {interviewers_list}
> Interview format: {format: case study / competency / technical / exec conversation}
> One thing to prepare: {preparation_prompt}
> Parking or access notes (if in person): {logistics_note}
>
> Write a 180 to 220 word email. Open with a one-sentence welcome that does not sound automated. List the logistics clearly in a short block: time, location or link, who they'll meet, format. Give one specific prep suggestion — not "research the company" but something actually useful. Close with your direct contact details and a line that invites last-minute questions. Tone should be warm and competent — this email shapes how the candidate feels walking into the room.

**When to use it:** The afternoon before a final-round interview when you want the candidate to feel prepared rather than anxious.

**Pro tip:** If the interview includes a case study or presentation, the {preparation_prompt} variable is where you specify exactly what they should prepare. Vague prep notes like "be ready to discuss your experience" make final-round nerves worse, not better.
