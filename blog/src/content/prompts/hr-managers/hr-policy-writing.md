## Writing Core Employment Policies from Scratch

> You are an HR manager drafting a formal employment policy document.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Industry: {industry}
> Policy topic: {policy_topic} (e.g. remote work eligibility, meal break requirements)
> Jurisdiction: {state_or_country}
> Workforce size: {headcount}
> Tone: {formal / plain-language / bilingual-note-required}
> Effective date: {effective_date}
>
> Write a complete 400 to 500 word policy document with the following sections in order: Purpose, Scope, Policy Statement, Procedures, Responsibilities, and Non-Compliance Consequences. Use numbered subsections. Write every clause in plain English so a new hire with no HR background can understand it. Do not include a review schedule section — that will be added separately.

**When to use it:** Monday morning when legal has flagged a compliance gap and wants a first draft by end of day.

**Pro tip:** If your jurisdiction is California, add "include meal-period penalty language consistent with California Labor Code Section 512" at the end of the prompt — generic output often misses the premium pay obligation for missed breaks.

> You are an HR compliance specialist writing an attendance and punctuality policy.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Work model: {fully_onsite / hybrid / fully_remote}
> Shift structure: {fixed_hours / flexible_hours / shift_based}
> Notice requirement for absences: {hours_notice_required}
> Progressive discipline stages: {verbal_warning / written_warning / PIP / termination}
> Jurisdiction: {state_or_country}
>
> Write a 350 to 450 word attendance policy. Open with a one-sentence purpose statement. Define "tardy", "absence", and "no-call no-show" with specific time thresholds. Lay out the progressive discipline path with trigger points (e.g. number of occurrences per rolling 90 days). Close with a paragraph on accommodations for FMLA or ADA-covered situations. Use plain, direct language — no legal jargon.

**When to use it:** After a manager comes to you complaining that your current attendance policy does not define what "late" actually means.

**Pro tip:** Specify the rolling window (30, 60, or 90 days) in {trigger_period} if you want the output to be directly usable — otherwise the AI defaults to a calendar-year count that almost never matches how your managers actually track attendance.

> You are an HR manager writing a conflict of interest policy for a private employer.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Industry: {industry}
> Key risk scenarios in your business: {two_to_four_conflict_scenarios} (e.g. vendor relationships, secondary employment, hiring relatives)
> Disclosure process owner: {HR / Legal / direct_manager}
> Consequence for non-disclosure: {specific_discipline_outcome}
> Tone: {formal / plain-language}
>
> Write a 400 to 500 word conflict of interest policy. Define "conflict of interest" in the first paragraph using one concrete example from the scenarios provided. Include a disclosure procedure with named steps and a timeframe. Specify what happens if a conflict is found to be unavoidable. Close with a statement on annual re-acknowledgment. Do not produce a disclosure form — just the policy text.

**When to use it:** Before a board meeting where a governance gap in your employee handbook has been flagged.

**Pro tip:** If your company does government contracting, add "flag requirements under FAR 3.101 if applicable" to the prompt — output without that note will miss mandatory disclosure timelines that apply to federal subcontractors.

> You are an HR manager drafting a social media policy for employees.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Industry: {industry}
> Biggest documented risk: {data_breach / brand_damage / regulatory_violation / harassment}
> Employee types in scope: {all_employees / managers_only / customer-facing_roles}
> Examples of prohibited conduct: {two_to_three_specific_examples}
> Consequence range: {coaching_to_termination / written_warning_minimum}
>
> Write a 300 to 400 word social media policy. Lead with a one-sentence position statement that is not a ban. Cover personal account use, work-time use, confidential information, and brand representation in four short numbered sections. End with one paragraph on NLRA-protected activity, written in plain language without citing the statute by name. No bullet points — use short paragraphs throughout.

**When to use it:** After an employee posts something that makes it to the exec team's Slack before you've even seen it.

**Pro tip:** The NLRA paragraph is the most litigated section of social media policies. Tell the AI to "avoid language that could be read as prohibiting employees from discussing wages or working conditions with each other" — without that instruction it often produces language the NLRB would consider overbroad.

> You are an HR manager writing a dress code and appearance policy.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Work environment: {office / manufacturing_floor / customer-facing_retail / hybrid}
> Safety requirements that affect dress: {steel-toed_boots_required / none / PPE_specified_elsewhere}
> Religious accommodation language: {include / exclude — legal has separate doc}
> Examples of non-compliant attire in your context: {two_to_three_specific_examples}
> Tone: {formal / conversational}
>
> Write a 250 to 350 word dress code policy. Open with the business rationale in one sentence. Use three short sections: Business Attire Standards, Safety-Specific Requirements, and Exceptions and Accommodations. In the Exceptions section, note the process for requesting accommodation without describing every scenario. End with the consequence for repeated non-compliance. Write it so a new hire reads it in under two minutes.

**When to use it:** When a manager asks you three times in one month what "business casual" actually means and you realise you have never formally defined it.

**Pro tip:** Avoid gendered language in dress code policies — courts have increasingly treated gender-differentiated appearance standards as Title VII exposure. Add "use gender-neutral language throughout and do not specify different standards for men and women" to the prompt if you are in any industry where this has been litigated.

## Updating Policies After Legal or Regulatory Changes

> You are an HR manager rewriting an existing remote work policy to reflect a regulatory update.
>
> Current policy excerpt: {paste_current_policy_section}
> What changed legally or operationally: {description_of_change}
> Jurisdiction of change: {state_or_country}
> New effective date: {effective_date}
> Employees affected: {all_remote / hybrid_only / specific_department}
> Approval required before rollout: {yes — CHRO / yes — Legal / no}
>
> Rewrite the policy section provided. Keep all language that is still compliant. Flag any clause you are removing and write one sentence explaining why it no longer holds. Produce the revised section at 300 to 400 words. Use the same structure as the original. End with a one-paragraph "What Changed and Why" summary written for employees, not lawyers.

**When to use it:** The week a state-level wage or leave law goes into effect and your current policy is a patchwork from three years ago.

**Pro tip:** Paste the current policy text directly into the prompt rather than describing it — the AI will mirror your existing structure and numbering, which makes the legal review diff much faster because counsel can track exact changes.

> You are an HR compliance manager drafting an amendment notice for an existing PTO policy.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Policy being amended: {policy_name_and_version}
> What is changing: {specific_change_in_plain_terms}
> What is not changing: {elements_staying_the_same}
> Effective date of amendment: {effective_date}
> Distribution method: {email_to_all_staff / posted_to_intranet / handed_out_at_team_meetings}
>
> Write a 200 to 250 word policy amendment notice addressed to all employees. Open with the date and a one-sentence summary of what is changing and when. Use a short "What's New" paragraph and a "What Stays the Same" paragraph. Close with contact information for questions: {hr_contact_name} at {hr_email}. Do not use bullet points. Write it so it can be pasted into an email and sent without additional formatting.

**When to use it:** When you are rolling out a PTO payout change on short notice and need staff communication and the policy update in one go.

**Pro tip:** Keep "What Stays the Same" substantive — employees fixate on what is being taken away. If the notice only describes changes, you will get three times as many calls asking whether other benefits are also affected.

> You are an HR manager writing a policy update memo to department managers explaining a change to your expense reimbursement policy.
>
> Change being made: {description_of_change}
> Reason for change: {cost_control / IRS_update / audit_finding / vendor_change}
> New approval threshold: {dollar_amount}
> New submission deadline: {timeframe_after_expense}
> Who is affected: {all_managers / director_level_and_above / field_staff_only}
> Tone: {direct / explanatory}
>
> Write a 250 to 300 word internal memo from HR to department managers. Open with the effective date and the change in the first sentence. Explain the reason in two sentences without over-explaining. List the three most common expense types affected and how the new rule applies to each. Close with the manager's responsibility to communicate this to their direct reports and the deadline for doing so. Format as a memo: To, From, Date, Re, Body.

**When to use it:** After finance sends you an audit finding on Friday afternoon and wants manager acknowledgment by Wednesday.

**Pro tip:** Name the three expense types based on what your finance team actually flags most — meal expenses, mileage, and software subscriptions are the most common, but if your managers mostly submit travel and accommodation costs, substitute those in the {description_of_change} variable so the output is immediately usable.

> You are an HR manager drafting a revised harassment and discrimination policy following a legal review.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Jurisdiction: {state_or_country}
> Protected classes to explicitly name: {list_from_legal_review}
> Reporting channels available: {HR_direct / anonymous_hotline / ombudsperson / external_investigator}
> Maximum investigation timeline: {number_of_business_days}
> Anti-retaliation language: {include_strong / include_standard}
>
> Write a 500 to 600 word harassment and discrimination policy. Sections: Policy Statement, Scope, Prohibited Conduct (with four concrete behavioral examples, not a general list), Reporting Procedure, Investigation Process, Anti-Retaliation, and Consequences. Write every example in the Prohibited Conduct section as a specific behavior, not a category label. Avoid the phrase "zero tolerance" — it creates legal risk if the company has ever issued a warning rather than immediate termination.

**When to use it:** When legal comes back from an EEOC charge and tells you the current policy language is too vague to be defensible.

**Pro tip:** "Zero tolerance" is the most common phrase that creates liability in harassment policies — if you have ever issued a written warning for conduct the policy calls zero tolerance, you have a gap a plaintiff's attorney will find. The prompt already flags this, but double-check the AI output for synonyms like "will not be tolerated under any circumstances" which carry the same risk.

> You are an HR manager rewriting a termination policy after a wrongful termination claim prompted a legal audit.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Employment type covered: {at-will / union / mixed}
> Termination types to address: {voluntary / involuntary_for_cause / layoff / end_of_contract}
> Documentation required before involuntary termination: {list_of_required_steps}
> Final pay timeline required by law: {jurisdiction_and_number_of_days}
> Severance offered: {yes — outline / no}
>
> Write a 450 to 550 word termination policy. Cover each termination type in its own numbered section. For involuntary termination, list the documentation steps in the order they must happen, with the responsible party named for each step. Include final pay language that matches the jurisdiction. End with a one-paragraph note on offboarding checklist completion — who owns it and the deadline. Do not include severance amounts — reference only that severance terms are governed by a separate agreement.

**When to use it:** After your employment attorney tells you the current policy has no documentation requirements and your managers are treating it as a formality.

**Pro tip:** The final pay section is where state-specific requirements cause the most problems — California requires final pay on the day of termination for involuntary separations, not within three days. Specify your state explicitly in the {jurisdiction_and_number_of_days} variable and verify the AI output against your state's Department of Labor site before using it.

## Disciplinary Letters and Performance Improvement Plans

> You are an HR manager writing a formal written warning letter for a conduct violation.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Job title: {job_title}
> Department: {department}
> Violation: {specific_conduct_violation_in_one_sentence}
> Date(s) of incident: {incident_dates}
> Prior warnings issued: {none / verbal_warning_on_date}
> Expected behavior going forward: {two_to_three_specific_expectations}
> Consequence if behavior continues: {up_to_and_including_termination / specific_next_step}
>
> Write a 300 to 350 word written warning letter. Use business letter format with date, employee name, and subject line. Open with the specific violation and dates — no preamble. State what policy was violated and cite the policy name. Describe the expected behavior change in concrete, observable terms. State the consequence of recurrence. Close with a signature line for the employee to acknowledge receipt, with a note that signature does not imply agreement. Do not use emotional or judgmental language.

**When to use it:** The afternoon a manager documents a conduct issue and HR needs to issue a formal letter before the business day closes.

**Pro tip:** "Signature does not imply agreement" is not optional — employees who refuse to sign a warning letter without that clause create a documentation gap. If your state requires a witness signature when the employee refuses, add that instruction to the prompt.
