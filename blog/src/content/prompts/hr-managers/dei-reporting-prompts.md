## Annual DEI Report Writing

> You are an HR Manager writing the annual DEI report for an organisation.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Reporting period: {reporting_year}
> Total headcount: {total_headcount}
> Current workforce diversity breakdown (by protected characteristic, as percentages): {diversity_breakdown}
> Year-on-year change from last cycle: {yoy_change_in_percentage_points}
> Three headline DEI initiatives run this year: {three_initiatives}
> Tone: {formal / plain-language / investor-facing}
>
> Write a 550 to 650 word annual DEI report section titled "Workforce Diversity Overview." Open with the headline numbers in a short paragraph. Dedicate one paragraph each to the three initiatives, describing what was done and what changed. Close with a two-sentence forward-looking commitment statement. Use plain declarative sentences. Do not use bullet points. Do not include graphs or placeholders for graphs.

**When to use it:** Monday morning before the Board pack is due and you still need the narrative section drafted.

**Pro tip:** If your year-on-year change is negative in any category, feed that number in honestly. The AI will frame it neutrally. If you soften the input to protect yourself, the executive team will notice the gap between the narrative and the actual data table sitting next to it.

> You are an HR Manager writing the pay equity section of an annual DEI report.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Reporting year: {reporting_year}
> Gender pay gap (mean, as a percentage): {mean_gender_pay_gap}
> Gender pay gap (median, as a percentage): {median_gender_pay_gap}
> Ethnicity pay gap (mean, if measured): {ethnicity_pay_gap_or_not_measured}
> Actions taken to close the gap this year: {two_to_three_actions}
> Target gap figure for next reporting cycle: {target_gap_figure}
>
> Write a 300 to 380 word pay equity narrative for inclusion in the annual DEI report. Lead with the median figure first, explain the mean figure second. Acknowledge the gap without minimising it. Name each action taken and state its specific intended effect. End with the target figure and the deadline. Tone: transparent and factual. No hedging language.

**When to use it:** When the gender pay gap data has just landed from payroll and you need the narrative ready before legal reviews the numbers.

**Pro tip:** The phrase "our gap is partly explained by occupational segregation" will appear in the AI's output if the data implies it. Review that sentence before it reaches your General Counsel. It is accurate but has specific legal implications in some jurisdictions.

> You are an HR Manager writing the DEI report section covering hiring and promotion equity for a mid-size organisation.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Reporting period: {reporting_year}
> Percentage of external hires from underrepresented groups: {hire_percentage}
> Percentage of promotions awarded to underrepresented groups: {promotion_percentage}
> Representation of underrepresented groups in the total workforce: {workforce_representation_percentage}
> Biggest structural barrier identified this year: {named_barrier}
> One concrete action planned for next year: {planned_action}
>
> Write a 350 to 420 word hiring and promotion equity section for the annual DEI report. Compare hire and promotion rates directly to workforce representation. Name whether each figure shows over-representation, proportional representation, or under-representation. Describe the structural barrier in one paragraph without blame language. Close with the planned action framed as a measurable commitment. Formal tone.

**When to use it:** When your HRIS data export has landed and you need to turn raw percentages into a readable board narrative within the hour.

**Pro tip:** If your promotion percentage is lower than your hire percentage for the same group, name that explicitly in the variable. The AI will highlight the gap. Leaving it implicit and letting reviewers find it themselves is worse for your credibility than surfacing it in the draft.

> You are an HR Manager writing the inclusion index section of an annual DEI report.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Reporting year: {reporting_year}
> Employee survey inclusion score (out of 100): {inclusion_score}
> Change from prior year: {score_change_in_points}
> Response rate: {response_rate_percentage}
> Lowest scoring survey item verbatim: {lowest_scoring_item}
> Action being taken on the lowest scoring item: {action_description}
>
> Write a 280 to 340 word inclusion survey results section. Open by stating the score and response rate in the same sentence. Note the year-on-year change with plain commentary on what it means. Quote the lowest scoring item verbatim. Describe the action being taken in concrete terms. Do not use the word "journey" or the phrase "continued progress." Tone: candid and specific.

**When to use it:** After Qualtrics or Culture Amp exports the annual pulse data and you have 45 minutes before the CHRO asks for a summary.

**Pro tip:** A response rate below 50 percent will make the inclusion score statistically weak. Feed the actual response rate in; the AI will flag the limitation in the text. Better to have it in the report than for a board member to raise it in the room.

> You are an HR Manager writing the disability and accessibility section of an annual DEI report.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Reporting year: {reporting_year}
> Percentage of workforce who have disclosed a disability: {disclosed_disability_percentage}
> Whether disclosure is voluntary or mandatory in your jurisdiction: {voluntary_or_mandatory}
> Reasonable adjustments made this year (number and type): {adjustment_count_and_types}
> One accessibility initiative completed this year: {initiative_description}
> Known disclosure gap or barrier: {disclosure_barrier}
>
> Write a 300 to 360 word disability and accessibility narrative for the annual DEI report. Open by contextualising the disclosure percentage relative to national prevalence rates (approximately 20 percent in most OECD countries). Address the disclosure gap candidly. Describe the adjustments and the initiative with specific language. Close with one sentence acknowledging what is still unknown due to under-disclosure. Formal tone. Do not describe disabled employees as "inspiring" or "overcoming challenges."

**When to use it:** When preparing the full annual report pack and the disability section is the last one your team has avoided drafting because the data feels thin.

**Pro tip:** If disclosed disability is below 3 percent of headcount, the AI will produce language that acknowledges likely under-disclosure. Do not delete that paragraph. Under-reporting is the real story in disability data and stakeholders who know DEI will look for it.

## Quarterly DEI Progress Updates for Leadership

> You are an HR Manager writing a quarterly DEI progress update for the executive leadership team.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Quarter and year: {quarter_and_year}
> One DEI metric that improved this quarter: {metric_and_change}
> One DEI metric that did not move or worsened: {metric_and_change_negative}
> Initiative launched or completed this quarter: {initiative_name_and_status}
> Planned action for next quarter: {next_quarter_action}
> Tone: {direct / cautiously optimistic / data-led}
>
> Write a 250 to 300 word quarterly DEI update for a leadership team deck. Use a three-part structure: what moved, what did not, and what is next. One paragraph per section. Name numbers precisely. Do not use the phrase "we are committed to." Do not pad with mission statements. Output is the written narrative only, not slide bullets.

**When to use it:** The Thursday before the monthly ExCo meeting when the DEI slide still says "TBC."

**Pro tip:** Feed in the metric that did not move before the metric that improved. The AI will sequence the narrative better — progress lands harder after the honest admission than before it.

> You are an HR Manager preparing a one-page DEI briefing for a new C-suite executive joining the organisation.
>
> Executive's name: {executive_name}
> Executive's role: {executive_role}
> Start date: {start_date}
> Company's current DEI headline metric (e.g. gender split at senior level): {headline_metric}
> One active DEI initiative relevant to their function: {relevant_initiative}
> One known DEI risk or gap in their area: {known_gap}
> Tone: {briefing-style / conversational / formal}
>
> Write a 300 to 350 word one-page DEI briefing document this executive will read before their first week. Open with the single most important DEI figure they need to know. Describe the active initiative in two sentences. Name the known gap directly, without softening it. Close with two questions the executive should be ready to answer from their team in week one. Headed document format with short paragraphs.

**When to use it:** When a new CPO, CFO, or COO starts next week and nobody has prepared them for what their team will expect on DEI.

**Pro tip:** The "two questions from their team" the AI generates are often more incisive than you expect. Run them past the CHRO before the document is sent — occasionally one will touch a live employee relations issue the AI cannot know about.

> You are an HR Manager writing a mid-year DEI checkpoint memo to a senior leadership team.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Checkpoint date: {date}
> Annual DEI target set in January: {annual_target}
> Current progress toward that target in numbers: {current_progress_figures}
> Whether you are on track, behind, or ahead: {on_track_behind_or_ahead}
> Root cause of gap or explanation of outperformance: {root_cause_or_explanation}
> Proposed adjustment to plan: {proposed_adjustment}
>
> Write a 220 to 270 word mid-year memo to a senior leadership team. State the target and the current position in the first two sentences. Explain the gap or outperformance with a named cause, not a general statement. Propose the adjustment with clear language about what changes, who owns it, and by when. Memo format with date, to/from lines, and subject line. No bullet points.

**When to use it:** July, when the annual DEI targets set in January look optimistic and you need to tell leadership before they ask.

**Pro tip:** If you are behind target, avoid inputting "external factors" as the root cause unless you can name a specific one. Vague attribution produces weak memos that leadership will push back on in the room.

> You are an HR Manager writing a leadership team talking points document for an all-hands meeting that will cover DEI results.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> All-hands date: {meeting_date}
> DEI result being shared: {result_and_figure}
> Whether the result is positive or disappointing: {positive_or_disappointing}
> Likely employee question the result will provoke: {anticipated_question}
> Honest answer to that question: {honest_answer}
> Tone: {transparent / motivating / steady}
>
> Write a 300 to 360 word talking points document for the CEO or CHRO to use at an all-hands meeting covering DEI results. Structure: opening statement on the result (two sentences), context for the number (one paragraph), address the anticipated employee question directly using the honest answer provided, and a closing commitment with a named next action and timeline. Format as labelled talking points the speaker reads from, not a script they deliver verbatim.

**When to use it:** 48 hours before an all-hands where a DEI figure is going public internally and leadership has not prepared their narrative.

**Pro tip:** Input the anticipated question as the bluntest version employees will actually ask, not a polished version. If the true question is "why has nothing changed in two years," put that in. The AI will produce an answer that addresses the real concern rather than a softened version of it.

> You are an HR Manager writing a DEI dashboard commentary for a board of directors pack.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Board meeting date: {board_date}
> Three KPIs on the DEI dashboard with current figures: {kpi_one_name_and_figure}, {kpi_two_name_and_figure}, {kpi_three_name_and_figure}
> Single biggest DEI risk on the board's radar: {board_level_risk}
> Recommended board action or decision: {recommended_action}
>
> Write a 200 to 250 word board pack commentary section that sits below the DEI data table. Do not repeat the numbers already visible in the table. Instead, interpret what the numbers mean: identify the trend, name the risk, and make the recommended action explicit. Use board-level language — concise, risk-aware, decision-oriented. End with a one-sentence ask of the board.

**When to use it:** The evening before board papers are locked and the DEI dashboard data is in but the written commentary is still blank.

**Pro tip:** "Board-level language" means cutting any sentence that does not tell the board something they need to decide or know. If the AI produces more than two sentences of context before naming the risk, trim them. Boards read commentary fast and stop at the first thing that feels like background.

## DEI Incident and Response Communications

> You are an HR Manager drafting an internal communication to all staff following a reported workplace discrimination incident.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Date of communication: {communication_date}
> Nature of incident (general terms, no names or identifying details): {incident_description_in_general_terms}
> What action has been taken so far: {actions_taken}
> What employees need to know about next steps: {next_steps}
> Tone: {firm and reassuring / transparent / legally cautious}
>
> Write a 250 to 300 word all-staff communication. Open by acknowledging that an incident occurred without naming individuals or implying guilt. State what the organisation has done in response. Tell employees what they can do if they have concerns or information. Close with a one-paragraph restatement of the organisation's position on discrimination. Avoid legal conclusions. Avoid minimising language like "isolated incident."

**When to use it:** Within 24 hours of a discrimination complaint becoming known more broadly in the team, before rumours outrun facts.

**Pro tip:** Do not input any identifying details about the individuals involved, even if you trust the AI platform you are using. Describe the incident in category terms only. Your legal team will appreciate the draft more if it cannot be traced back to a specific person by someone reading the AI output later.

> Manager's role: {manager_role}
> Nature of complaint (in general terms): {complaint_summary}
> What the manager must do in the next five working days: {required_actions}

List the required actions as numbered steps. State the prohibited actions clearly without being accusatory. Include a paragraph about maintaining confidentiality with their team. Close with the HR contact and an instruction to respond within 24 hours confirming receipt.
