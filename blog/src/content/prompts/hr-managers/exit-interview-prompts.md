## Exit Interview Conversation Scripts

> You are an HR Manager preparing to run a 30-minute exit interview.
>
> Departing employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}, {department}
> Tenure: {tenure_in_years} years
> Reason given for leaving: {stated_reason: e.g. better pay, relocation, career change, unclear}
> Main sensitivity to manage: {sensitivity: e.g. team conflict, manager relationship, burnout, competitor move}
> Tone: {tone: neutral / empathetic / formal}
>
> Write a conversation script the HR Manager speaks aloud, from opening to close. Include a warm opening that normalises the conversation, five open questions that move from safe to specific, one probing follow-up per section, and a closing that thanks the employee and sets expectations for next steps. Format as a script with clear HR MANAGER and TRANSITION cues. 400 to 500 words.

**When to use it:** Monday morning before a Tuesday exit interview with a departing employee whose manager relationship may have soured — you want to approach it right the first time.

**Pro tip:** If the {sensitivity} involves a named manager, swap that manager's name into the probing follow-up question directly. Asking "how did the relationship with your team lead affect your decision?" surfaces more than "how was your management experience?"

> You are an HR Manager conducting an exit interview with a long-tenured senior employee.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Title: {job_title}
> Years at company: {tenure: must be 10 or more}
> Business unit: {business_unit}
> Departure type: {departure_type: voluntary retirement / voluntary resignation}
> Key knowledge risk: {knowledge_risk: e.g. client relationships, technical systems, regulatory history, supplier contacts}
>
> Write a structured 30-minute exit interview script focused on knowledge transfer and institutional memory. Open with acknowledgement of their tenure. Include three questions that surface undocumented knowledge, two questions about team readiness, and one direct question about what the organisation should protect after they leave. Close with a transition to a knowledge handover conversation scheduled for the following week. 450 to 550 words.

**When to use it:** When a 12-year director announces retirement with six weeks' notice and you need the conversation to do double duty — emotional offboarding and knowledge triage.

**Pro tip:** If the employee is retiring rather than resigning, frame the knowledge questions around legacy ("What would you want the next person in this role to know that they'd never find in a handbook?") — this lands far better than clinical "document your processes" language.

> You are an HR Manager running an exit interview with a junior employee who is leaving within their first year.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Months employed: {months_employed: must be under 12}
> Team: {team_name}
> Onboarding quality rating (internal): {onboarding_rating: poor / average / strong}
> Likely real reason for leaving: {likely_reason: e.g. role mismatch, manager style, compensation, culture fit}
>
> Write a 20-minute exit interview script for a short-tenure departure. Open by acknowledging the brief time without making the employee feel like a failure. Include two questions about their onboarding experience, two about their day-to-day reality vs. expectations, and one question about what would have made them stay. Close with an offer to serve as a reference and a note about boomerang rehire eligibility. Script format with HR MANAGER cues. 300 to 380 words.

**When to use it:** A new hire on the 10-month mark hands in notice and your instinct says the job was misrepresented at interview — this script gets you the data to take back to the hiring manager.

**Pro tip:** The "what would have made you stay" question is the highest-yield question in short-tenure exits. Put it last so trust is established first — if you open with it, you get a polished deflection.

> You are an HR Manager preparing for an exit interview where the employee was managed out after a performance improvement plan.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> PIP duration: {pip_duration: e.g. 60 days, 90 days}
> PIP outcome: {pip_outcome: did not meet targets}
> Legal risk level: {legal_risk: low / medium / high}
> Documentation status: {doc_status: fully documented / partially documented / minimal documentation}
>
> Write a 20-minute exit interview script for a managed-out employee. Tone is respectful and legally careful. Avoid admissions of fault or comparative statements about performance. Include two neutral open questions about their experience of support, one question about whether any concerns were left unaddressed, and a clear closing that covers final pay timeline, reference policy, and confidentiality reminder. Flag any language the HR Manager should avoid in brackets. 350 to 430 words.

**When to use it:** Friday afternoon before a Monday managed-exit conversation — when legal has flagged medium risk and you want the script reviewed before you walk in.

**Pro tip:** Set {legal_risk} to high and ChatGPT will tighten the language considerably. Cross-check any bracketed "avoid this" flags with your employment counsel before the conversation — AI flags patterns but doesn't know your jurisdiction's specific case law.

> You are an HR Manager facilitating an exit interview for an employee leaving because they were passed over for promotion.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Time in role: {time_in_role}
> Promotion decision date: {promotion_decision_date}
> Person promoted instead: {do_not_name_the_other_person: use "the selected candidate" throughout}
> Stated reason employee is leaving: {stated_reason}
>
> Write a 25-minute exit interview script that acknowledges the employee's disappointment without relitigating the promotion decision. Include two questions about their career development experience, one about whether feedback they received was actionable, and one question about what the company could do better for employees in similar situations. Close with a genuine acknowledgement of their contribution and information on alumni network access. 350 to 420 words. Avoid any language that implies the promotion decision was wrong.

**When to use it:** When a strong performer hands in notice two weeks after a promotion announcement — you want honest data about the process without creating legal exposure or false hope.

**Pro tip:** Never use the promoted employee's name in the script or in your notes. "The selected candidate" is a neutral term that keeps the conversation from becoming a comparison exercise.

## Post-Exit Interview Debrief Notes and Internal Summaries

> You are an HR Manager writing an internal debrief note after completing an exit interview.
>
> Departing employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}, {department}
> Interviewer: {hr_manager_name}
> Date of interview: {interview_date}
> Primary reason for departure: {primary_reason}
> Secondary themes raised: {secondary_themes: up to 3 bullet points of what was said}
> Recommended action: {recommended_action: e.g. manager coaching, compensation review, team culture audit, no action}
>
> Write a 250 to 300 word internal debrief note addressed to the HRBP or Head of HR. Open with a one-sentence summary of the departure. Summarise the key themes in two short paragraphs. Include a clear recommended next step with an owner and a 30-day timeline. Flag if the information is sensitive and should not be shared with the departing employee's direct manager. Formal tone.

**When to use it:** Immediately after the exit interview ends — before the next meeting pulls you away and the detail disappears.

**Pro tip:** If {recommended_action} involves manager coaching, name the specific manager in the note even if you use coded language ("the employee's line manager") in the body. The HRBP needs clarity on who the note is about when they read it three days later.

> You are an HR Manager writing a quarterly exit interview trends summary for the leadership team.
>
> Quarter: {quarter: e.g. Q2 2026}
> Total exits: {total_exits}
> Voluntary exits: {voluntary_exits}
> Top three stated reasons across all exit interviews: {top_three_reasons: in bullet format}
> Department with highest exit rate: {high_exit_department}
> Any repeated theme involving a specific manager: {manager_theme: yes / no — if yes, describe without naming}
> Tone: {tone: analytical / frank / diplomatic}
>
> Write a 400 to 500 word executive summary of exit interview trends for the quarter. Use a data-led opening that states voluntary exit rate. Cover the three themes in short paragraphs with one specific quote or paraphrase from exit interviews (anonymised). End with three concrete recommendations for leadership action, each with an owner function (e.g. Compensation, L&D, People Operations). No bullet overload — mostly prose.

**When to use it:** The week before a quarterly HR review meeting when the CHRO asks for a written summary by EOD Thursday.

**Pro tip:** If {manager_theme} is yes, write the recommendation in structural terms ("review span of control and feedback cadence in the [department] team") not personal terms. Leadership summaries that name managers without a performance process behind them create liability.

> You are an HR Manager writing a confidential note flagging an exit interview that raised a potential HR risk.
>
> Departing employee: {employee_name} (anonymise in the note)
> Risk type: {risk_type: harassment allegation / discrimination concern / wage dispute / safety violation / policy breach}
> What was said verbatim (paraphrased): {verbatim_paraphrase}
> Evidence mentioned by employee: {evidence_mentioned: e.g. emails, witnesses, none}
> Who the allegation involves: {allegation_involves: describe role, not name}
> Recipient of this note: {recipient: e.g. Head of HR, Legal, CEO}
>
> Write a 200 to 280 word confidential internal escalation note. Open with the date and a one-line classification (e.g. "Confidential — Potential Harassment Allegation"). Summarise what was disclosed without embellishment or interpretation. Note what evidence exists. State recommended immediate next steps (e.g. preserve communications, engage legal, begin investigation). Close with a line confirming this note is not shared with the subject of the allegation. Formal, factual tone. No conclusions about guilt.

**When to use it:** When an exit interview takes an unexpected turn and an employee discloses something that requires escalation before the end of the working day.

**Pro tip:** Do not paraphrase aggressively when filling in {verbatim_paraphrase}. The closer your input is to what was actually said, the more legally defensible the note. Review the output with your employment counsel before sending — AI will produce clean language but cannot assess jurisdictional disclosure obligations.

> You are an HR Manager writing a brief internal note summarising an exit interview for a departing employee's hiring manager.
>
> Departing employee: {employee_name}
> Hiring manager: {manager_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Primary reason for leaving: {primary_reason: must be shareable — not a grievance about this manager}
> Development feedback shared (anonymised): {development_feedback: one or two themes the employee raised about their own growth}
> Anything that should NOT be shared with this manager: {do_not_share: yes / no}
>
> Write a 150 to 200 word note from HR to the hiring manager. Keep it factual and forward-looking. Summarise the primary reason for departure in one sentence. Share one piece of development feedback that may help the manager support the next hire. Close with a note on backfill timeline or next steps HR is taking. If {do_not_share} is yes, omit any sensitive content and add a line that the full debrief is available from HR on request.

**When to use it:** After every voluntary exit interview — the manager deserves a summary and this keeps HR in the loop rather than the rumour mill.

**Pro tip:** Keep this note to facts the employee explicitly said were okay to share. If you didn't ask for permission in the exit interview, default to sharing only the stated reason and development themes — nothing that could identify a secondary grievance.

> You are an HR Manager drafting an anonymised case study from an exit interview to use in an internal talent retention workshop.
>
> Role type (not specific title): {role_type: e.g. mid-level individual contributor, senior people manager}
> Tenure at departure: {tenure_at_departure}
> Key theme from exit interview: {key_theme: e.g. lack of career progression, compensation gap, manager relationship, burnout}
> One direct quote (anonymised): {anonymised_quote}
> What the company could have done differently: {company_gap: one specific thing}
> Workshop audience: {workshop_audience: e.g. people managers, HRBPs, senior leaders}
>
> Write a 250 to 320 word anonymised case study in narrative form. Open with a two-sentence description of the employee (no identifiers). Walk through the key theme and why it went unaddressed. Include the anonymised quote. End with two reflection questions for the workshop audience. Tone is instructive, not blaming. Format: prose followed by two numbered reflection questions.

**When to use it:** When you're building a manager capability session and want a real story (anonymised) rather than a hypothetical that the audience dismisses as generic.

**Pro tip:** Change at least two contextual details (tenure range, department type) before using this in a workshop — even with anonymisation, colleagues sometimes recognise the situation. Ask the departing employee's permission if the case is distinctive.

## Exit Interview Follow-Up Emails

> You are an HR Manager writing a follow-up email to a departing employee after their exit interview.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Last working day: {last_working_day}
> Key themes acknowledged in the interview: {acknowledged_themes: two or three brief points}
> Next step HR is taking based on feedback: {hr_next_step: be specific, e.g. reviewing compensation bands, speaking with team lead}
> Rehire eligibility: {rehire_eligible: yes / no / conditional}
>
> Write a 180 to 220 word follow-up email from HR to the departing employee. Open by thanking them for their candour. Confirm one or two themes you heard and what HR intends to do with that feedback (without over-promising). Provide their last-day checklist items (equipment return, system access, final pay date). Close with a warm line about staying connected and, if eligible, note their rehire status positively. Professional but human tone.

**When to use it:** Same day as the exit interview — send it before 5pm so the employee feels heard while the conversation is still fresh.

**Pro tip:** The {hr_next_step} line is the most important sentence in the email. If you write something vague like "we will consider your feedback," the employee reads it as dismissal. Name the specific action, even if it's small. It changes the tone of the entire email.
