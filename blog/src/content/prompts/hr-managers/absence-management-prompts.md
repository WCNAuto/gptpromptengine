## Return-to-Work Letters and Meeting Scripts

> You are an HR manager writing a return-to-work letter following a period of absence.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Job title: {job_title}
> Absence dates: {absence_start_date} to {absence_return_date}
> Absence reason (as declared): {absence_reason}
> Number of absence days: {total_days_absent}
> Any phased return or adjustments agreed: {phased_return_details — or write "none agreed"}
> Tone: {formal / supportive / neutral}
>
> Write a 250 to 300 word return-to-work letter. Open by welcoming the employee back and confirming the dates of absence. In the body, confirm any agreed adjustments or state that standard duties resume immediately. Note that a return-to-work meeting will be held within 48 hours and briefly explain its purpose (not disciplinary, a welfare check). Close with a clear next step the employee should take on their first day. Do not use legal jargon. Output a finished letter ready to print or email.

**When to use it:** Monday morning when an employee is back after a week off and you need the letter on their desk before the 9am return-to-work meeting.

**Pro tip:** If {phased_return_details} involves reduced hours, name the exact schedule in the variable (e.g. "4 hours per day, 9am to 1pm, for two weeks") rather than leaving it vague. ChatGPT will echo that specificity into the letter and save you one revision cycle.

> You are an HR manager preparing a return-to-work meeting script for a line manager to follow.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Line manager name: {manager_name}
> Duration of absence: {total_days_absent} days
> Absence pattern concern: {yes — this is the third absence in 90 days / no — isolated absence}
> Medical documentation received: {yes / no / fit note provided}
> Tone: {coaching / neutral / structured}
>
> Write a 400 to 500 word script the line manager reads during the return-to-work meeting. Structure it in three parts: (1) a warm opening that does not feel interrogative, (2) five specific questions to ask the employee covering wellbeing, any workplace factors, support needed, and whether medical advice has been followed, (3) a closing that confirms what happens next and reminds the employee of EAP or occupational health access. Flag in brackets anywhere the manager should pause and listen rather than speak. Output a finished script the manager can hold in their hand.

**When to use it:** The night before a line manager runs a return-to-work meeting for the first time and calls you asking what to say.

**Pro tip:** Set {absence_pattern_concern} to "yes" only if you have the Bradford Factor or trigger data to back it up. If you trigger that branch without data, the script will sound accusatory and the manager won't know how to de-escalate.

> You are an HR manager writing a letter confirming the outcome of a return-to-work meeting.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Meeting date: {meeting_date}
> Key points discussed: {two_to_four_bullet_points_of_discussion}
> Support agreed: {support_agreed — e.g. referral to occupational health, flexible start time, none}
> Next review date: {next_review_date}
> Tone: {supportive / formal}
>
> Write a 200 to 250 word letter confirming the meeting took place, summarising what was discussed, and documenting any support measures agreed. This letter is for the employee's personnel file and as a written record sent to the employee. It must not read as a warning or imply fault. Close by confirming the next review date and who the employee should contact if their situation changes. Output a finished letter.

**When to use it:** Immediately after a return-to-work meeting, before the end of the working day, to create a contemporaneous record.

**Pro tip:** Keep {two_to_four_bullet_points_of_discussion} factual and brief. If you paste in full conversation notes, the AI will summarise them well, but watch for it softening anything the employee said about a workplace grievance — review that section before sending.

> You are an HR manager writing a short email to an employee's line manager summarising the return-to-work outcome and the adjustments HR has authorised.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Line manager name: {manager_name}
> Adjustments authorised: {list_of_adjustments}
> Duration of adjustments: {adjustment_duration}
> Absence pattern flag: {yes — Bradford trigger reached / no}
> Action required from manager: {specific_action_required}
>
> Write a 150 to 200 word internal email from HR to the line manager. Be direct. State what adjustments are in place, how long they run, what the manager must do (e.g. log hours, check in weekly, refer to OHR by a specific date), and one sentence on what to do if the employee's attendance worsens. Do not repeat the full absence history — the manager was in the meeting. Output a finished email ready to send.

**When to use it:** Right after the return-to-work meeting closes, before the manager goes back to the floor and forgets what was agreed.

**Pro tip:** The {specific_action_required} variable is where most HR managers write something vague like "monitor attendance." Instead write "log hours on the rota system by Friday and email HR if the employee leaves early more than twice this week." The AI will build that specificity into the call to action.

> You are an HR manager writing a letter inviting an employee to a welfare meeting following a pattern of short-term absences.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Number of absences in the trigger period: {number_of_absences}
> Trigger period: {trigger_period — e.g. "the last 12 weeks"}
> Meeting date and time: {meeting_date_and_time}
> Meeting location: {meeting_location — office address or video call link}
> Right to be accompanied: {yes — they may bring a trade union rep or colleague / no — informal welfare meeting only}
>
> Write a 250 to 300 word letter inviting the employee to the meeting. Clarify that this is a welfare conversation, not a disciplinary hearing. Name the absence pattern without overstating it. State the date, time, and location clearly. If {right_to_be_accompanied} is yes, explain the right in one sentence. Close by telling the employee who to contact if they cannot attend. Tone: professional but not threatening. Output a finished letter.

**When to use it:** When the Bradford Factor trigger fires and you need to open a formal welfare conversation without spooking the employee into a grievance.

**Pro tip:** Do not include the Bradford Factor score in the letter itself — it can read as punitive and invite challenge. Include it in your internal notes variable instead and let the letter reference "a pattern of short-term absences" only.

## Long-Term Absence: Medical, Fit Note, and Occupational Health Letters

> You are an HR manager writing a letter to an employee who has been absent for more than four consecutive weeks.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Job title: {job_title}
> Absence start date: {absence_start_date}
> Fit note status: {fit_note_received_and_valid / fit_note_overdue / no_fit_note_received}
> Occupational health referral: {already_made / to_be_made / not_applicable}
> Tone: {compassionate / formal}
>
> Write a 300 to 350 word letter. Acknowledge the employee's continued absence with care. Confirm the current fit note status. If a referral to occupational health has been made or is being made, explain the purpose (to support the employee's return, not to investigate). Ask the employee to maintain contact with their named HR contact fortnightly. Close by confirming what happens next and when HR will be in touch again. Do not threaten capability proceedings in this letter. Output a finished letter.

**When to use it:** Week five of an employee's continuous absence when the manager has been calling you every other day asking what to do next.

**Pro tip:** If {fit_note_status} is "overdue," the AI will often phrase the chase in language that sounds accusatory. Add the instruction "do not imply the employee is at fault for the missing fit note" to the variable field and it will soften that section appropriately.

> You are an HR manager writing a letter to accompany an occupational health referral.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Job title: {job_title}
> Reason for referral: {brief_medical_or_absence_context — two to three sentences, anonymised where needed}
> Questions you need occupational health to answer: {three_to_five_specific_questions}
> Deadline for report: {report_deadline_date}
> Consent confirmation: {employee_has_signed_consent / consent_to_be_obtained_at_appointment}
>
> Write a 300 to 400 word referral letter to the occupational health provider. Open with the business context and why the referral has been made. Include the three to five questions exactly as provided, numbered. Confirm consent status. State the deadline for the report. Close by naming the HR contact for follow-up. Tone: clinical and factual. No narrative padding. Output a finished letter ready to send to the OHR provider.

**When to use it:** When you're preparing the OHR pack and the manager has sent you a rambling email with no clear questions — use this to turn their email into a focused referral.

**Pro tip:** The questions you put in {three_to_five_specific_questions} are what determine the usefulness of the OHR report. Prompts like "is the employee fit for work?" produce weak reports. Use prompts like "what adjustments, if any, would enable this employee to perform their core duties within the next eight weeks?" — write those into the variable before running the prompt.

> You are an HR manager writing a letter to an employee following receipt of an occupational health report.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> OHR report date: {report_date}
> Key findings summary: {two_to_four_key_findings_from_report}
> Adjustments recommended by OHR: {recommended_adjustments — or "none recommended"}
> Next steps HR is taking: {next_steps}
> Meeting to discuss: {yes — meeting date is {meeting_date} / no — letter is standalone}
>
> Write a 300 to 350 word letter to the employee summarising the report's findings, stating what adjustments HR will implement or is considering, and explaining the next steps. If a meeting is scheduled, name the date and tell the employee they may bring a companion. Do not quote the OHR report verbatim — paraphrase findings in plain English. Tone: transparent and supportive. Output a finished letter.

**When to use it:** When the OHR report arrives and the employee hasn't seen it yet — this letter frames the findings before the conversation, reducing the chance of a reactive response.

**Pro tip:** Never paste protected health data directly into a public AI tool. Summarise findings in {two_to_four_key_findings_from_report} using generic clinical language (e.g. "the report identifies a musculoskeletal condition affecting standing for prolonged periods") before running this prompt.

> You are an HR manager writing a letter inviting an employee to a long-term absence review meeting where capability may be discussed.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Length of absence to date: {absence_duration}
> Number of OHR referrals completed: {ohr_referral_count}
> Reason capability is now being considered: {brief_rationale — two sentences}
> Meeting date and time: {meeting_date_and_time}
> Right to be accompanied: yes — trade union representative or colleague
>
> Write a 350 to 400 word formal letter. Open by acknowledging the employee's situation with care. State that the meeting will review whether a return to work is likely within a reasonable timeframe and what options exist. Options should include: phased return, redeployment, ill-health retirement if applicable, and, as a last resort, termination on capability grounds. Explain the right to be accompanied. Tell the employee they may submit a written statement before the meeting. Tone: formal, fair, humane. Output a finished letter.

**When to use it:** After six to nine months of long-term absence when previous support has been exhausted and the business needs to formally review viability.

**Pro tip:** In {brief_rationale}, cite the specific support already offered (number of OHR reports, adjustments trialled, timeframes discussed) before referencing capability. If you don't, the letter will read like the company moved straight to capability without a fair process, which is exactly what an employment tribunal will look for.

> You are an HR manager writing an internal briefing note to a senior manager explaining the current situation with a long-term absent employee.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Absence duration: {absence_duration}
> Summary of support provided to date: {three_to_five_bullet_points}
> Current OHR position: {ohr_position_summary}
> Risk to the business if absence continues: {business_risk_description}
> Recommended next step: {recommended_action}
>
> Write a 300 to 350 word internal briefing note in headed paragraph format (not bullet points throughout). Open with the current situation in two sentences. Summarise support provided. State the OHR position. Describe the business risk plainly without dramatising it. End with a clear recommended next step and what sign-off is needed from the senior manager. Tone: objective and factual. Output a finished briefing note.

**When to use it:** Before a meeting with a senior leader or legal team to ensure everyone has the same picture before a capability or termination decision is made.

**Pro tip:** Write {business_risk_description} in operational terms, not emotional ones. "The role has been covered by agency staff at £X per week for Y weeks, and key client deliverables have slipped" will land better with a senior manager than "the team is struggling."

## Formal Absence Warning Letters

> You are an HR manager writing a first written warning letter for unacceptable attendance.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Job title: {job_title}
> Absence record summary: {number_of_absences} absences totalling {total_days_absent} days over {review_period}
> Outcome of absence review hearing: {outcome_summary — two sentences}
> Improvement required: {specific_attendance_improvement_required}
> Review period going forward: {review_period_duration}
> Right to appeal: yes — within {appeal_deadline} working days to {appeal_contact}
>
> Write a 400 to 450 word first written warning letter. Open with the hearing date and who attended. State the absence record clearly in one short paragraph. Confirm the outcome (first written warning). State exactly what improvement is required during the review period and what will happen if the improvement is not achieved. End with the appeal right, deadline, and contact. Tone: formal, factual, fair. Do not use threatening language. Output a finished letter.
