## Annual Performance Reviews

> You are an HR manager writing a year-end performance review for a high performer being considered for promotion.
> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}, {tenure} at the company
> Rating: {exceeds_expectations / meets_expectations / below_expectations}
> Key wins this year: {three_major_accomplishments}
> Growth areas: {two_development_opportunities}
> Promotion readiness: {ready / needs_6_months / needs_12_months}
> Manager tone: {supportive / direct / formal}
> 
> Write a 400-500 word performance review using the SBI model (Situation, Behaviour, Impact). Open with specific recognition of their strongest win. Address growth areas as development opportunities, not shortcomings. Close with clear promotion timeline and next steps.

**When to use it:** When you're reviewing a top performer and need to balance praise with honest development feedback for promotion consideration.

**Pro tip:** If promotion isn't happening this cycle, spend extra words on the specific skills or experiences they need to develop, not vague "leadership potential" language.

> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Position: {job_title}
> Tenure: {months_in_role}
> Strong start period: {first_quarter_wins}
> Performance decline started: {month_when_issues_began}
> Current challenges: {specific_performance_gaps}
> Personal factors (if known): {family_issues / health / workload / team_changes}
> Improvement timeline: {30_days / 60_days / 90_days}
> 
> Write a 350-400 word review that acknowledges their early success, addresses the decline without shame, and creates a clear improvement plan. Use a constructive tone that assumes they can turn it around.

**When to use it:** When an employee's performance has visibly dropped and you need documentation while keeping the door open for recovery.

**Pro tip:** Reference specific dates and examples from their strong period to show this isn't about their capability, it's about current circumstances.

## Difficult Conversations

> You need to document a formal warning for attendance issues.
> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Position: {job_title}
> Attendance pattern: {specific_dates_and_absences}
> Previous verbal warnings: {dates_of_prior_conversations}
> Impact on team: {specific_coverage_issues_or_workload_impacts}
> Employee's stated reasons: {their_explanation_for_absences}
> Accommodation requests: {any_formal_requests_made}
> Improvement timeline: {30_days / 60_days / 90_days}
> 
> Write a formal 250-300 word written warning that documents the pattern, explains business impact, and sets clear expectations. Include specific consequences if attendance doesn't improve. Professional tone, no emotional language.

**When to use it:** When verbal coaching hasn't worked and you need to create formal documentation before escalating further.

**Pro tip:** Always include the specific business impact of their absences, not just policy violations. It strengthens your documentation if this leads to termination.

> You are preparing talking points for a difficult mid-year conversation about performance decline.
> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Performance decline areas: {specific_metrics_or_examples}
> Timeline of decline: {when_issues_started}
> Previous support offered: {training_coaching_or_resources_provided}
> Current impact: {how_its_affecting_team_or_projects}
> Employee's awareness level: {know_theres_a_problem / seem_surprised / in_denial}
> Your relationship: {strong / neutral / strained}
> 
> Create a conversation script with 5-7 talking points. Start with care, present facts without emotion, ask for their perspective, then collaborate on solutions. Include specific questions to ask them and specific support to offer.

**When to use it:** When you need structure for a performance conversation that could go sideways without careful handling.

**Pro tip:** Write down the exact questions you'll ask them about their perspective. When these conversations get tense, managers often forget to listen.

> You need to address a complaint about an employee's attitude affecting team morale.
> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Specific behaviors reported: {what_teammates_have_said}
> Frequency: {daily / weekly / during_certain_projects}
> Team members affected: {number_of_people_or_specific_roles}
> Work quality: {still_good / declining / unaffected}
> Previous feedback given: {none / informal / formal}
> Employee's likely reaction: {defensive / surprised / aware}
> 
> Write a 200-250 word conversation opener that addresses behavior impact without attacking character. Focus on specific examples and business impact. Include questions that help them understand the team perspective.

**When to use it:** When multiple team members have complained about someone's attitude but you need to address it without revealing who spoke up.

**Pro tip:** Never say "people are saying" or "the team feels." Use your own observations and the business impact you've witnessed directly.

> You are documenting a performance improvement plan for an employee at risk of termination.
> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Performance gaps: {specific_measurable_deficiencies}
> Business impact: {how_gaps_affect_results_or_team}
> Previous interventions: {coaching_training_or_support_already_tried}
> Improvement timeline: {30_days / 60_days / 90_days}
> Success metrics: {specific_measurable_improvements_needed}
> Support being provided: {additional_training_coaching_or_resources}
> Review schedule: {weekly / biweekly / monthly_check_ins}
> 
> Write a formal 400-500 word Performance Improvement Plan that clearly defines expectations, support, timeline, and consequences. Include specific metrics and review dates. Professional tone that assumes they can succeed but documents the stakes.

**When to use it:** When informal coaching hasn't worked and you need formal documentation before considering termination.

**Pro tip:** Make the success metrics so specific that both you and the employee will know exactly whether they've met them. Vague goals create legal problems later.

> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Incident details: {what_was_said_or_done}
> Setting: {meeting / email / casual_conversation / public}
> People present: {who_witnessed_or_was_affected}

> Company policy: {specific_policy_they_violated}
> Previous incidents: {none / similar_issues_before / first_time}
> 
> Write a 300-350 word coaching conversation that addresses the incident clearly, explains why it's problematic, and prevents future issues. Assume good intent but be firm about standards. Include specific language about what's acceptable going forward.

**Pro tip:** Don't get drawn into debating their intent or whether others were "really" offended. Focus on professional standards and business impact.

## Feedback and Recognition

> You are writing an email to recognize an employee who went above and beyond during a crisis.
> 
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Crisis situation: {specific_emergency_or_challenge}
> Their specific actions: {what_they_did_beyond_normal_duties}
> Impact: {how_their_actions_helped_company_or_team}
> Personal cost: {extra_hours_stress_or_sacrifice_they_made}
> Team members who benefited: {who_else_was_helped}
> Recognition type: {email_only / copying_leadership / public_announcement}
> 
> Write a 150-200 word recognition email that specifically describes their actions and impact. Make it personal and genuine, not generic praise. Include how their example sets the standard for others.

**When to use it:** Right after a crisis or busy period when someone stepped up significantly and you want to acknowledge it while it's fresh.

**Pro tip:** Send this within 48 hours of the action you're recognizing. Late recognition feels like an afterthought, even if you mean it sincerely.

**When to use it:** After an employee has a visible win with senior leadership and you want to reinforce the behaviors that led to success.

**Pro tip:** Be specific about which parts of their presentation worked best. They need to know what to repeat in future high-stakes situations.

**When to use it:** When someone tried hard but the result wasn't what you needed, and you want to maintain their confidence while improving their approach.

**Pro tip:** Lead with effort recognition before addressing the gaps. People who tried hard need to know you saw that before they can hear criticism.

**Pro tip:** Give them specific roles in upcoming meetings, like "I'd like you to share the data findings" or "Can you speak to the technical feasibility?" It's easier than general encouragement to "speak up more."
