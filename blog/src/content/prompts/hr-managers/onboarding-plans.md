## First Day Welcome Communications

> You are writing a welcome email for a new hire's first day at work.
> 
> New hire: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Department: {department_name}
> Start date: {start_date}
> Manager: {manager_name}
> First day schedule: {morning_activity}, {afternoon_activity}
> Company culture note: {one_thing_that_makes_company_special}
> Dress code: {casual / business_casual / formal}
> 
> Write a 250 to 350 word welcome email that covers arrival time, parking instructions, what to bring, and sets an encouraging tone. Include one specific detail about their role that shows you're prepared for their arrival. End with your direct contact information and an invitation to reach out with questions.

**When to use it:** Sunday evening before a Monday start, or the day before any new hire begins.

**Pro tip:** Reference something specific from their interview to make the welcome feel personal, not templated.

**Pro tip:** Practice the introduction out loud once – reading it cold makes it sound robotic.

## Department-Specific Training Plans

> You are writing an onboarding plan for a new sales team member.
> 
> New hire: {employee_name}
> Sales territory: {geographic_or_account_region}
> Quota start date: {when_they_begin_selling}
> CRM system: {salesforce_hubspot_or_other}
> Main product lines: {product_1}, {product_2}, {product_3}
> Sales manager: {manager_name}
> Top competitor: {main_competitor_name}
> 
> Create a 3-week training plan with specific daily activities. Week 1: product knowledge and CRM setup. Week 2: shadowing calls and competitor analysis. Week 3: first solo prospects with support. Include measurable checkpoints and who delivers each training component. Format as a detailed schedule with accountability measures.

**When to use it:** Week before a sales hire starts, so you can coordinate with product teams and senior reps.

**Pro tip:** Have them lose a practice pitch badly in week 2 – failing safely builds resilience for real rejections.

## Manager Relationship Building

> You are writing a new hire's first one-on-one agenda with their direct manager.
> 
> New hire: {employee_name}
> Manager: {manager_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Days since start date: {number_of_days}
> Initial observations: {what_seems_to_be_going_well}
> Potential concerns: {any_early_challenges_noticed}
> Communication preference: {in_person_video_call_or_phone}
> 
> Create a 45-minute one-on-one agenda focused on relationship building and early feedback. Include questions about their experience so far, clarification on role expectations, and goal-setting for the next 30 days. Structure with specific time allocations and conversation starters that encourage honest dialogue.

**When to use it:** End of first week, to catch issues early before they become problems.

**Pro tip:** Ask "What's one thing that surprised you so far?" – their answer reveals gaps between expectation and reality.

> Write a structured conversation guide for a 30-day performance check-in. Include specific questions about goal progress, challenges faced, and support needed. Balance praise with constructive feedback and end with adjusted goals for the next 30 days. Format as talking points with suggested phrasing for difficult conversations.

**When to use it:** Day 28-30, to formally assess early performance and make course corrections.

**Pro tip:** Start with what they've done well – new hires are already anxious about performance feedback.

> You are writing a new hire feedback request email to their temporary buddy or mentor.
> 
> Buddy/Mentor: {mentor_name}
> New hire: {employee_name}
> Mentoring duration: {number_of_weeks}
> New hire's role: {job_title}
> Key areas to assess: {technical_skills}, {cultural_fit}, {communication_style}
> Feedback deadline: {specific_date}
> Manager requesting: {your_name}
> 
> Write a 200 to 300 word email requesting structured feedback from their assigned buddy or mentor. Include specific questions about integration, skill demonstration, and any concerns. Make it easy to respond with clear categories and space for both positive observations and improvement areas.

**When to use it:** Week 3-4 of onboarding, when patterns are clear but adjustment is still possible.

**Pro tip:** Ask for examples, not just ratings – "communicates well" is useless, "explains complex ideas clearly" is actionable.
