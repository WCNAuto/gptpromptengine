## Pre-Survey Communications

> You are writing an email to announce an upcoming employee engagement survey to all staff.
> 
> Company: {company_name}
> Survey duration: {start_date} to {end_date}
> Previous survey completion rate: {percentage}
> Key concern from last survey: {main_issue_addressed}
> Survey tool: {platform_name}
> CEO/leadership sponsor: {sponsor_name}
> Time to complete: {minutes}
> Anonymity level: {fully_anonymous / confidential_but_tracked / identified}
> 
> Write a 250 to 350 word announcement email that builds excitement, addresses survey fatigue, and emphasizes how previous feedback led to real changes. Open with the specific change made from last year's survey. Include a clear call-to-action with dates.

**When to use it:** Two weeks before survey launch when you need to build momentum and address skepticism about whether leadership actually listens.

**Pro tip:** Reference one concrete change from your last survey in the opening line—it immediately proves surveys drive action.

> 
> Days remaining: {number_days}
> Current completion rate: {percentage}
> Target completion rate: {target_percentage}
> Department with highest completion: {top_department}
> Survey topic getting most comments: {hot_topic}
> Incentive offered: {incentive_or_none}
> Manager name sending reminder: {manager_name}

> Write a 150 to 200 word reminder email that creates urgency without being pushy. Mention the positive response so far and the department leading participation. Keep the tone encouraging, not guilt-inducing.

**When to use it:** Midway through your survey period when completion rates are stalling and you need to re-energize participation.

**Pro tip:** Lead with departments that are participating well rather than shaming the laggards—positive peer pressure works better than guilt.

## Survey Question Development

> You are writing engagement survey questions focused on manager effectiveness for a company experiencing high turnover in mid-level roles.
> 
> Industry: {industry_type}
> Average tenure of managers: {years_experience}
> Main turnover trigger identified: {exit_interview_theme}
> Company size: {number_employees}
> Management training provided: {training_type_or_none}
> Performance review frequency: {review_schedule}
> Recent management change: {recent_leadership_change}
> Rating scale preference: {likert_scale / numeric / yes_no}
> 
> Write 8 to 12 survey questions that diagnose specific manager behaviors driving turnover. Include 3 questions about communication, 3 about development/growth, 3 about decision-making, and 2 open-ended questions. Use clear, behavioral language employees can easily answer.

**When to use it:** When exit interviews point to manager issues but you need specific data about which management behaviors are the biggest problems.

**Pro tip:** Ask about specific behaviors ("My manager gives me feedback within 48 hours of important meetings") rather than abstractions ("My manager communicates well").

> You are writing survey questions about workload and burnout for a remote-first company where productivity is high but engagement scores are dropping.
> 
> Remote work policy: {policy_type}
> Average hours worked per week: {hours}
> Busiest season: {time_period}
> Collaboration tools used: {tools_list}
> Flexible schedule policy: {flexibility_level}
> Mental health benefits offered: {benefits_available}
> Team size average: {team_size}
> Time zone spread: {timezone_range}
> 
> Write 10 to 15 survey questions that uncover the relationship between remote work, workload, and burnout. Include questions about boundaries, meeting fatigue, isolation, and workload distribution. Mix rating scales with open-ended questions for deeper insight.

**When to use it:** When remote work is working operationally but you're seeing warning signs in engagement data and need to diagnose the specific stressors.

**Pro tip:** Ask about meeting frequency and after-hours communication separately—remote burnout often comes from always-on culture, not just work volume.

> You are writing survey questions about career development for a company where high performers are leaving for external opportunities.
> 
> Average time to promotion: {months_years}
> Career ladder clarity: {clear / somewhat_clear / unclear}
> Mentorship program: {formal / informal / none}
> Learning budget per employee: {dollar_amount}
> Internal fill rate for senior roles: {percentage}
> Performance review focus: {performance_only / includes_development / development_focused}
> Skills gap in industry: {specific_skills_needed}
> Competitor advantage in development: {what_competitors_offer}
> 
> Write 8 to 10 survey questions that identify specific gaps in career development. Focus on promotion transparency, skill building opportunities, mentorship quality, and future role clarity. Include questions that compare internal vs external growth perceptions.

**When to use it:** When exit interviews reveal people are leaving for better growth opportunities and you need to pinpoint exactly what's missing in your development approach.

**Pro tip:** Ask "How clearly can you describe your next role at this company?" rather than "Are you satisfied with career development?"—clarity predicts retention better than satisfaction.

> You are writing survey questions about recognition and rewards for a company where compensation is competitive but employees report feeling undervalued.
> 
> Compensation philosophy: {market_based / performance_based / equity_focused}
> Recognition program type: {peer_nomination / manager_driven / points_based / informal}
> Bonus structure: {annual / quarterly / project_based / none}
> Public recognition frequency: {weekly / monthly / quarterly / rare}
> Performance rating distribution: {forced_ranking / normal_distribution / no_ratings}
> Team celebration budget: {budget_amount_or_none}
> Non-monetary perks: {perks_list}
> Industry recognition norms: {industry_standards}
> 
> Write 10 to 12 survey questions that separate compensation satisfaction from recognition and appreciation. Include questions about timing of recognition, public vs private acknowledgment, peer recognition, and non-monetary appreciation. Focus on emotional rewards, not just financial ones.

**When to use it:** When pay isn't the issue but people still don't feel valued, and you need to diagnose what types of recognition would actually motivate your workforce.

**Pro tip:** Ask about recognition timing specifically—employees often care more about when they're recognized than how much they're rewarded.

**When to use it:** When rapid hiring is diluting company culture and you need to measure how well cultural elements are surviving growth and scaling.

**Pro tip:** Ask long-tenure employees to compare culture now vs. two years ago—they'll identify cultural drift that new employees can't detect.

## Post-Survey Analysis and Reporting

> You are writing an executive summary of engagement survey results for senior leadership who need to make budget and strategy decisions.
> 
> Overall engagement score: {percentage}
> Participation rate: {percentage}
> Year-over-year change: {up_down_percentage}
> Highest scoring area: {strength_area}
> Lowest scoring area: {problem_area}
> Department with biggest concern: {department_name}
> Top driver of engagement: {key_factor}
> Budget implication: {cost_to_address}
> Competitor benchmark: {comparison_data}
> 
> Write a 400 to 600 word executive summary that presents data, identifies root causes, and recommends 3 specific actions with resource requirements. Lead with business impact, not just scores. Include timeline and success metrics for recommendations.

**When to use it:** When presenting survey results to leadership who care more about business implications than detailed data analysis.

**Pro tip:** Lead with the business risk or opportunity, not the engagement score—executives need to understand what happens if they don't act.

> Write a 300 to 450 word all-company email that shares results honestly, celebrates what's working, acknowledges what needs improvement, and outlines next steps. Balance transparency with hope. End with specific timeline commitments.

**When to use it:** Four to six weeks after survey close when you've analyzed results and need to maintain trust by sharing findings transparently.

**Pro tip:** Share one surprising positive finding alongside the challenges—it shows you're reading all the feedback, not just focusing on problems.
