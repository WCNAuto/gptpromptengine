## Peer-Nominated and Spot Recognition Messages

> You are an HR Manager writing a public employee recognition message.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Nominated by: {nominator_name}, {nominator_relationship_to_employee: peer / manager / direct_report}
> What they did: {specific_action_or_behaviour_in_one_to_two_sentences}
> Impact: {outcome_or_result_of_the_action}
> Where this will appear: {Slack_channel / company_intranet / team_meeting / email_announcement}
>
> Write a 120 to 160 word recognition message that opens by naming the employee and the specific behaviour, not a generic opener like "we are pleased to recognise". Use plain, warm language. Name the nominator in the second sentence. Close with one sentence that connects the behaviour to a team or company value. Do not use hashtags, bullet points, or emojis.

**When to use it:** You have a peer nomination sitting in your inbox on a Friday afternoon and the employee's manager is already asking when it will go out.

**Pro tip:** If the nominator wrote their submission in vague terms ("she's always so helpful"), ask ChatGPT to flag where it had to infer specifics so you can fill them in before publishing. Add: "Mark any sentence where you assumed a detail with [ASSUMED]" to the prompt.

> You are an HR Manager writing a spontaneous spot-recognition message for a Slack channel.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Team: {team_name}
> What happened: {one_specific_thing_the_employee_did_this_week}
> Who was affected: {client_name_or_internal_team_affected}
> Tone: {casual_and_warm / professional / enthusiastic}
> Character limit: {character_limit: e.g. 280 / 500 / no_limit}
>
> Write a single Slack-ready recognition post within the character limit. Open with the employee's name in bold. Name the specific action in the first sentence. Do not use the phrase "shoutout to" or "kudos to" — these are overused. Close with one sentence inviting the team to add a reaction or reply. No bullet points.

**When to use it:** You saw something great happen in a meeting or in another channel and want to amplify it before the moment passes.

**Pro tip:** The character limit variable prevents ChatGPT from writing a 400-word wall of text that no one reads in Slack. Set it to 280 for channels with high message volume.

> You are an HR Manager writing a private recognition message from a senior leader to a junior employee.
>
> Senior leader: {leader_name}, {leader_title}
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Employee tenure: {tenure: e.g. 6 months / 2 years}
> What the employee did: {specific_contribution}
> Why it mattered at the leadership level: {strategic_or_business_impact}
> Preferred tone from the leader: {warm_and_personal / formal / conversational}
>
> Write a 150 to 200 word direct message the senior leader can send by email or Teams. Write it in first person from the leader's perspective. Open with the employee's name, not "I wanted to reach out". Name the specific contribution in the first two sentences. Explain why it mattered from a leadership vantage point without using jargon. Close with a genuine forward-looking sentence, not a generic "keep it up".

**When to use it:** A VP or C-suite leader asks you to draft a message to an employee they noticed but don't manage directly — they need it to sound like them, not like HR.

**Pro tip:** Add one sentence to the prompt describing the leader's usual communication style if you have examples: "This leader typically writes short sentences and never uses exclamation marks." It stops ChatGPT defaulting to corporate cheerfulness.

> You are an HR Manager writing a team-wide recognition message after a high-pressure project delivery.
>
> Team name: {team_name}
> Project or event: {project_name_or_description}
> Delivery date or period: {delivery_date_or_timeframe}
> Key challenge the team overcame: {main_obstacle_or_difficulty}
> Notable individual contributors (optional): {name_1}, {name_2}, {name_3}
> Audience: {whole_company / department / team_only}
> Format: {email / Slack / intranet_post}
>
> Write a 200 to 250 word recognition message addressed to the team collectively. Open by naming the project and the challenge, not with "I am proud to announce". Call out the named individuals in the middle paragraph with one specific action each. Close with a sentence that acknowledges the cost of the effort — late nights, tradeoffs, pressure — before a forward-looking close. Tone: genuine, not celebratory to the point of ignoring the difficulty.

**When to use it:** A major product launch, system migration, or client crisis just wrapped and the team needs acknowledgement before Monday's retrospective.

**Pro tip:** The line about "acknowledging the cost of effort" prevents the message from feeling tone-deaf when the team is exhausted. If the project had no major difficulty, remove that variable and replace it with {what_made_this_project_unique}.

> You are an HR Manager writing a recognition message for an employee who received a formal peer award nomination but did not win the award.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Award name: {award_name}
> Who nominated them: {nominator_name}
> What they were nominated for: {nomination_reason_in_one_to_two_sentences}
> Number of nominees total: {total_nominees}
> Tone: {warm / matter-of-fact / encouraging}
>
> Write a 130 to 170 word private message from HR that tells the employee they were nominated (not that they lost), describes what their nominator said about them, and closes with a specific reason this behaviour will be remembered or acted on. Do not use consolation language like "although you didn't win" or "we hope this softens the disappointment". Do not mention who won the award.

**When to use it:** Award results are out and you have a list of non-winners who still deserve acknowledgement before they hear the winner announced in the all-hands.

**Pro tip:** The instruction to avoid consolation language is critical here. ChatGPT defaults to it. If you see any phrase like "while the outcome wasn't what we hoped", regenerate with: "Rewrite this removing any language that implies a loss."

## Anniversary and Milestone Recognition Messages

> You are an HR Manager writing a work anniversary recognition message.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Anniversary year: {years: e.g. 1 / 5 / 10 / 20}
> Role: {current_job_title}
> One or two things that define this employee's contribution: {defining_contributions_in_bullet_form}
> How the company has changed during their tenure: {one_specific_organisational_change}
> Recipient of the message: {employee_only / team_and_employee / whole_company}
> Length: {short_100_words / medium_200_words / long_300_words}
>
> Write the anniversary message at the specified length. Open with the employee's name and the anniversary milestone in the first sentence — not "on this special day". Connect one defining contribution to a specific period in the company's history using the context provided. Close with one sentence about the future that is specific to their role, not generic praise. No poem. No numbered list. No emojis.

**When to use it:** Your HRIS anniversary alert fires Monday morning and you have a 10-year milestone that needs a real message, not the default platform template.

**Pro tip:** For 1-year anniversaries, the tone should lean toward "you made it through a hard first year and here's what we noticed" rather than nostalgic. Add "This is a 1-year milestone, so do not write in a retrospective or sentimental tone" if the output feels overwrought.

> You are an HR Manager writing a retirement recognition message on behalf of the company.
>
> Retiree: {employee_name}
> Years of service: {total_years}
> Roles held (most recent two): {role_1}, {role_2}
> One story or moment that defines their career here: {specific_anecdote_or_achievement}
> Who is sending this: {CEO / HR_Director / direct_manager}
> Where it will appear: {retirement_event_speech / email_to_all_staff / card}
> Tone: {warm_and_personal / formal_and_respectful / light_and_celebratory}
>
> Write a 300 to 400 word retirement recognition piece in first person from the sender's perspective. Open with the years of service and one line that captures what kind of professional this person was — specific, not generic. Use the anecdote in the second paragraph. Close with a genuine wish for the next chapter that references something personal about them if available. Do not use the phrase "leaving a lasting legacy" or "end of an era".

**When to use it:** You are writing the farewell speech or all-staff email two days before someone's last day and the manager has given you three bullet points to work with.

**Pro tip:** If the anecdote given is thin ("she was always reliable"), add this line to the prompt: "If the anecdote is vague, expand it into a plausible illustrative scene using the context provided, and mark it [EXPANDED] so the sender can verify or replace it."

> You are an HR Manager writing a promotion announcement that doubles as a recognition message.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Previous title: {old_title}
> New title: {new_title}
> Tenure in previous role: {tenure_in_role}
> Two to three reasons for promotion: {reason_1}, {reason_2}, {reason_3}
> New team or scope: {new_responsibilities_or_team_size}
> Audience: {department / whole_company}
> Tone: {professional / warm / direct}
>
> Write a 180 to 220 word promotion announcement that reads as a genuine recognition, not a press release. Name the employee's previous contributions in sentence two — not buried at the end. State the new role and scope clearly in the middle. Close with one line from the manager or HR Director that is personal, not corporate boilerplate. Use plain language throughout. Do not use "we are thrilled to announce" or "please join us in congratulating".

**When to use it:** The promotion decision was approved this morning and the manager wants the announcement sent before the team hears it informally in the corridor.

**Pro tip:** "Please join us in congratulating" at the end of a promotion announcement is so overused it actively reduces the perceived sincerity. If ChatGPT includes it, that is your signal to regenerate the closing paragraph.

> You are an HR Manager writing a message to mark an employee's completion of a significant internal certification or training programme.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Programme name: {programme_name}
> Duration of programme: {programme_duration: e.g. 6 weeks / 3 months}
> What the programme required: {key_requirements_or_challenges_of_the_programme}
> How this applies to their role: {one_to_two_sentences_on_role_application}
> Message format: {Slack_message / email / certificate_citation}
> Tone: {encouraging / formal / brief_and_direct}
>
> Write a 100 to 150 word message in the specified format. Lead with what the employee achieved, not what the programme is. Explain in one sentence why completing this programme was non-trivial. Connect the achievement to a specific capability they now bring to the team. Close with one forward-looking sentence. No bullet points.

**When to use it:** Your LMS sends you a completion notification and you want to do more than the automated system email that already fired.

**Pro tip:** If the programme is external (e.g. a CIPD qualification or AWS certification), add {external_or_internal} as a variable and include "If external, note that the employee balanced this alongside their day job" in the prompt — this detail is often the most meaningful part of the recognition.

> You are an HR Manager writing a milestone message for an employee who has just managed their first direct report for a full year.
>
> Manager: {employee_name}
> Their direct report: {direct_report_name} (first name only is fine)
> Key management moment from the year: {one_specific_challenge_they_navigated_as_a_manager}
> What their manager observed about their growth: {manager_observation_in_one_to_two_sentences}
> Format: {private_1on1_message / team_mention / written_note}
> Tone: {developmental_and_warm / direct / celebratory}
>
> Write a 150 to 180 word message recognising the first full year as a people manager. Open by naming the milestone specifically — not "what a year it's been". Acknowledge the specific management challenge. Include the manager's observation in your own words. Close by naming one quality you expect to see develop in year two. Do not give management advice. This is recognition, not coaching.

**When to use it:** The one-year mark for a new manager passes and you want to mark it before their next 1:1 with their own manager.

**Pro tip:** The final instruction — "do not give management advice, this is recognition not coaching" — is necessary because ChatGPT almost always pivots to tips and suggestions when the topic is management development. Include it verbatim.

## Recognition for Difficult Contributions (Invisible Work, Hard Periods, Support Roles)

> You are an HR Manager writing a recognition message for an employee who carried significant workload during a colleague's extended leave.
>
> Employee: {employee_name}
> Their role: {job_title}
> How long they covered extra responsibilities: {cover_duration: e.g. 8 weeks / 4 months}
> Specific tasks or responsibilities they absorbed: {two_to_three_additional_responsibilities}
> Impact on the team or business: {tangible_outcome_or_what_did_not_fall_over}
> Format: {private_email / team_Slack / 1on1_conversation_script}
> Tone: {genuine_and_specific / warm / understated}
>
> Write a 150 to 200 word recognition message that opens by naming the specific period and what the employee took on — not a vague "you went above and beyond". Acknowledge that this kind of contribution is often invisible because it prevents problems rather than creates visible wins. Close with one concrete next step: a thank-you meeting, a note to their file, or a commitment from leadership. Match the format specified.

**When to use it:** A colleague returns from parental or sick leave and you realise the person who covered never got formally thanked.

**Pro tip:** The phrase "prevents problems rather than creates visible wins" is the key reframe for this type of recognition. Employees who cover for colleagues are often resentful that their effort was invisible — naming this directly in the message is the thing that makes it land.
