## Workforce Summary and Headcount Narrative

> You are an HR manager writing the workforce overview section of your company's annual HR report.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Reporting year: {report_year}
> Headcount at start of year: {headcount_start}
> Headcount at end of year: {headcount_end}
> Net change and reason (e.g. expansion, restructure, attrition): {net_change_reason}
> Departments with largest headcount movement: {top_departments}
> Tone: {executive / board-facing / internal}
>
> Write a 250 to 300 word workforce summary for an HR annual report. Open with the net headcount figure and what drove the change. Break down movement by department in the middle paragraph. Close with one forward-looking sentence about workforce planning priorities for the next year. Write in clear, third-person narrative prose — no bullet points, no subheadings.

**When to use it:** Monday morning when the CHRO asks for a draft of the opening workforce section before Thursday's board pack deadline.

**Pro tip:** If your headcount numbers shifted mid-year due to a merger or acquisition, add that event as a variable and instruct the AI to treat it as a separate paragraph — otherwise the narrative will blend organic growth with acquisition headcount and the numbers will read as misleading to a board audience.

**Pro tip:** If contractor numbers are commercially sensitive, instruct the AI to refer to them as "contingent workforce" and omit the raw count — just use the percentage of total workforce instead.

## Recruitment and Talent Acquisition Results

> You are an HR manager writing the annual recruitment results section of an HR annual report.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Total hires this year: {total_hires}
> Internal fills vs external hires split: {internal_vs_external_split}
> Average time to hire in days: {avg_time_to_hire}
> Average cost per hire: {avg_cost_per_hire}
> Hardest roles to fill this year: {hard_to_fill_roles}
> Tone: {results-focused / executive-level / plain}
>
> Write a 300 to 350 word recruitment performance narrative for an annual HR report. Lead with total hiring volume and internal vs external split. Present time-to-hire and cost-per-hire in the context of industry norms if possible. Name the hardest-to-fill roles and what drove the difficulty. Close with one sentence on what will change in recruitment strategy next year. Write in paragraph form — no bullet points or subheadings.

**When to use it:** Wednesday afternoon, when the recruitment team has sent you the year-end numbers and you need to turn a spreadsheet into a report section before the Friday draft deadline.

**Pro tip:** If your cost-per-hire figure is higher than the previous year, instruct the AI to contextualise it against hiring volume — a higher cost can be a reasonable trade-off if you hired significantly more people. Without that framing, the number looks like a failure in isolation.

## Employee Retention and Attrition Analysis

> You are an HR manager writing the overall attrition section of an annual HR report.
>
> Organisation name: {org_name}
> Overall attrition rate for the year: {overall_attrition_rate}
> Voluntary attrition rate: {voluntary_attrition_rate}
> Involuntary attrition rate: {involuntary_attrition_rate}
> Attrition rate year prior (for comparison): {prior_year_rate}
> Departments or functions with highest voluntary attrition: {high_attrition_areas}
> Tone: {analytical / direct / executive}
>
> Write a 300 to 350 word attrition narrative for an annual HR report. Open with the headline rate and the year-on-year movement. Separate voluntary from involuntary clearly. Identify where attrition was concentrated and what that signals. Close with one sentence on the retention focus for the next reporting year. Paragraph form only — no bullet points or subheadings.

**When to use it:** Tuesday morning when the HRIS export is in front of you and the CHRO needs the attrition story written before the board pack goes to print.

**Pro tip:** Avoid instructing the AI to call attrition "healthy churn" — the phrase reads as dismissive in board documents and will invite pushback from non-HR directors who see people leaving as a cost problem, not a feature.

> You are an HR manager writing the retention risk section of an annual HR report, focused on flight risk segments.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Employee segments identified as highest retention risk: {risk_segments}
> Key factors driving retention risk in those segments: {risk_factors}
> Retention interventions introduced this year: {interventions}
> Measurable impact of those interventions if available: {intervention_impact}
> Remaining gaps not yet addressed: {remaining_gaps}
> Tone: {honest / solution-focused / concise}
>
> Write a 250 to 300 word retention risk narrative for an annual HR report. Name the at-risk segments, explain the drivers, describe what was done, and report the impact. Be direct about what has not yet been fixed. Close with one sentence on next year's priority. Paragraph form only.

**When to use it:** When you need to show the board that HR has identified risks proactively, not just reported attrition after the fact.

**Pro tip:** If your interventions show limited measurable impact, do not have the AI pad the section with activity descriptions. Name the gap plainly and state what will be different next year — that builds more credibility with a board than a long list of initiatives that didn't move the dial.
