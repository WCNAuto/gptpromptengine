## Drafting OKRs for the HR Function

> You are an HR Director writing quarterly OKRs for the HR function of a mid-to-large company.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Quarter and year: {quarter_year}
> HR team size: {hr_team_size}
> Business priority this quarter: {top_business_priority}
> Current HR challenge: {biggest_hr_pain_point}
> Tone: {formal / plain-language}
>
> Write 3 HR Objectives for this quarter. Under each Objective, write 3 Key Results. Key Results must be measurable — include a number, percentage, or deadline in every one. Format as a clean numbered list. Total output 300 to 400 words. Do not include commentary or explanation. Output the OKRs only.

**When to use it:** Monday morning before your leadership sync, when you need a first draft of HR OKRs to pressure-test with the CHRO before they go to the exec team.

**Pro tip:** If {top_business_priority} is something vague like "growth," replace it before you paste. Write "hire 40 net-new salespeople by end of Q3" instead. Vague inputs produce unmeasurable Key Results that will get rejected in review.

> You are an HR Manager writing a single stretch OKR for a high-performing HR Business Partner.
>
> HRBP name: {hrbp_name}
> Supported business unit: {business_unit}
> Quarter: {quarter}
> One capability the HRBP needs to grow: {development_focus}
> One business outcome their unit must hit: {unit_business_outcome}
> Measure of success already agreed: {agreed_metric}
>
> Write one Objective and exactly 3 Key Results for this HRBP's personal development OKR. The Objective should connect their growth area to the business unit outcome. Every Key Result must reference {agreed_metric} or a leading indicator of it. 150 to 200 words. Output the OKR only — no preamble, no explanation.

**When to use it:** After a mid-year development conversation where you and the HRBP agreed on a focus area but haven't converted it to a measurable commitment yet.

**Pro tip:** If the HRBP pushes back that the KR is too ambitious, run the same prompt with "70% confidence stretch" added to the constraint line. ChatGPT will soften the target range while keeping it measurable.

> You are writing the OKR section of a quarterly business review slide deck for HR.
>
> Quarter being reviewed: {review_quarter}
> HR OKR 1 — Objective: {objective_1}
> HR OKR 1 — Final score (0.0 to 1.0): {score_1}
> HR OKR 2 — Objective: {objective_2}
> HR OKR 2 — Final score: {score_2}
> What went well: {one_to_three_wins}
> What missed and why: {one_to_two_misses_with_reason}
>
> Write a 200 to 250 word narrative summary of HR's OKR performance this quarter. Use plain language an exec with no HR background can follow. Lead with the score, then the story behind it. Name what drove the wins. Name the miss without making excuses — one sentence on root cause, one on the fix. Close with the carry-forward priority for next quarter.

**When to use it:** The day before your QBR when you have the scores but not the story. Drop this narrative into your slide notes or the summary slide itself.

**Pro tip:** Keep {one_to_two_misses_with_reason} honest — if you write "missed due to headcount freeze" the output will sound like an excuse. Write "missed because we underestimated onboarding backlog volume by 35%" and the output reads like accountability.

> You are an HR Manager writing OKRs for a new HR function that has never used the OKR framework before.
>
> Company stage: {startup / scale-up / enterprise}
> Number of employees: {headcount}
> Current HR priorities (list up to 3): {hr_priorities}
> Biggest operational bottleneck in HR right now: {bottleneck}
> Time horizon: {quarter or half-year}
>
> Write 2 Objectives with 2 Key Results each. Keep language simple — these will be read by an HR team that has not used OKRs before. Avoid jargon. Every Key Result must be binary (done / not done) or have a specific number. After the OKRs, write a 3-sentence plain-language explanation of how to track these weekly. Total output 250 to 350 words.

**When to use it:** When you've just been asked to "implement OKRs in HR" and need something concrete to show the team in the kickoff meeting this week.

**Pro tip:** For first-time OKR teams, binary Key Results ("all job descriptions audited for bias language by March 31") outperform percentage targets in the first cycle. People track them without needing a dashboard.

>
> Company's stated DEI commitment: {dei_commitment_verbatim}
> Current representation baseline: {current_baseline_data}
> Target improvement: {target_and_timeframe}

**Pro tip:** Pull {current_baseline_data} from your ATS or HRIS before running this prompt. If you leave it vague, the Key Results will be generic and won't survive a CFO challenge on measurability.

## Cascading OKRs from Company Level to Team Level

> You are an HR Manager helping a department head cascade company OKRs into their team's quarterly goals.
>
> Company-level Objective: {company_objective}
> Company Key Result this team contributes to: {company_kr}
> Department: {department_name}
> Department head name: {dept_head_name}
> Team size: {team_size}
> Current team output metric: {existing_team_metric}
>
> Write a 1-page cascade brief. Section 1: one sentence explaining how this team's work connects to {company_kr}. Section 2: one team-level Objective and 3 Key Results. Section 3: a 3-sentence explanation the department head can read aloud at their next team meeting to explain why these goals matter. Total 250 to 350 words. Plain language. No bullet soup.

**When to use it:** When a department head says "I don't know how to turn the company OKRs into something my team can actually do" and needs a ready-made brief before their Monday standup.

**Pro tip:** If the team's work is indirect (e.g., Finance supporting a Sales OKR), the cascade brief often feels forced. Add "the connection may be indirect — write it honestly, not as a stretch" to the prompt and the output will be more credible to the team.

> You are writing talking points for an HR Business Partner who needs to explain OKR cascading to a skeptical senior manager.
>
> Manager's name: {manager_name}
> Manager's objection: {stated_objection — e.g. "my team's work doesn't map to numbers"}
> HRBP name: {hrbp_name}
> Company OKR the team must contribute to: {company_okr}
> One concrete example from this team's recent work: {recent_team_example}
>
> Write a 200-word script the HRBP can use in a 10-minute one-to-one with {manager_name}. Open by acknowledging {stated_objection} directly — do not dismiss it. Then use {recent_team_example} to show one way the team's work is already measurable. Close with a question that invites the manager to suggest their own Key Result. Conversational tone. No corporate language.

**When to use it:** Before an HRBP meets with a manager who has been visibly resistant in OKR planning sessions and risks derailing the cascade for their whole business unit.

**Pro tip:** The closing question matters more than the rest of the script. If the manager suggests their own KR, they own it. Don't let the AI write a weak close — if it does, ask ChatGPT to rewrite just the last 2 sentences as "an open question that transfers ownership."

> You are an HR Manager writing a cascade alignment memo for the full leadership team.
>
> Company OKRs (list all): {company_okrs_bulleted}
> Quarter: {quarter}
> Number of departments in cascade: {number_of_departments}
> One department that is misaligned: {misaligned_department}
> Nature of the misalignment: {misalignment_description}
>
> Write a 300 to 400 word internal memo addressed to the leadership team. Summarise the cascade status across all departments. Flag {misaligned_department}'s misalignment clearly but without blame — one paragraph. Propose one concrete action HR will take to resolve it before the end of the quarter. Professional tone. Memo format with To / From / Date / Subject header.

**When to use it:** After you've reviewed all department OKR submissions and found at least one team whose goals don't connect to the company objectives at all.

**Pro tip:** Don't soften the misalignment paragraph — executives respect direct language in memos. If the draft buries the issue, add "flag the misalignment in the first sentence of that paragraph, not the last" as a follow-up instruction.

> You are an HR Manager preparing a 30-minute OKR cascade workshop agenda for first-line managers.
>
> Workshop date: {workshop_date}
> Number of attending managers: {attendee_count}
> Company OKRs they must cascade from: {company_okrs_summary}
> Biggest confusion managers had last quarter: {last_quarter_confusion}
> Facilitator name: {facilitator_name}
>
> Write the full workshop agenda. Include timings for each section. Include one 5-minute activity where managers write their own draft Key Result live in the room. Address {last_quarter_confusion} directly in the agenda — build in a moment that specifically resolves it. Close with a commitment: each manager leaves with at least one draft KR written. 250 to 300 words. Agenda format.

**When to use it:** The week before your quarterly planning cycle opens and you need to run a manager enablement session before the submission deadline.

**Pro tip:** Build the live-writing activity before the Q&A, not after. If managers write their draft KR first and then ask questions, the questions are specific to their situation. If you flip the order, you get generic theory questions that eat the clock.

> You are an HR Manager writing a quick-reference one-pager explaining OKR cascading for individual contributors.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Quarter: {quarter}
> One example company Objective: {example_company_objective}
> One example team Key Result: {example_team_kr}
> One example individual contribution that feeds that KR: {example_individual_contribution}
> Reading level: {plain / technical / exec}
>
> Write a one-page explainer an individual contributor can read in under 3 minutes. Use the examples provided to make it concrete. Structure: what cascading means (2 sentences), why it matters to the IC (2 sentences), the worked example showing company → team → individual (use the variables), and one action the IC should take before the quarter starts. 200 to 250 words.

**When to use it:** When ICs are asking why their goals need to connect to company OKRs and you need a shareable explainer that doesn't require a 45-minute all-hands to explain.

**Pro tip:** The worked example is the only part ICs actually read. Run the prompt, then check that the three-level chain in the output is genuinely logical. If the individual contribution doesn't obviously feed the team KR, rewrite {example_individual_contribution} before sending.

## OKR Check-In and Progress Conversations

> You are an HR Business Partner preparing talking points for a mid-quarter OKR check-in with a manager whose team is behind on all Key Results.
>
> Manager name: {manager_name}
> Team: {team_name}
> Quarter: {quarter}
> Key Results and current scores: {kr_list_with_scores}
> Known reason for underperformance: {known_blocker}
> Relationship dynamic (select one): {strong_trust / neutral / strained}
>
> Write a 200-word conversation guide for a 20-minute check-in. Open with a question that invites the manager to self-assess before you share the data. Use the scores to surface the gap without leading with blame. Address {known_blocker} directly — ask what HR can do to remove it. Close with one concrete commitment from each side. Tone must match {relationship_dynamic}.

**When to use it:** When you can see from the OKR dashboard that a team is behind and you need to have the conversation this week, not wait for end-of-quarter.

**Pro tip:** If {relationship_dynamic} is "strained," the AI will sometimes produce language that's still too direct. Add "soften the opening — the manager is defensive" and it will adjust the first two lines specifically.

> You are an HR Manager writing a mid-quarter OKR status update email to the CHRO.
>
> Quarter: {quarter}
> Number of OKRs on track: {on_track_count}
> Number at risk: {at_risk_count}
> Number off track: {off_track_count}
> Biggest risk and why: {biggest_risk_description}
> Action HR is taking: {hr_action}
>
> Write a 150 to 200 word status update email. Subject line included. Lead with the numbers. Name the biggest risk in plain terms. Name what HR is doing about it — one specific action. Do not pad the email. CHRO tone: direct and data-led.

**When to use it:** Friday afternoon before a Monday leadership meeting, when the CHRO needs a quick read on HR OKR health without wading through a dashboard.

**Pro tip:** CHROs notice when the "action HR is taking" is vague. Before you paste, make sure {hr_action} is something concrete that started this week — not "we are monitoring the situation."
