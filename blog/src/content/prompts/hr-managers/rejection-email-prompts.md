## After-Application Rejections (Pre-Screen Stage)

> You are an HR Manager sending a rejection email to a candidate who applied but was not selected for a phone screen.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role applied for: {job_title}
> Department: {department}
> Application date: {application_date}
> Primary reason not progressing (internal only, do not state explicitly): {reason_eg_missing_required_qualification}
> Tone: {warm_and_brief / professional_and_neutral}
>
> Write a 120 to 150 word rejection email. Open by thanking them for applying. Do not state the reason for rejection. Include one sentence acknowledging the competitive applicant pool. Close with a genuine encouragement to apply for future roles and a sign-off from the HR team. Do not use phrases like "we regret to inform you" or "unfortunately at this time".

**When to use it:** Monday morning after the job posting closes and you have 40+ applicants to clear before the shortlist meeting at 2pm.

**Pro tip:** If your ATS auto-sends rejections but candidates still reply asking for feedback, run this prompt with `{tone: warm_and_brief}` — the warmer close reduces reply volume by giving candidates a sense of closure without inviting a conversation.

> You are an HR Manager rejecting a candidate who applied via a job board but whose CV does not meet the minimum requirements stated in the job description.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Job title: {job_title}
> Specific requirement not met (for your reference only): {unmet_requirement_eg_5_years_experience}
> Job board the candidate applied through: {job_board_name}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Tone: {formal / conversational}
>
> Write a 100 to 130 word rejection email. Do not reference the specific unmet requirement. Open with thanks. State clearly they will not be progressed. Include one sentence pointing them toward the careers page for future openings. End with a polite sign-off. No bullet points.

**When to use it:** When you're clearing the unqualified tier from a high-volume role — the kind of application you need to close in under 60 seconds per candidate.

**Pro tip:** Remove `{job_board_name}` from the output before sending — candidates sometimes feel flagged or tracked when they see which platform is named. The variable is useful for your own records but strip it from the final email.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who applied for a role that has been filled internally before external interviews began.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Time between application and rejection: {time_elapsed_eg_two_weeks}
> Tone: {apologetic_and_warm / neutral_and_direct}
> Is the role likely to reopen externally within 6 months: {yes / no / unsure}
>
> Write a 130 to 160 word email. Explain the role was filled through an internal process — do not imply the candidate was unqualified. If `{is_role_likely_to_reopen}` is yes or unsure, invite them to stay connected or re-apply. If no, close with a genuine wish for their search. Do not use generic phrases like "we'll keep your CV on file" unless you actually will.

**When to use it:** When a role gets pulled mid-process due to an internal promotion or restructure and you have external candidates who applied in good faith.

**Pro tip:** The "we'll keep your CV on file" line destroys candidate trust when it's not true. This prompt forces you to make an active decision on that before the email goes out — which also protects your employer brand on review sites.

> You are an HR Manager sending a bulk rejection email to candidates who applied for a graduate scheme but did not pass the initial screening criteria.
>
> Graduate scheme name: {scheme_name}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Intake year: {intake_year}
> Number of applications received (include in email): {total_applications}
> Closing stage for this group: {cv_screen / online_assessment / video_interview}
> Tone: {encouraging / professional_and_brief}
>
> Write a 150 to 180 word rejection email suitable for early-career candidates. Acknowledge the effort they put into applying. Mention the high volume of applications as context, not excuse. Include a single, specific encouragement about their career stage. Do not promise feedback. Close with a wish for their next step. No bullet points. No headers.

**When to use it:** After the first cull on a graduate intake — typically when you're closing out 500+ applications and need a message that doesn't feel like a form letter even though it technically is.

**Pro tip:** Mentioning the actual number of applications (`{total_applications}`) in the email is the single most effective way to make a mass rejection feel less personal. Candidates process "we had 1,200 applications" differently from "we had a competitive pool."

> You are an HR Manager rejecting a referred candidate — someone who was recommended by a current employee — who did not meet the requirements after CV review.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role applied for: {job_title}
> Name of employee who referred them: {referring_employee_name}
> Relationship context (colleague, friend, former manager): {relationship_context}
> Tone: {warm_and_respectful / formal}
> Should the referring employee receive a separate note: {yes / no}
>
> Write a 140 to 170 word rejection email to the referred candidate. Acknowledge the referral graciously without over-explaining it. Do not imply the referrer vouched for their qualifications. Be clear they are not progressing. If `{should_referring_employee_receive_note}` is yes, add a short 3-sentence internal note the HR Manager can send separately to the referrer.

**When to use it:** When an employee has referred someone and you need to reject that candidate without damaging the internal relationship or making the referrer feel embarrassed.

**Pro tip:** The 3-sentence internal note for the referrer is the part most HR Managers skip — and it's the part that causes friction. Use it to close the loop before the referring employee hears from their friend that they were rejected.

## Post-Phone-Screen Rejections

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email after a candidate completed an initial phone screen but will not be progressed to the interview stage.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Phone screen date: {screen_date}
> Interviewer name (optional, for sign-off): {interviewer_name}
> Primary gap identified during screen (internal use, do not state explicitly): {gap_eg_salary_expectations_misaligned}
> Tone: {warm_and_direct / formal}
>
> Write a 160 to 200 word rejection email. Thank them for their time on the call. Reference the conversation positively without fabricating specific praise. State clearly they will not be moved forward. Do not give the specific reason. Close with a genuine note — if salary was the gap, do not hint at it. Sign off warmly. No bullet points.

**When to use it:** Same day as the phone screen, before the end of business — candidates who've spoken to someone expect faster feedback than CV-only applicants.

**Pro tip:** Never hint at salary misalignment in a post-screen rejection. Candidates will use it to negotiate or push back, and you'll spend 20 minutes managing a reply that could have been avoided.

> You are an HR Manager rejecting a candidate after a phone screen for a senior leadership role. The candidate was strong but the role requires deeper sector-specific experience than they currently have.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title_eg_Head_of_Operations}
> Sector experience required: {required_sector_eg_financial_services}
> Candidate's current sector: {candidate_sector}
> Length of call: {call_duration_eg_30_minutes}
> Tone: {respectful_and_direct / warm_and_professional}
>
> Write a 200 to 240 word rejection email. Open by thanking them for the quality of the conversation. Acknowledge their experience without being patronising. State they are not progressing without giving specific sector detail. Close with a door-open line if their profile fits a more generalist role you hire for regularly — otherwise close cleanly. Do not use the phrase "overqualified."

**When to use it:** When you've spoken to a credible senior candidate who misses on one specific dimension — you want to reject clearly but keep the relationship intact for future pipeline.

**Pro tip:** The phrase "overqualified" has legal risk in several jurisdictions as it can imply age discrimination. This prompt deliberately excludes it — but check your own notes and verbal summaries to make sure it doesn't appear anywhere in your file either.

> You are an HR Manager sending a rejection email to a candidate who completed a phone screen for a part-time or flexible-hours role and whose availability does not match the position's requirements.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Required working pattern: {required_pattern_eg_three_days_onsite_Tuesday_to_Thursday}
> Candidate's stated availability: {candidate_availability}
> Tone: {empathetic / professional_and_brief}
> Is there another open role with different hours: {yes_with_role_name / no}
>
> Write a 150 to 180 word rejection email. Do not frame the availability mismatch as a problem with the candidate. State clearly the role's fixed requirements cannot be adjusted. If `{is_there_another_open_role}` includes a role name, mention it specifically. If no, close without a placeholder offer. No bullet points.

**When to use it:** When flexible working expectations come up on the screen and the candidate's needs genuinely don't fit the role — not as a proxy for any other reason.

**Pro tip:** Only redirect to another open role if you have genuinely reviewed the candidate's profile against it. Sending a hollow redirect and then rejecting them again in a week is worse for your brand than a clean close here.

> You are an HR Manager rejecting a candidate after a phone screen where the candidate was rude, unprepared, or behaved in a way that raised concerns — but you need to send a professional rejection without referencing the conduct.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Date of screen: {screen_date}
> Specific conduct concern (internal only, never include in email): {conduct_note_eg_interrupted_repeatedly_spoke_disparagingly_about_previous_employer}
> Tone: {professional_and_neutral / brief_and_final}
>
> Write a 100 to 130 word rejection email. Say only that they will not be progressed. Do not give feedback. Do not imply anything negative. Do not invite a reply or a request for feedback. Close cleanly and finally. This email should leave no opening for a follow-up conversation.

**When to use it:** When you need to close out a candidate who raised red flags on the call, and you want a paper trail that is professional and undisprovable.

**Pro tip:** Add your conduct notes to the candidate's file in your ATS the same day and tag the record so the same candidate doesn't enter your pipeline again on a different role six months later.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email after a phone screen where the candidate was genuinely impressive but the role was cancelled or put on hold after the screen was already booked.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role that was cancelled: {job_title}
> Reason role was paused (internal use only): {internal_reason_eg_headcount_freeze}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Expected timeline for role to reopen (if known): {timeline_or_unknown}
> Tone: {apologetic_and_genuine / warm_and_professional}
>
> Write a 180 to 220 word rejection email. Be honest that the role is on hold — not that the candidate didn't qualify. Apologise for their time specifically. If a realistic timeline exists, mention it. Invite them to connect on LinkedIn or stay in contact only if you mean it — include a genuine line, not a placeholder. Do not use "keep you on file" unless you have a system to actually do it.

**When to use it:** When a hiring freeze lands after interviews are already booked — one of the most damaging candidate experience scenarios if handled with a generic email.

**Pro tip:** If you genuinely want to re-engage this candidate when the role reopens, paste their name and the reopen date into a calendar reminder now, before you send the email. The email promise means nothing without the follow-through mechanism.

## Post-Interview Rejections (First and Second Round)

> You are an HR Manager writing a post-first-interview rejection email for a candidate who interviewed well but another candidate was stronger on a specific technical dimension.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Interview date: {interview_date}
> Hiring manager who interviewed them: {hiring_manager_name}
> One specific strength the candidate demonstrated (from interview notes): {genuine_strength_eg_strong_stakeholder_communication}
> Tone: {warm_and_specific / professional}
>
> Write a 200 to 250 word rejection email. Open by thanking them for their time. Name the specific strength genuinely — one sentence, no padding. State they were not selected without explaining who was or why in detail. Close with a specific, honest encouragement. If there is a natural reason to stay in touch (talent community, related roles), state it once. Do not fabricate closeness or warmth that was not there.

**When to use it:** After a strong first-round interview — typically 24 to 48 hours after the panel debrief when the hiring decision is confirmed.

**Pro tip:** Pull the strength directly from your interview scorecard before running this prompt. Fabricated praise ("you came across very professionally") reads as generic and candidates notice. One real detail outperforms five vague compliments.

> You are an HR Manager rejecting a candidate after a second-round interview for a management role. They reached the final two but the other candidate was selected.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Number of interview rounds completed: {rounds_completed}
> Genuine strengths observed across the process: {two_or_three_strengths_from_notes}
> Tone: {warm_and_respectful / direct_and_honest}
> Will you offer a feedback call: {yes / no}
>
> Write a 250 to 300 word rejection email. Acknowledge the investment of time they made. Do not use "we went with someone with more experience" — it is vague and often untrue. Name the strengths genuinely. If `{will_you_offer_feedback_call}` is yes, include a specific offer with a timeframe (e.g. "a 15-minute call this week"). If no, close without implying feedback is available. Do not say "we'll keep you in mind" unless a specific follow-up is planned.

**When to use it:** When you're closing out the runner-up in a final-round decision — the highest-stakes rejection email you will send in a hiring cycle.
