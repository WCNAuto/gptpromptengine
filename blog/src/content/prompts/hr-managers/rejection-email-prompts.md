## Post-Interview Rejection Emails

> You are an HR Manager writing a post-interview rejection email.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role they applied for: {job_title}
> Interview stage reached: {interview_stage: first-round / second-round / final}
> Main reason for not progressing (internal only, do not state explicitly): {internal_reason}
> One genuine strength you observed: {candidate_strength}
> Tone: {tone: warm / neutral / formal}
>
> Write a 150 to 200 word rejection email that opens by thanking the candidate for their time at the {interview_stage} interview. Name their {candidate_strength} authentically — one sentence, specific, not generic. Decline clearly without explaining the internal reason. Close by wishing them well in their search. Do not use the phrase "we have decided to move forward with other candidates" — say it differently. Output a subject line and the full email body.

**When to use it:** Monday morning after a Friday panel interview when three candidates need rejections before the hiring manager asks for an update.

**Pro tip:** If your {internal_reason} is salary mismatch, delete it entirely before pasting the prompt — AI sometimes surfaces it indirectly in the framing even when told not to. Check the output before sending.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email after a second-round technical interview.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Technical area where they fell short: {technical_gap}
> Strongest moment in the interview: {best_moment}
> Whether to invite them to reapply in future: {reapply: yes / no}
> Tone: {tone: direct / encouraging}
>
> Write a 180 to 220 word email. Open with thanks. In one sentence acknowledge {best_moment} without being patronising. Decline clearly. If {reapply} is yes, include a single sentence inviting them to watch for future openings in {technical_gap}-adjacent roles. If {reapply} is no, omit that entirely. Close warmly. Include subject line.

**When to use it:** After a technical screen where the candidate was strong culturally but missed on a specific skill — and you genuinely want them back in the pipeline later.

**Pro tip:** Only set {reapply} to yes if you mean it. Candidates follow up on those sentences. If your ATS doesn't tag them for future outreach, the invitation is empty and damages trust.

> You are an HR Manager sending a rejection after a final-round panel interview for a senior leadership role.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Seniority level: {seniority: Director / VP / C-Suite}
> Length of process they went through: {process_length: e.g. "four rounds over six weeks"}
> One area of genuine distinction in their candidacy: {standout_quality}
> Whether the hiring manager wants to stay in touch personally: {hm_followup: yes / no}
>
> Write a 250 to 300 word rejection email appropriate for a {seniority}-level candidate who invested {process_length} in this process. Acknowledge the time investment directly. Name {standout_quality} specifically. Decline with clear language. If {hm_followup} is yes, include a line that the hiring manager will reach out separately. Tone is respectful, peer-to-peer, not corporate. Include subject line.

**When to use it:** After a six-week executive search process ends and the runner-up was genuinely excellent — this email has to hold the relationship, not just close the process.

**Pro tip:** Senior candidates talk to each other. A vague email after a long process gets shared. The {standout_quality} line does more reputational work than any employer brand campaign.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email after a final interview where the candidate was the second-choice hire.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Reason they weren't selected (internal framing): {selection_reason: e.g. "the other candidate had directly relevant sector experience"}
> How close the decision was: {decision_closeness: very close / clear}
> Tone: {tone: warm / formal}
> Open role in a related team that may suit them: {related_role: role title or "none"}
>
> Write a 200 to 250 word rejection email. If {decision_closeness} is very close, communicate that authentically in one sentence without making it sound like a consolation prize. Do not disclose the other candidate or your {selection_reason}. If {related_role} is not "none", mention it briefly at the end. Close professionally. Include subject line.

**When to use it:** The hire is made, the offer is accepted, and you owe the runner-up a proper email before they read about the new hire on LinkedIn.

**Pro tip:** Send this before the new hire announcement goes out — not after. Candidates who find out via social media before getting a rejection feel the process was disrespectful, regardless of how good the email is.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email for a candidate who gave a strong presentation in a final-round task but wasn't selected.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Presentation topic they tackled: {presentation_topic}
> One specific element of the presentation that was genuinely impressive: {impressive_element}
> Primary gap that led to the decision: {decision_gap: do not state this in the email}
> Tone: {tone: professional / warm}
>
> Write a 180 to 230 word rejection email. Reference {presentation_topic} by name so the candidate knows this is not a bulk email. Acknowledge {impressive_element} in one specific sentence. Decline without citing {decision_gap}. Wish them well. Include subject line. Do not use the phrase "we were impressed by your passion".

**When to use it:** After a task-based final round where the candidate clearly worked hard and deserves acknowledgement that someone actually watched their work.

**Pro tip:** Naming {presentation_topic} in the subject line gets the email opened instead of ignored — candidates on active job searches get a lot of rejections and skim subject lines first.

## High-Volume Application Rejection Emails

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email for candidates who applied but were not shortlisted for a phone screen.
>
> Role: {job_title}
> Department: {department}
> Volume of applicants (for internal context, do not include in email): {applicant_volume}
> One honest reason most applications didn't progress (do not state directly): {screening_criterion}
> Tone: {tone: brief / warm}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 100 to 130 word rejection email that feels human, not automated. Thank the candidate for applying to {company_name}. Decline clearly. Do not explain the {screening_criterion}. Do not say "we received an overwhelming number of applications" — find a different way to acknowledge competition. Close with a genuine wish for their search. Include subject line. This email will be sent to hundreds of candidates — it must read as respectful at scale.

**When to use it:** After closing applications on a high-volume role where you screened 300 CVs and 260 won't advance past the application stage.

**Pro tip:** Avoid any line that implies the candidate can reapply immediately — high-volume roles often close and reopen, and you'll get hundreds of duplicate applications within 48 hours if you suggest it.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection for a candidate who applied for a role that has been put on hold or cancelled.
>
> Candidate name (or "Dear Applicant" if bulk): {candidate_name}
> Role they applied for: {job_title}
> Reason role is paused (do not include in email): {internal_reason: e.g. "headcount freeze"}
> Whether the role may reopen: {may_reopen: likely / uncertain / no}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Tone: {tone: transparent / formal}
>
> Write a 120 to 160 word email. Inform the candidate the role is on hold — do not cite {internal_reason}. If {may_reopen} is likely, invite them to reapply if it reopens and offer to retain their details. If {may_reopen} is uncertain or no, close cleanly without a false promise. Apologise briefly for the disruption to their search. Include subject line.

**When to use it:** When a hiring freeze lands mid-process and you have 40 active applicants who deserve an honest update rather than silence.

**Pro tip:** If {may_reopen} is uncertain, do not write "we hope to revisit this in the future." That sentence generates follow-up emails for months. Be cleanly inconclusive instead.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who applied for a role that was filled by an internal promotion.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role they applied for: {job_title}
> Whether to disclose the internal fill (yes or no): {disclose_internal: yes / no}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Time since they applied: {application_age: e.g. "three weeks"}
> Tone: {tone: transparent / standard}
>
> Write a 130 to 170 word rejection email. Thank them for applying. If {disclose_internal} is yes, include a single sentence explaining the role was filled internally — no more detail than that. If {disclose_internal} is no, decline without explanation. Acknowledge if {application_age} means they've been waiting longer than normal. Close respectfully. Include subject line.

**When to use it:** When a succession planning decision closes an external search you posted publicly — candidates deserve more than a generic no.

**Pro tip:** Most candidates actually appreciate the internal-fill disclosure — it removes self-doubt about their application. Legal rarely objects to that single sentence. Check with your TA lead before making it a blanket policy, though.

> You are an HR Manager writing a batch rejection email for candidates who passed an initial screen but will not progress to interview because the shortlist is now full.
>
> Role: {job_title}
> Number of shortlisted candidates (do not include in email): {shortlist_size}
> Stage the candidate reached: {stage_reached: application screen / CV review / skills assessment}
> Whether you might contact them if a shortlisted candidate drops out: {reserve_pool: yes / no}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 140 to 180 word rejection email. Acknowledge that their {stage_reached} showed enough to pass initial review — say this sincerely, not generically. Decline clearly. If {reserve_pool} is yes, tell them you'll keep their details for 30 days in case a space opens — only if you actually have a process for this. If {reserve_pool} is no, close without that line. Include subject line.

**When to use it:** When your shortlist of six is locked but you screened 30 strong CVs and need to close them out cleanly.

**Pro tip:** Only use the {reserve_pool} yes option if someone on your team is actually monitoring that list. Empty reserve pool promises are the most common source of complaint emails from rejected candidates.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who completed a take-home assessment but will not be invited to interview.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Time they likely spent on the assessment: {effort_estimate: e.g. "two to three hours"}
> What the assessment asked them to do: {assessment_type: e.g. "a 500-word written brief"}
> One genuine positive from their submission: {submission_positive}
> Reason they're not progressing (do not state in email): {decline_reason}
>
> Write a 160 to 200 word rejection email. Open by acknowledging the time invested in {assessment_type} — be specific about {effort_estimate}. Name {submission_positive} in one sentence. Decline clearly. Do not offer detailed feedback unless your process supports it — instead, offer a brief note that detailed written feedback is not available at this stage. Close respectfully. Include subject line.

**When to use it:** After a written exercise round where 20 candidates submitted work and only six move forward — these emails matter because unpaid effort deserves acknowledgement.

**Pro tip:** Do not include a feedback request survey link in a rejection email for an unpaid assessment. It reads as tone-deaf. If you want feedback on your assessment process, use a separate post-process survey two weeks later.

## Rejection Emails for Specific Candidate Situations

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who was referred by an existing employee.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role they applied for: {job_title}
> Name of the employee who referred them: {referring_employee}
> Whether {referring_employee} knows the outcome already: {referrer_informed: yes / no}
> Reason for rejection (do not state in email): {rejection_reason}
> Tone: {tone: warm / professional}
>
> Write a 160 to 200 word rejection email directly to {candidate_name}. Thank them for applying and acknowledge the referral from {referring_employee} briefly. Decline clearly without citing {rejection_reason}. If {referrer_informed} is no, do not mention that in the email — that's a separate conversation. Close warmly. Include subject line. Do not make the email read as if rejecting the referring employee's judgement.

**When to use it:** When a staff referral doesn't make the cut and you need to send the rejection before the referring employee accidentally tells them the outcome in the corridor.

**Pro tip:** Always speak to the referring employee before this email goes out — not after. Finding out a friend was rejected via forwarded email is a morale issue, not just an admin one.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who has applied to the same company multiple times and has been unsuccessful again.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role they applied for this time: {job_title}
> Number of previous applications: {previous_applications: e.g. "two"}
> Whether to address the repeat applications directly: {address_repeat: yes / no}
> Specific gap that keeps leading to rejection (do not state, but use to shape tone): {persistent_gap}
> Tone: {tone: honest / standard}
>
> Write a 160 to 210 word rejection email. If {address_repeat} is yes, include one sentence that gently acknowledges their continued interest — do not list the number of rejections. Decline clearly. If {address_repeat} is yes and {tone} is honest, add one sentence suggesting they may want to build experience in a specific area before applying again — keep it constructive, not discouraging. If {address_repeat} is no, treat it as a standard rejection. Include subject line.

**When to use it:** When someone applies for the fourth time to a role they're consistently underqualified for and a form rejection is doing them a disservice.

**Pro tip:** The "one sentence of honest guidance" only works if it's genuinely useful — do not write it if {persistent_gap} is something the candidate cannot control, like sector experience requirements that are structurally unlikely for them to acquire.
