## Post-Interview Rejection Emails

> You are an HR Manager writing a post-interview rejection email for a candidate who reached the final stages of a hiring process.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role they applied for: {job_title}
> Interview stage they reached: {interview_stage} (e.g. second interview, panel interview, final round)
> Honest reason for rejection (internal only, do not quote directly): {internal_reason} (e.g. stronger candidate on technical depth, culture fit concern, salary mismatch)
> One genuine positive observation from the interview: {positive_observation}
> Would we consider them for future roles: {future_consideration: yes / no / possibly}
> Tone: {tone: warm / professional / brief}
>
> Write a 150 to 200 word rejection email. Open by thanking them for their time at the {interview_stage}. Name the positive observation in a specific, non-generic sentence. Decline clearly without giving the internal reason. If {future_consideration} is yes or possibly, close with a sincere one-sentence invitation to stay in touch. Do not use phrases like "we've decided to move in a different direction" or "we wish you all the best in your search" — write like a human, not a form letter.

**When to use it:** Friday afternoon when you're closing out a week-long final-round process and have three or four finalists to decline.

**Pro tip:** If your {internal_reason} involves salary mismatch, do not hint at it in any form — even softened language like "the role's scope evolved" can open a negotiation you've already decided to close. Keep the email silent on reasons entirely and let the positive observation carry the warmth.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who completed a take-home assignment or skills test but did not progress to interview.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Assignment they completed: {assignment_description} (e.g. 30-minute data task, writing sample, code challenge)
> Time the candidate likely spent on it: {estimated_effort} (e.g. 1 hour, half a day)
> Primary reason they did not progress: {rejection_reason} (e.g. output did not meet the technical bar, presentation was unclear)
> Tone: {tone: warm / direct}
>
> Write a 130 to 160 word rejection email. Acknowledge the time and effort they put into the {assignment_description} in the first sentence — be specific about what that kind of task requires, not just "we appreciate your effort". Decline clearly. Do not give detailed feedback on the assignment unless it is genuinely constructive and brief. Close with one sentence that treats them with respect as a professional.

**When to use it:** When a candidate spent real time on a task and a generic "thank you for applying" feels dismissive — but you also don't have the bandwidth to write individual feedback.

**Pro tip:** If you're rejecting multiple candidates from the same assignment batch, change {positive_observation} for each one rather than sending identical emails. Candidates talk, especially in tight professional communities. Even one sentence of specificity per person closes that risk.

> You are an HR Manager writing a post-first-interview rejection email for a candidate who interviewed but will not proceed to the next stage.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Interviewer name or team: {interviewer_name}
> What the candidate did well: {strength_noted} (e.g. strong communication, good understanding of the industry)
> Why they are not proceeding: {gap} (e.g. the role requires deeper experience in X, another candidate was further ahead on Y)
> Is the gap something they could close in 6 to 12 months: {closable_gap: yes / no}
> Tone: {tone: warm / formal}
>
> Write a 160 to 200 word rejection email from the perspective of {interviewer_name} or the HR team. Thank them for attending the first interview. Name {strength_noted} as a specific, non-vague positive. Decline for the next stage without a lengthy explanation. If {closable_gap} is yes, include a single honest sentence noting that the gap is not permanent and they are welcome to reapply. Do not pad the email with reassurances or hollow encouragement.

**When to use it:** Monday morning after a Friday interview day when you have six first-round declines to write before candidates start chasing for feedback.

**Pro tip:** "You are welcome to reapply" is only worth writing if you mean it. If your ATS will auto-reject the same person again in 12 months because of a duplicate profile flag, either fix the ATS rule first or remove that sentence from the output.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email after a panel interview where the candidate performed unevenly across different panel members' assessments.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Panel composition: {panel_description} (e.g. hiring manager, technical lead, and a peer from the team)
> Overall panel consensus: {panel_consensus} (e.g. strong on leadership presence, weaker on technical specifics)
> Decision: {decision: not progressing}
> One area the panel agreed was a genuine strength: {agreed_strength}
> Tone: {tone: professional / warm}
>
> Write a 150 to 180 word rejection email. Do not reference individual panel members' opinions or any disagreement within the panel. Mention the {agreed_strength} as a factual positive without overselling it. Decline clearly and in one sentence. Do not explain the scoring methodology or invite the candidate to dispute the outcome. Close with a professional but human sign-off.

**When to use it:** After a structured panel process where the scorecard produced a clear decision but you still want the email to sound like a person wrote it, not a committee.

**Pro tip:** Never include phrases like "it was a very competitive process" unless the role genuinely had high applicant volume. Senior candidates with market awareness will know if you had six applicants — and being patronised makes them less likely to reapply or refer others in future.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who interviewed for a senior leadership role and reached the final two.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title} (e.g. Head of Finance, VP of Operations)
> Brief description of what made the chosen candidate stronger: {differentiator} (keep it honest but non-damaging, e.g. longer direct experience managing a P&L at scale)
> Relationship to preserve: {relationship_type: industry contact / potential future hire / referred by board member}
> Tone: {tone: warm / executive}
> Recruiter or HR contact name: {hr_contact_name}
>
> Write a 200 to 250 word rejection email suitable for a senior professional. Open with a direct and respectful acknowledgment that this was a close decision. Share a version of {differentiator} that is honest without being comparative or diminishing. Do not use any language that sounds like a junior HR form letter. Offer a brief call with {hr_contact_name} if they would like to discuss. Close in a way that leaves the door genuinely open given the {relationship_type}.

**When to use it:** When the runner-up is someone you may hire in 18 months or who sits in the same industry network as your executive team.

**Pro tip:** For board-referred candidates, send this email same-day as the decision — before the referring board member hears through back channels. The sequence matters more than the wording.

## High-Volume and Early-Stage Rejection Emails

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email for a candidate who applied but was screened out at the CV or application review stage, before any interview.
>
> Role: {job_title}
> Department: {department}
> Core reason for screening out (do not quote in email): {screening_reason} (e.g. did not meet minimum qualification, no right to work, location mismatch)
> Volume of applicants for this role: {applicant_volume} (e.g. 80, 200+)
> Company tone of voice: {tone_of_voice: formal / conversational / neutral}
> Recruiter sign-off name: {recruiter_name}
>
> Write a 100 to 130 word application rejection email. Do not reference the {screening_reason} directly. Acknowledge their interest briefly. Decline clearly. Invite them to check future openings if appropriate. Sign off from {recruiter_name}. The email must read like a considered human wrote it, not like an ATS auto-reply — avoid phrases like "after careful review of many strong applications" if the role has obvious minimum criteria the candidate did not meet.

**When to use it:** When you're clearing an inbox of 40-plus screened-out applicants on a Tuesday and need something better than the ATS default but can't write 40 individual emails.

**Pro tip:** If your ATS auto-sends a different rejection email at application stage, make sure this prompt's output does not duplicate that send. Check the candidate status in your system before triggering — double rejections are the fastest way to get a negative Glassdoor review from someone who was already handling a difficult job search.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email for a candidate who had a phone screen with a recruiter but will not proceed to a formal interview.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Phone screen conducted by: {recruiter_name}
> Duration of the call: {call_duration} (e.g. 20 minutes, 30 minutes)
> One genuine thing that came up positively in the call: {positive_from_call}
> Reason not progressing (internal): {internal_reason}
> Tone: {tone: warm / direct / brief}
>
> Write a 130 to 160 word rejection email from {recruiter_name}. Reference the call naturally in the first sentence without being stiff about it. Note {positive_from_call} in one honest sentence. Decline for the role clearly. Do not give the {internal_reason}. Close warmly but without hollow phrases like "we wish you every success".

**When to use it:** After a batch of recruiter phone screens when you have five to eight candidates to decline before moving the shortlist to the hiring manager.

**Pro tip:** If the {positive_from_call} you noted was actually something the candidate volunteered about a competitor's product or a personal situation, don't echo it back — it can feel surveillance-like. Only reference things they'd expect you to note professionally.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who applied for a role that has now been put on hold or cancelled, not due to anything wrong with their application.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role they applied for: {job_title}
> Reason the role is on hold (internal, do not state directly): {internal_reason} (e.g. headcount freeze, restructure, budget cut)
> Stage the candidate had reached: {application_stage} (e.g. applied, phone screened, first interview)
> Will the role likely reopen: {likely_to_reopen: yes / no / uncertain}
> Tone: {tone: transparent / professional}
>
> Write a 150 to 180 word email. Be honest that the role is on hold without explaining internal business reasons. Acknowledge their time at the {application_stage} stage. If {likely_to_reopen} is yes, give a realistic timeframe if you have one, and invite them to confirm if they want to be contacted when it does. Do not promise anything you cannot deliver. Do not apologise excessively.

**When to use it:** When a hiring freeze hits mid-pipeline and you have candidates who interviewed in good faith and deserve a real explanation, not a vague decline.

**Pro tip:** If {likely_to_reopen} is uncertain and you write "we will be in touch," set a calendar reminder to actually follow up — or remove that sentence. Candidates remember these promises, and a role that reopens six months later with a different team silently hired is a trust-destroying sequence.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who applied for an internal promotion or transfer but was not selected.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Their current role: {current_role}
> Role they applied for internally: {applied_for_role}
> Decision maker: {hiring_manager_name}
> Reason not selected (internal): {internal_reason} (e.g. another internal candidate had more relevant project experience, external hire selected)
> Next steps for this employee's development: {development_next_steps} (e.g. quarterly check-in with manager, leadership programme in Q3)
> Tone: {tone: warm / direct / developmental}
>
> Write a 180 to 220 word internal rejection email. Acknowledge the courage it takes to put yourself forward for an internal role. Decline clearly and without excessive softening. Reference {development_next_steps} as a concrete next step, not a consolation prize. Do not compare them to the successful candidate. Do not imply the decision was close if it was not.

**When to use it:** When an employee applied for a promotion and you need the written communication to hold up alongside the verbal conversation the hiring manager is having with them on the same day.

**Pro tip:** Send this email on the same day as the verbal conversation — never before it. An employee reading a rejection email before their manager has spoken to them is a serious relationship breakdown waiting to happen. Sequence the conversation first, confirm the email is following as a written record.

> You are an HR Manager writing a batch rejection email for candidates who submitted speculative applications or reached out directly about roles that do not currently exist.
>
> Company name: {company_name}
> Roles the candidate expressed interest in: {expressed_interest} (e.g. marketing roles, data analyst positions)
> Whether there are live roles in that area now: {live_roles_now: yes / no}
> Whether the company has a talent pool or careers page candidates can follow: {talent_pool_available: yes / no}
> Talent pool or careers page URL: {careers_page_url}
> Tone: {tone: professional / friendly}
>
> Write a 120 to 150 word response to a speculative application. Acknowledge their interest in {expressed_interest} roles. Be honest about whether there are {live_roles_now}. If {talent_pool_available} is yes, include {careers_page_url} and a specific invitation to register. Do not make vague promises about future roles. Close in a way that leaves a good impression without over-committing.

**When to use it:** When your recruiter inbox fills up after a press mention, award announcement, or LinkedIn post goes semi-viral and you have 20-plus speculative applications to respond to.

**Pro tip:** If {careers_page_url} is a broken link or has not been updated in six months, fix it before including it. Sending candidates to a stale page with last year's roles is worse than sending no link at all.
