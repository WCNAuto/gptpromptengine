## Post-Phone Screen Rejections

> You are an HR manager sending a rejection email after a phone screen.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role applied for: {job_title}
> Team or department: {department}
> Reason for rejection (internal only, do not repeat verbatim): {internal_reason — e.g. "salary expectation mismatch", "insufficient experience in X"}
> Tone: {warm / professional / brief}
> Open to future roles: {yes / no}
>
> Write a 120 to 150 word rejection email. Open by thanking them for their time on the call. State clearly but kindly that we will not be moving forward. Do not give specific feedback — this is a post-phone-screen rejection and detail creates legal risk. If {open_to_future_roles} is yes, include one sentence inviting them to apply again. Close with a genuine good-luck line. Sign off from HR, not a named individual.

**When to use it:** Monday morning when you have a stack of Friday phone screens to clear before the hiring manager asks for a shortlist update.

**Pro tip:** Do not paste {internal_reason} into the final email as-is. It is a context variable for the AI only. If your output accidentally quotes it back, delete that line before sending — "salary mismatch" in a rejection email opens a discrimination conversation you do not want.

> You are an HR manager sending a same-day rejection to a candidate who completed a phone screen this morning.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Specific skill gap identified on the call: {skill_gap — e.g. "no experience with enterprise CRM platforms"}
> Competing candidate strength that made this decision easy: {winning_differentiator — internal context only}
> Tone: {direct / empathetic}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 100 to 130 word rejection email sent within hours of the call. Acknowledge the speed of the decision and frame it as a courtesy to the candidate so they can keep looking. Give one sentence of honest, non-legally-risky feedback tied to {skill_gap} — phrase it as "we need someone who already has X" rather than "you lack X". Do not mention {winning_differentiator}. End with a wish for their search.

**When to use it:** When you know immediately after a call that a candidate is not right and want to avoid leaving them waiting over a weekend.

**Pro tip:** "We need someone who already has X" is legally safer than "you don't have X" because it frames the gap as a role requirement, not a personal deficiency. It also lands better with candidates and reduces the chance they reply asking for a detailed explanation.

> You are an HR manager writing a bulk rejection email for candidates who applied but were not selected for a phone screen.
>
> Role: {job_title}
> Department: {department}
> Volume of applicants this round: {applicant_volume — e.g. "over 200"}
> Time from application to this email: {days_since_application — e.g. "14 days"}
> Tone: {professional / warm}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 90 to 110 word rejection email suitable for sending to all applicants who did not progress past CV review. Do not imply a personalised review took place if {applicant_volume} is high. Thank them for applying. State that we had an exceptional volume of strong applicants and have progressed a shortlist. Wish them well. Keep it clean and final — no invitation to reapply unless instructed.

**When to use it:** After closing a high-volume role where 150+ people applied and you need to clear the ATS queue in one send.

**Pro tip:** If your ATS has a merge field for {job_title}, drop it into the subject line ("Your application for {job_title} at {company_name}") before sending. A personalised subject line reduces the chance the email reads as spam and increases the likelihood candidates actually see the closure — which cuts down on follow-up enquiries.

> You are an HR manager rejecting a candidate who passed the CV screen but gave a poor phone screen performance on culture-fit questions.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> One specific moment from the call that indicated misalignment (internal context only): {misalignment_note}
> Company values relevant to this role: {two_company_values}
> Tone: {honest / diplomatic}
> Hiring manager name (for context only): {hiring_manager_name}
>
> Write a 130 to 160 word rejection email. Do not reference culture fit directly — that phrase is legally ambiguous. Instead, frame the decision around the specific requirements of the role and team environment. Do not mention {misalignment_note} or {hiring_manager_name} in the output. Give the candidate a clear final answer. Keep the tone respectful — a rejected candidate often refers others.

**When to use it:** When the real reason is "they weren't a fit" but you need to communicate that in a way that won't get screenshotted and posted on Glassdoor.

**Pro tip:** Avoid the phrase "cultural fit" entirely in rejection emails. Employment lawyers flag it as a potential proxy for discriminatory reasoning in unfair dismissal and bias complaints. "Requirements of the role" and "working style of the team" carry the same meaning with significantly less legal exposure.

> You are an HR manager sending a rejection to a candidate who was referred by an existing employee but did not pass the phone screen.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Referring employee name: {referrer_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Reason referral didn't progress (internal only): {internal_reason}
> Tone: {warm / careful}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 140 to 170 word rejection email. Acknowledge the referral with genuine appreciation — the referring employee took a reputational risk. State clearly that we are not progressing {candidate_first_name} at this time. Do not give detail that could get back to {referrer_name} and create an awkward conversation. Do not suggest the rejection reflects on the referrer. Invite the candidate to apply for future roles if appropriate. Close warmly.

**When to use it:** When a colleague put their name behind someone who didn't make it through screening and you need the rejection email to protect that internal relationship.

**Pro tip:** Send this email before the referrer hears the outcome from the candidate. If the referring employee finds out informally before receiving any communication from you, they feel blindsided. A 10-second heads-up Slack to the referrer ("Just a note — I've sent a kind decline to your referral, they didn't quite match the brief this time") prevents a lot of awkwardness.

## Final-Round Rejection Emails

> You are an HR manager sending a rejection to a candidate who reached the final interview round but was not selected.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Number of interview stages they completed: {stages_completed — e.g. "3 rounds over 4 weeks"}
> One genuine strength observed during the process: {candidate_strength}
> Reason for non-selection (internal only): {internal_reason}
> Tone: {warm / honest}
> Open to staying in talent pool: {yes / no}
>
> Write a 200 to 250 word rejection email. Open by acknowledging the time and effort they invested across {stages_completed}. Name {candidate_strength} explicitly — they earned that feedback. State clearly that we are moving forward with another candidate. Do not apologise excessively. If {open_to_staying_in_talent_pool} is yes, include a specific sentence about keeping their profile on file for relevant future roles and what that means in practice. Close with a genuine recommendation to keep in touch.

**When to use it:** When a finalist has spent a month going through your process and a form letter would damage your employer brand as well as their experience.

**Pro tip:** Naming one real strength ("Your approach to the case study presentation stood out clearly") does more for your employer brand than four sentences of generic praise. Candidates who get this kind of email often post about it positively. Candidates who get a form letter after four rounds do not.

> You are an HR manager calling a final-round candidate to reject them by phone and following up with a written email summary.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Verbal call completed: {yes / no}
> One strength to acknowledge: {candidate_strength}
> One honest development area they can act on: {development_area — keep legally safe and forward-looking}
> Tone: {direct / compassionate}
>
> Write a 180 to 220 word follow-up email to send within one hour of the rejection call. Open with "As discussed on our call" so it does not blindside anyone who missed the call. Confirm the decision in writing. Reference {candidate_strength}. Include {development_area} framed as "something that may help in future applications" rather than a personal criticism. Invite them to reach out if they have questions. Do not promise a call back if you cannot commit to one.

**When to use it:** After you have called a finalist to give the news verbally — this email creates a paper trail, gives them something to refer back to, and closes the loop professionally.

**Pro tip:** If the call went to voicemail, send this email immediately and change "As discussed on our call" to "I attempted to reach you by phone today." Do not leave a detailed rejection on voicemail — candidates have shared those recordings publicly, and the tone rarely lands as intended when heard back.

> You are an HR manager sending a final-round rejection where the decision came down to two equally strong candidates.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Deciding factor (internal context only): {deciding_factor — e.g. "other candidate had direct sector experience"}
> Duration of process: {process_duration}
> Genuine thing that impressed the panel: {panel_observation}
> Tone: {honest / warm}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 200 to 230 word rejection email. Acknowledge that this was a genuinely close decision without being patronising about it. Name {panel_observation} specifically. Do not reveal {deciding_factor}. Be clear the role is filled. Express that the panel was impressed and that this reflects well on the candidate's calibre. Include an offer to be considered for future senior openings if that is a real commitment — do not include it otherwise.

**When to use it:** When a very strong candidate narrowly missed out and you want to keep the door genuinely open rather than just saying so.

**Pro tip:** "It was a really tough decision" reads as hollow to a candidate who has heard it before. Replace it with a specific statement from the panel: "The hiring team commented specifically on your approach to X." One concrete detail transforms a form phrase into something that actually lands.

> You are an HR manager rejecting a final-round candidate who accepted a counter-offer from their current employer mid-process, then reapplied.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Previous round they withdrew from: {previous_round — e.g. "offer stage, 6 months ago"}
> Current round they reached: {current_round}
> Tone: {professional / neutral}
> Hiring manager's view on reapplication (internal only): {hm_view}
>
> Write a 150 to 180 word rejection email that is neutral and final. Do not reference the previous withdrawal — that opens a discussion you do not need. Do not punish them in tone for the counter-offer situation. Simply close this application professionally. State that we are moving forward with another candidate. Do not invite future applications unless {hm_view} explicitly supports it.

**When to use it:** When a candidate who ghosted or withdrew previously reapplied and made it far enough that they need a proper rejection rather than an ATS auto-response.

**Pro tip:** Check your ATS notes before writing this email. If the previous withdrawal was acrimonious or the hiring manager has flagged the candidate as "do not progress," escalate before sending anything — even a neutral email can look like re-engagement if the candidate was flagged for conduct reasons.

> You are an HR manager writing a rejection email for a final-round candidate who was very strong but applied for a role that was cancelled after the process began.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role they applied for: {job_title}
> Reason role was cancelled (internal only): {cancellation_reason — e.g. "headcount freeze"}
> Stages completed by candidate: {stages_completed}
> Similar role likely to open: {yes / no / uncertain}
> Tone: {transparent / apologetic / professional}
>
> Write a 180 to 210 word email notifying the candidate that the role has been cancelled and that this is not a reflection of their application or performance. Be honest that the decision was internal. Acknowledge the time they invested across {stages_completed}. If {similar_role_likely_to_open} is yes, make a specific commitment — not a vague "we'll keep you in mind" — about what the follow-up will look like and when. If no or uncertain, do not make a commitment you cannot keep.

**When to use it:** When a reorg, budget cut, or leadership change kills a role after candidates have already done two or three rounds of interviews.

**Pro tip:** "We'll keep you in mind" without a concrete action is worse than nothing to a candidate who just spent four weeks on your process. If the role is genuinely likely to reopen, name a timeframe and a contact person. If it is not, say so plainly — candidates respect honesty far more than non-committal language that makes them wait.

## Internal Candidate Rejections

> You are an HR manager writing a rejection email to an internal employee who applied for a promotion or role change and was not selected.
>
> Employee name: {employee_first_name}
> Current role: {current_role}
> Role they applied for: {target_role}
> Manager informed before this email: {yes / no}
> One genuine strength they showed in the process: {process_strength}
> Development area that influenced the decision: {development_area}
> Tone: {direct / supportive}
>
> Write a 200 to 240 word internal rejection email. Acknowledge their application and the courage it takes to apply internally. Name {process_strength} specifically. Be honest about {development_area} without being harsh — this person still works for the company. Confirm the decision is final. Include a clear invitation for a follow-up conversation to discuss development next steps. Do not promise the next promotion will be theirs — that creates expectation you may not be able to meet.

**When to use it:** Before or after the in-person conversation with an internal candidate who will see you in the office tomorrow.

**Pro tip:** Send this email after the in-person conversation, not before. An internal employee should never read a rejection for the first time in their inbox. The email confirms what was said in the conversation and gives them something to refer back to when thinking about next steps — it is a record, not a surprise.
