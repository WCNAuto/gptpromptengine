## Post-Application Rejection Emails (No Interview)

### Prompt 1 — High-volume screening rejection for an unqualified applicant

> You are an HR manager sending a rejection email to a candidate who applied but did not meet the minimum requirements for the role. You did not interview them.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role applied for: {job_title}
> Department: {department}
> Reason for rejection: {missing_requirement — e.g. "no degree in civil engineering", "fewer than 3 years relevant experience", "no right to work in the US"}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Tone: {warm / neutral / formal}
>
> Write a 100 to 130 word rejection email. Open by thanking them for applying. In one sentence, note the role was highly competitive. Do not repeat the specific missing requirement back to them. Close by encouraging them to watch for future openings. No hollow platitudes like "we wish you all the best in your future endeavours." Sign off with "The {company_name} Talent Team."

**When to use it:** Monday morning when you're clearing the weekend application pile and need 20 rejections sent before 10am.

**Pro tip:** If your ATS auto-populates a candidate's first name in the subject line, change the `{candidate_name}` variable to first name only and remove the "Dear" prefix so you don't end up with "Dear Jane Jane."

### Prompt 2 — Overqualified candidate rejection after resume review

> You are an HR manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who is clearly overqualified for the advertised role, based on their resume. You have not spoken with them.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role applied for: {job_title}
> Their apparent seniority: {seniority_level — e.g. "former VP of Sales", "10+ years at director level"}
> Your company stage: {company_stage — e.g. "50-person startup", "enterprise, 4,000 staff"}
> Hiring manager name: {hiring_manager_name}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 120 to 150 word rejection email. Acknowledge the strength of their background without being sycophantic. Explain honestly that the role scope likely wouldn't match their experience level. Avoid implying they should have applied for a senior role you don't currently have open. Offer no false hope. Close with a single sincere sentence. Do not use the phrase "we were blown away."

**When to use it:** When a $250k executive applies for a $65k coordinator role and you want to close politely without the awkwardness of pretending it's a close call.

**Pro tip:** Don't reference a specific senior role you might have "in the future" unless one is actively being scoped. Candidates have long memories and will follow up in three weeks citing your email.

### Prompt 3 — Duplicate applicant rejection (applied to same role twice)

> You are an HR manager writing to a candidate who has submitted duplicate applications for the same open role within a short time window.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Number of applications submitted: {application_count}
> Time between applications: {time_gap — e.g. "4 days", "2 weeks"}
> Whether their application is still under active review: {still_reviewing — yes / no}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 90 to 110 word email. Confirm you received both applications and will consider them as a single submission. If still_reviewing is "yes," say the process is ongoing and they'll hear by {decision_timeline}. If "no," deliver the rejection calmly. Keep the tone neutral. Do not embarrass them or suggest the duplication was inappropriate.

**When to use it:** When your ATS flags a candidate who applied twice and you need to close the loop without making them feel called out.

**Pro tip:** Check whether your ATS merged or duplicated the record before sending. If their profile was duplicated internally, you may have inadvertently advanced one version and rejected another — fix that before this email goes out.

### Prompt 4 — Rejection for a candidate referred by an employee (pre-interview)

> You are an HR manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who was referred by a current employee but did not advance past the application screening stage.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Referring employee name: {referrer_name}
> Reason for non-advancement (internal only, do not include in email): {internal_reason}
> Whether the referrer knows yet: {referrer_informed — yes / no}
> Tone: {warm / professional}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 130 to 160 word rejection email. Acknowledge the referral warmly without over-emphasising it. Explain the role requirements and competitive volume without disclosing the internal reason. Do not make the candidate feel their referral was meaningless. Close by thanking them for their interest in {company_name}. Do not ask them to keep watching the careers page unless you genuinely have relevant roles coming up.

**When to use it:** Before the referring employee hears about it through the grapevine — ideally send this the same day you inform the referrer.

**Pro tip:** If `{referrer_informed}` is "no," send the internal note to the referring employee first. Getting this order wrong creates awkward conversations that land back on your desk.

### Prompt 5 — Mass rejection for applicants after a role is cancelled mid-hire

> You are an HR manager writing to candidates who applied for a role that has since been cancelled due to a business decision. No interviews were conducted.
>
> Role title: {job_title}
> Department: {department}
> Number of applicants affected: {applicant_count}
> Reason the role was cancelled (brief, shareable version): {cancellation_reason — e.g. "organisational restructure", "budget reallocation", "role scope has changed significantly"}
> Whether the role may reopen: {may_reopen — yes / no / unclear}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 110 to 140 word rejection email suitable for sending to all {applicant_count} applicants with only the name personalised. Be transparent that the role was withdrawn, not that their application was unsuccessful — this distinction matters. If may_reopen is "yes," say so briefly and directly. Maintain a respectful, human tone. Avoid corporate-speak like "we have made the difficult decision."

**When to use it:** The week after a hiring freeze or reorg hits a role that had 80 applicants and a full pipeline.

**Pro tip:** If even one candidate has already completed a first interview, do not send them this batch email. They need a personal call or individual email first — receiving a mass notification after an interview is a significant employer brand risk.

## Post-First-Interview Rejection Emails

### Prompt 6 — Rejection after a phone screen (candidate was not a fit)

> You are an HR manager writing a post-phone-screen rejection email to a candidate who did not move forward after a 20 to 30 minute initial screening call.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Interviewer name: {interviewer_name}
> Core reason for non-advancement (internal, do not quote directly): {rejection_reason}
> Whether to offer feedback: {offer_feedback — yes / no}
> Candidate's current employment status (to calibrate urgency): {employment_status — employed / actively job-seeking / recently made redundant}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 140 to 170 word email. Thank them for their time with the interviewer. Deliver the rejection clearly in the first two sentences — do not bury it. If offer_feedback is "yes," include one specific, constructive sentence of feedback (not a list). If "no," keep it respectful but brief. Acknowledge employment_status only if "recently made redundant" — in that case add one genuine sentence of acknowledgement. No empty encouragement.

**When to use it:** Within 24 hours of the screen, while the conversation is still fresh and the candidate hasn't started following up.

**Pro tip:** If `{rejection_reason}` is something legally sensitive — compensation mismatch revealed in the call, a protected characteristic mentioned in passing — do not include any version of that reason in the email. Send the no-feedback variant and document the real reason internally only.

### Prompt 7 — Rejection after a first-round panel interview (strong candidate, role went another direction)

> You are an HR manager writing a rejection email to a strong candidate who completed a first-round panel interview but was not selected because the role requirements shifted or a stronger match was found.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Interview panel members: {panel_names}
> Specific strength observed during interview: {candidate_strength — e.g. "strong stakeholder communication", "depth of technical knowledge in X"}
> Reason for non-selection (internal framing): {non_selection_reason — e.g. "another candidate had direct industry experience", "scope of role narrowed post-interview"}
> Whether to keep them in the pipeline for future roles: {pipeline — yes / no}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 180 to 220 word rejection email. Deliver the news directly in paragraph one. Cite the specific strength in paragraph two — one sentence, genuine, not generic. Explain the reason for non-selection honestly but without over-explaining. If pipeline is "yes," extend that invitation with one concrete next step (e.g. "I'll add your details to our talent pool and reach out if a relevant role opens in the next six months"). Do not use the phrase "we were impressed."

**When to use it:** When closing a genuinely strong silver medallist you'd actually rehire in a different context.

**Pro tip:** Only set `{pipeline}` to "yes" if someone will actually own that follow-through. An empty pipeline promise damages trust more than a clean rejection.

### Prompt 8 — Rejection after a technical assessment or skills test

> You are an HR manager sending a rejection email to a candidate who completed an unpaid technical or skills assessment as part of the recruitment process and did not advance.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Assessment type: {assessment_type — e.g. "two-hour coding test", "45-minute data analysis task", "writing sample"}
> Time investment required: {time_investment — e.g. "approximately 2 hours"}
> Whether feedback on the assessment is available: {feedback_available — yes / no}
> Hiring manager name: {hiring_manager_name}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 150 to 190 word rejection email. Open by explicitly acknowledging the time they invested in the assessment — this is not optional. Reject clearly in paragraph one. If feedback_available is "yes," offer one or two specific points from the assessment (not generic advice). If "no," acknowledge the limitation honestly: "We aren't in a position to give detailed feedback on every submission." Do not use "unfortunately" as your opening word.

**When to use it:** Any time you've asked candidates to complete unpaid work as part of screening — sending a form rejection after a two-hour task is a serious employer brand problem.

**Pro tip:** If your company uses automated scoring for assessments and you're rejecting someone based solely on that score — and you haven't actually reviewed their work — do not write as though a human evaluated it. Candidates sometimes ask follow-up questions and you need your email to be accurate.

### Prompt 9 — Rejection after a video interview (async, no live conversation)

> You are an HR manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who completed an asynchronous video interview through a platform like HireVue or Spark Hire and did not advance.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Platform used: {video_platform — e.g. "HireVue", "Spark Hire", "Willo"}
> Questions they answered (brief summary): {interview_focus — e.g. "three questions on client-facing communication and conflict resolution"}
> Reason for non-advancement (internal only): {rejection_reason}
> Tone: {empathetic / professional / brief}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 130 to 160 word rejection email. Acknowledge that async video interviews require effort with no live feedback loop — one sentence is enough. Deliver the rejection in the first paragraph. Do not offer vague feedback. Close with something that respects their time without being patronising. Avoid phrases like "this was a tough decision" unless that is genuinely true.

**When to use it:** When closing async video review rounds — typically after the hiring manager has reviewed recordings and you're eliminating 60 to 70% of that stage.

**Pro tip:** Some async platforms display "viewed" status to candidates. If you reviewed their video three days ago and are only emailing now, don't write "we recently reviewed your submission" — they can see the timestamp.

### Prompt 10 — Rejection for a candidate who was late or no-showed to a first interview (still rejecting them)

> You are an HR manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who either arrived significantly late to or no-showed a scheduled first interview, and after consideration, you are declining to reschedule.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Interview format: {interview_format — e.g. "in-person at our offices", "Zoom call"}
> What happened: {incident — e.g. "did not attend and contacted us 4 hours later", "arrived 35 minutes late with no prior notice"}
> Whether they gave a reason: {reason_given — yes, specify / no}
> Tone: {professional / neutral}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 100 to 130 word rejection email. Acknowledge the scheduled interview factually. Do not lecture them or editoralise about professionalism. State clearly that you will not be moving forward. If reason_given includes a genuinely extenuating circumstance (e.g. medical emergency), soften the tone slightly but maintain the decision. Do not leave the door open unless you mean it.

**When to use it:** When the hiring manager has made the call not to reschedule and you need to close the candidate out cleanly and without drama.

**Pro tip:** Keep this email factual and short. A longer email signals either justification or irritation — neither serves you. If the candidate disputes the rejection later, a brief factual record is easier to stand behind.
