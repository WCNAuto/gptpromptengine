## After the Phone Screen: Rejections Before the First Interview

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who completed a phone screen but will not move forward to the interview stage.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role they applied for: {job_title}
> Reason for rejection (internal only, used to shape tone): {rejection_reason: e.g. salary expectations too high / lacks required certification / experience level too junior}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Hiring manager or HR contact name: {sender_name}
> Tone: {warm_but_clear / formal / brief}
>
> Write a 120 to 150 word rejection email. Do not state the internal rejection reason directly. Open by thanking the candidate for their time on the call. State clearly they will not be moving forward. Offer one genuine, non-generic encouragement line relevant to the role type. Close with an invitation to apply for future roles if appropriate. No bullet points. Plain paragraphs. Sign off from {sender_name}.

**When to use it:** Monday morning when you have a stack of phone screen follow-ups to clear before the week's interview panel begins.

**Pro tip:** If salary mismatch is the real reason, avoid any language like "we found a stronger fit"—candidates who were rejected for budget reasons sometimes reapply or refer others, and vague language breeds resentment. Keep it to "we're not able to move forward at this stage" and leave it there.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who applied online and was screened out at the CV review stage, before any direct contact.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Department: {department}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Time between application and rejection: {days_since_application} days
> Tone: {professional / brief / empathetic}
>
> Write a 90 to 120 word rejection email. Acknowledge their application by name and role. Confirm they will not be progressing. Do not invent feedback you don't have—no fabricated reasons. If {days_since_application} is over 21, include a brief apology for the delay. Close by wishing them well. No fluff. No "we were blown away by your credentials." Plain, respectful language. Sign off with {company_name} Talent Team.

**When to use it:** When clearing a backlog of applicants who never had a conversation with your team and you need volume without sounding robotic.

**Pro tip:** If your ATS auto-sent a receipt email when they applied, reference it indirectly by confirming which role this is about—candidates who've applied to multiple companies in a short period often can't place your company by name alone.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email after a candidate completed an online skills assessment but scored below the threshold to progress.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Assessment name or type: {assessment_type: e.g. Excel skills test / situational judgement test / coding challenge}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Can you share any feedback on the assessment? {yes / no}
> If yes, one line of honest feedback: {assessment_feedback}
> Sender name: {sender_name}
>
> Write a 150 to 180 word rejection email. Thank the candidate for completing the assessment. State clearly they will not be progressing to interview. If {yes}, include {assessment_feedback} framed constructively—one sentence, no grade or score. If {no}, do not fabricate feedback; instead acknowledge the assessment was competitive. Close with encouragement and an invitation to reapply in future cycles if the role recurs. Sign off from {sender_name} at {company_name}.

**When to use it:** After automated assessment scoring is returned and you need to notify non-passing candidates before the interview invites go out the same day.

**Pro tip:** Never include a numerical score in a rejection email even if you have it. Candidates sometimes challenge scores and you'll create unnecessary back-and-forth. Stick to qualitative language.

> You are an HR Manager writing a same-day rejection email to a candidate who was a strong CV match but whose references came back with concerns before the interview was scheduled.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Stage at rejection: pre-interview, post-reference check
> Company name: {company_name}
> Can you disclose the reference issue? {yes / no}
> Sender name: {sender_name}
> Tone: {careful / formal / brief}
>
> Write a 100 to 130 word rejection email. Do not assign blame or mention the reference provider. If {yes}, state in one careful sentence that a reference check raised concerns that prevent progression. If {no}, use neutral language: "following our pre-screening process we are not able to move forward." No false praise. No invitation to reapply unless {sender_name} has specifically decided this is appropriate. Sign off formally from {sender_name}.

**When to use it:** A situation most HR Managers face quarterly—a candidate who looks excellent on paper but whose background check or references introduce a flag you can't ignore or explain in detail.

**Pro tip:** Run this email past your legal or HR compliance team before sending if the reference concern touches on anything protected (health, criminal history, or safeguarding). A 10-minute check now prevents a grievance later.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who completed a video screening interview through an asynchronous platform and was not selected to progress.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Platform used: {platform_name: e.g. HireVue / Spark Hire / Willo}
> Reason for non-progression (internal): {internal_reason: e.g. communication clarity / insufficient industry experience / cultural fit concerns}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Sender name: {sender_name}
> Tone: {professional / warm / neutral}
>
> Write a 130 to 160 word rejection email. Thank the candidate for completing the video interview and acknowledge it takes effort to record asynchronously. State they will not progress to the live interview stage. Do not reference {internal_reason} directly. Include one genuine, non-patronising line about continuing their search. Close with a clear sign-off from {sender_name} at {company_name}. No bullet points.

**When to use it:** After your panel reviews a batch of async video responses and you need to clear non-progressing candidates before scheduling live interviews with the shortlist.

**Pro tip:** If your platform sends automated "your video has been reviewed" notifications, candidates may have already seen that status change before your email arrives. Open with acknowledgment of that—"you may have seen your application status has updated"—to avoid the email feeling out of sync.

## After the First or Second Interview: Mid-Process Rejections

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate after a first-round panel interview. They interviewed well but another candidate is being progressed.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Interview date: {interview_date}
> Specific strength observed in the interview: {observed_strength: e.g. strong stakeholder management examples / clear analytical thinking}
> Reason they are not progressing (internal): {internal_reason}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Sender name: {sender_name}
>
> Write a 180 to 220 word rejection email. Open by thanking them for attending on {interview_date} and naming {observed_strength} genuinely—one sentence, not a paragraph. State clearly they will not be progressing to the next round. Do not over-explain or list reasons. Acknowledge the disappointment without being condescending. If appropriate for the role level, include one sentence inviting them to stay in touch for future roles. Close warmly from {sender_name} at {company_name}.

**When to use it:** The day after a first-round panel when you have a shortlist confirmed and need to notify the candidates who didn't make it before they follow up themselves.

**Pro tip:** Use the {observed_strength} variable carefully. If you mention a specific skill they showed and then they apply again six months later, you're implicitly committed to that being a genuine positive. Only name strengths you actually observed—not generic softeners.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate after a second-round interview who came very close to being selected but the offer went to another candidate.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Number of interview rounds completed: {rounds_completed}
> Time they invested in the process: {time_invested: e.g. three interviews over six weeks / two half-day assessments}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Hiring manager name: {hiring_manager_name}
> Sender name: {sender_name}
>
> Write a 200 to 250 word rejection email. Acknowledge explicitly that {time_invested} is significant and thank them for it. State the offer went to another candidate—do not use vague language like "we've decided to go in a different direction." Express genuine regret proportionate to how far they got. If the hiring team discussed keeping them in mind for future openings, say so once, briefly and only if true. Close from {sender_name} with {hiring_manager_name} copied or mentioned as appropriate. No bullet points.

**When to use it:** When your final-stage candidate doesn't get the offer and you need to send a rejection that reflects how much of their time this process took.

**Pro tip:** "We went with another candidate" lands better than "we found someone whose experience more closely matched our needs"—the second phrasing invites a rebuttal. Keep it factual and short on explanation.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email after a candidate completed a full-day assessment centre and was not selected for the role.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Assessment centre date: {assessment_date}
> Activities completed: {activities_completed: e.g. group exercise, written case study, competency interview}
> Feedback available: {yes / no}
> If yes, one key development area: {development_area}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Sender name: {sender_name}
>
> Write a 220 to 280 word rejection email. Open by acknowledging the full day they gave on {assessment_date} and naming {activities_completed}. State clearly they have not been selected. If {yes}, include {development_area} framed as a forward-looking development point, not a criticism of their performance on the day. If {no}, do not invent feedback. Offer to provide a brief feedback call if your policy allows—one sentence only. Close from {sender_name} at {company_name}.

**When to use it:** After a graduate scheme or management trainee assessment centre when candidates have invested a full day and expect more than a one-line rejection.

**Pro tip:** If you offer a feedback call, only do it if you have the capacity to follow through within five working days. An offered call that never materialises damages your employer brand more than no offer at all.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate after a technical interview where it became clear they lacked the depth of technical knowledge required for the role.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Technical area where gap was identified: {technical_gap: e.g. Python data structures / AWS infrastructure / SQL query optimisation}
> Interview date: {interview_date}
> Seniority level of role: {seniority_level: e.g. mid-level / senior / lead}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Sender name: {sender_name}
>
> Write a 150 to 190 word rejection email. Thank the candidate for their time on {interview_date}. State they will not be progressing. Include one honest, non-embarrassing reference to the {technical_gap} area framed as "the role requires deep expertise in {technical_gap} and on this occasion we need someone who is already operating at {seniority_level} depth in this area." Do not suggest they are incompetent—frame it as a match issue, not a capability failure. Close from {sender_name}.

**When to use it:** When a candidate was honest in their technical interview and you want to give them a clear signal about the gap without making them feel humiliated.

**Pro tip:** If the candidate disclosed in interview that they were self-taught in {technical_gap}, don't reference that in the email—it can read as dismissive of self-taught learning even if that's not your intent.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email after a final-round interview where the candidate's salary expectation came in significantly above budget after the offer stage was reached.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Stage reached: final interview / verbal offer conversation
> Salary expectation vs budget gap: {gap_description: e.g. candidate expects £75k, budget is capped at £58k}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Sender name: {sender_name}
> Leave door open for future: {yes / no}
>
> Write a 140 to 170 word rejection email. Acknowledge they reached the final stage and thank them for their time throughout the process. State clearly that following offer discussions, you are not able to proceed due to a mismatch in compensation expectations—be direct, not evasive. Do not imply the candidate's expectations are unreasonable. If {yes}, include one sentence leaving the door open. If {no}, close cleanly. Sign off from {sender_name} at {company_name}.

**When to use it:** When a late-stage salary conversation kills the process and you need to close the loop before they chase for an update.

**Pro tip:** Do not include the specific numbers ({gap_description}) in the email—that's for your notes only. Naming figures in a rejection email creates a paper trail that can complicate future negotiations if the candidate reapplies when the budget changes.

## Rejecting Internal Candidates and Referrals

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to an internal employee who applied for an internal promotion or transfer and was not selected after a formal interview process.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Current role: {current_role}
> Role they applied for: {applied_role}
> Interview date: {interview_date}
> Their line manager aware: {yes / no}
> One genuine development area to surface: {development_area}
> Sender name: {sender_name}
> Follow-up conversation planned: {yes / no}
>
> Write a 200 to 240 word rejection email. Acknowledge the courage it takes to apply internally. State clearly they were not selected for {applied_role}. Include {development_area} framed as an investment in their continued growth at the company, not a reason for rejection. If {follow-up conversation planned} is {yes}, confirm the meeting date or time in the email. If their line manager is not yet aware ({no}), do not reference anything the employee hasn't been told yet. Close warmly. Sign off from {sender_name}.

**When to use it:** After an internal promotion panel when you need to notify the unsuccessful internal candidate before they hear through the grapevine that someone else got the role.

**Pro tip:** Never send this email before you've confirmed the successful candidate has accepted. Internal candidates talk. If the offer falls through after this rejection has been sent, you'll have a retention problem
