## Post-Application Screen Rejections

> You are an HR Manager sending a rejection email to a candidate who applied for a role but did not pass the initial resume screen.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role applied for: {job_title}
> Department: {department}
> Reason for screen failure (internal, do not quote directly): {screen_failure_reason — e.g. missing required qualification, location mismatch, salary expectation gap}
> Company tone: {warm_and_human / professional_and_brief / formal}
> Open to future roles: {yes / no}
>
> Write a 120 to 150 word rejection email. Address the candidate by first name. Acknowledge their application specifically by role. Do not mention the internal reason for rejection. If open to future roles is yes, include one sentence inviting them to watch the careers page. Close with a genuine wish for their search. Do not use the phrase "we regret to inform you" or "unfortunately at this time."

**When to use it:** Monday morning when you're clearing the weekend's application stack before the 9am hiring manager call.

**Pro tip:** If your ATS exports candidate names in bulk, you can chain this prompt with a mail merge by generating 10 personalised versions in one ChatGPT session — paste all 10 names and roles into a numbered list above the prompt variables and ask for numbered outputs.

> You are an HR Manager sending a high-volume batch of post-screen rejection emails for an entry-level role that received over 200 applications.
>
> Role: {job_title}
> Department: {department}
> Volume of rejections to send: {number_of_candidates}
> One genuine thing about the role or company to reference: {role_or_company_detail — e.g. "we received applications from 14 countries"}
> Tone: {encouraging / neutral / brief}
> Response time since application: {days_since_application — e.g. 12 days}
>
> Write a single 100 to 120 word rejection email suitable for all candidates who applied but were not screened through. The email should feel considered, not automated. Acknowledge the response volume without making the candidate feel like a number. Do not promise feedback. Do not use "fit" or "culture fit." Close with two words of genuine encouragement for their search.

**When to use it:** When a junior role blew up on LinkedIn and you have 180 rejection emails to send before the week ends.

**Pro tip:** Avoid referencing specific timelines like "we reviewed hundreds of applications in three days" — it can read as dismissive rather than impressive. Let the candidate infer the volume from a single factual line.

> You are an HR Manager rejecting a candidate who applied for a role that has been put on hold or cancelled before interviews began.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role applied for: {job_title}
> Reason role is on hold (internal only): {internal_reason — e.g. headcount freeze, reorg, budget cut}
> What you can share publicly: {public_reason — e.g. "we're reviewing our team structure" / "the role is currently on hold"}
> Reopen likelihood: {likely_within_6_months / uncertain / unlikely}
> Tone: {transparent_and_warm / professional}
>
> Write a 130 to 160 word email informing the candidate their application cannot proceed because the role is on hold. Use the public reason only. If reopen likelihood is likely_within_6_months, invite them to reapply when the role re-posts. If uncertain or unlikely, wish them well without over-promising. Do not apologise more than once. Do not use "unforeseen circumstances."

**When to use it:** When a hiring freeze hits mid-recruitment and you have candidates sitting in the pipeline who deserve a real explanation.

**Pro tip:** If the freeze is company-wide and public knowledge (e.g. covered in tech press), you can reference it briefly — candidates often already know and a vague "role on hold" email reads as evasive.

> You are an HR Manager sending a rejection email to a candidate who applied but whose salary expectations were significantly above the approved band for the role.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Candidate's stated salary expectation: {candidate_salary_figure}
> Approved band for the role: {approved_salary_band}
> Whether you want to keep the door open at a lower level: {yes / no}
> Tone: {direct / warm}
>
> Write a 120 to 140 word rejection email. State that compensation expectations and the approved range for this role are not aligned. Do not ask the candidate to lower their expectations. Do not reveal the full band unless yes is selected for keeping the door open. If yes, briefly mention whether there is a lower-level equivalent role and invite a conversation. If no, close cleanly without false hope.

**When to use it:** After a recruiter screen surfaces a candidate whose TC expectation is 40% above band — and you want to close the loop cleanly rather than ghost them.

**Pro tip:** Never state the band ceiling in a rejection email unless you're actively inviting negotiation. Candidates sometimes screenshot and share salary data publicly, and a misquoted figure causes more problems than a vague one.

> You are an HR Manager rejecting a candidate whose resume had strong credentials but who applied for a role outside their geographic availability and remote work is not on offer.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Office location: {office_city_and_country}
> Candidate's stated location: {candidate_location}
> Remote or hybrid policy for this role: {fully_onsite / hybrid_with_minimum_days}
> Tone: {professional / warm}
>
> Write a 110 to 130 word rejection email. Acknowledge the strength of their background in one sentence without being generic. Explain the onsite or hybrid requirement clearly. Do not suggest relocation unless you are prepared to offer relocation support. Close by encouraging them to check for remote roles on the careers page if any exist, or wish them well if not.

**When to use it:** When a strong candidate applied from another country and you did not make the location requirement clear enough in the job posting.

**Pro tip:** If your company's remote policy has been inconsistently applied across teams, do not reference hybrid minimums in writing — it can create a paper trail that contradicts what other hiring managers have offered. Keep it to "this role requires regular in-person attendance at our {office_city_and_country} office."

## Post-First Interview Rejections

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who completed a first-round interview but will not be progressed to the next stage.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Interviewer name or panel: {interviewer_name_or_initials}
> Stage completed: {first_round_stage — e.g. 30-minute recruiter call, panel video interview}
> General reason (non-specific, safe to share): {decline_reason — e.g. "we had exceptionally strong competition at this stage", "another candidate's background was a closer match for this phase of the team's growth"}
> Tone: {warm / professional / brief}
>
> Write a 150 to 180 word rejection email. Thank the candidate by name for their time at the interview stage specifically — not just "your application." Deliver the decline in the second paragraph, one sentence, no hedging. Include the safe-to-share reason verbatim. Do not promise feedback unless you plan to provide it. Close with a specific and authentic line — not "we wish you all the best in your future endeavours."

**When to use it:** Thursday afternoon after the hiring manager sends you a shortlist and you need to decline five first-round candidates before they start following up.

**Pro tip:** "We had exceptionally strong competition" is more credible than "you weren't the right fit" — the latter sounds like a euphemism for something the candidate did wrong. Use competition-framing when the candidate was genuinely good but outranked.

> You are an HR Manager sending a rejection email after a first-round technical screen where the candidate's technical skills did not meet the minimum bar for the role.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title — e.g. Senior Data Engineer}
> Skills assessed: {skills_list — e.g. SQL, Python, pipeline architecture}
> Assessment format: {assessment_format — e.g. 45-minute live coding session, take-home task}
> Whether to offer general feedback: {yes_brief / no}
> Tone: {direct / professional}
>
> Write a 140 to 170 word rejection email. Acknowledge the specific assessment format the candidate completed. Decline clearly in the second paragraph without using "skills gap" or "not the right fit." If yes_brief is selected, include two sentences of non-specific directional feedback (e.g. "We were looking for deeper experience in X and Y"). If no, decline without feedback. Do not invite them to reapply for the same role.

**When to use it:** After a technical screen where the candidate scored below the agreed pass mark and the hiring manager has confirmed the decision within the last 24 hours.

**Pro tip:** If the candidate was close to the bar and you're genuinely open to them reapplying in 12 months with more experience, say that explicitly — but only if a hiring manager has confirmed it. Vague "feel free to apply again" language without context reads as a hollow consolation.

> You are an HR Manager writing a post-first-interview rejection email for a candidate who interviewed well but is being declined because the role scope changed after the job was posted.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Original role title: {original_job_title}
> How the role changed: {role_change_description — e.g. "the role now requires someone to lead a team of six, which wasn't in the original brief", "the scope shifted from regional to global"}
> Whether a repost is planned: {yes_new_posting / no_not_reposting}
> Tone: {transparent / warm}
>
> Write a 160 to 200 word rejection email. Be transparent that the role changed after the candidate entered the process — do not frame this as a candidate performance issue. Acknowledge any inconvenience caused by the change. If yes_new_posting is selected, tell the candidate the role will be reposted with the updated scope and invite them to consider reapplying. If no_not_reposting, close cleanly with a specific and sincere note.

**When to use it:** When a restructure or leadership change shifts the job brief mid-recruitment and you have candidates mid-process who deserve an honest explanation.

**Pro tip:** Never let a candidate assume they underperformed when the real reason is an internal change. Candidates talk to each other — especially in niche industries — and vague rejections after role changes damage employer brand more than a transparent explanation does.

> You are an HR Manager declining a candidate after a first interview because the hiring manager and candidate's working style expectations were misaligned during the conversation.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Specific misalignment noted by hiring manager (internal, do not quote): {internal_note — e.g. "candidate wants fully autonomous work, manager runs a high-collaboration team"}
> Safe public framing: {public_framing — e.g. "team working style and the candidate's preferred ways of working weren't well aligned for this role"}
> Whether to offer to keep on file for other teams: {yes / no}
> Tone: {warm / professional}
>
> Write a 130 to 160 word rejection email. Thank the candidate for the interview. Deliver the decline in plain language using the public framing only. Do not use the internal note. Do not use "culture fit" — it has legal and bias risk. If yes, include one sentence about keeping their details for other team openings with a genuine qualifier. Close with one specific, non-generic sentence.

**When to use it:** After a debrief where the hiring manager says "they're good, just not right for how we work" — and you need to translate that into something you can actually send.

**Pro tip:** "Culture fit" has been successfully challenged in employment discrimination cases in several jurisdictions. Replace it with working-style, team-structure, or collaboration-model language every time. Train hiring managers to brief you using the same framing.

> You are an HR Manager sending a rejection email to a candidate who completed a first-round interview panel with three interviewers and received mixed feedback — two positive, one negative — with the hiring manager deciding to decline.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Panel composition (titles only): {interviewer_titles — e.g. Hiring Manager, Senior Engineer, Product Lead}
> Hiring manager's stated reason for declining: {hm_reason — e.g. "not enough hands-on experience in the core area we're hiring for"}
> Whether the candidate asked for feedback during the interview: {yes / no}
> Tone: {warm / direct}
>
> Write a 150 to 175 word rejection email. Do not reveal that the panel was divided. Do not attribute the decision to any individual interviewer. Frame the decline as a collective decision based on the role's requirements. If the candidate asked for feedback during the interview, offer one specific and constructive sentence of directional feedback. Close with a genuine note — not a cliché.

**When to use it:** After a split-panel debrief where the hiring manager has the final call and you need to communicate the outcome without exposing internal disagreement.

**Pro tip:** Never tell a candidate the panel was divided — even as a kind gesture to soften the blow. It invites them to appeal the decision by arguing against the dissenting view, which puts you in a very difficult position.

## Final-Round and Near-Miss Rejections

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who reached the final round of interviews — typically three or four stages — and was the second-choice candidate.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Number of interview stages completed: {stages_completed — e.g. 4}
> Specific strengths the candidate demonstrated: {two_to_three_genuine_strengths}
> Whether to keep them warm for future roles: {yes / no}
> Future role type if yes: {future_role_description — e.g. "a similar senior IC role opening in Q1"}
> Tone: {warm_and_genuine / professional}
>
> Write a 200 to 250 word rejection email. Open with direct acknowledgement of how far they progressed. Name the strengths specifically — not generically. Deliver the decline clearly in one sentence. If yes for future roles, be specific about what kind of role and realistic about timing. If no, close with a paragraph that makes the candidate feel genuinely respected for their time across multiple rounds. Do not use "incredibly difficult decision" or "we were blown away."

**When to use it:** Friday afternoon after the offer has been accepted by your first-choice candidate and you have a final-round runner-up who invested weeks in your process.

**Pro tip:** If you are genuinely keeping them warm for a real role, name a rough timeframe — "we expect to open a similar role in Q1 2027" beats "we'll keep you in mind." Vague warm-close language is remembered as insincere when nothing materialises.
