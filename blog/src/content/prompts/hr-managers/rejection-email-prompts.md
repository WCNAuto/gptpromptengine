## Early-Stage Rejections (Post-Application and Phone Screen)

> You are an HR Manager sending a rejection email to a candidate who applied but did not advance past the initial application review.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role applied for: {job_title}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Reason for decline (internal only, do not quote directly): {internal_reason}
> Tone: {warm_and_brief / professional_and_neutral}
>
> Write a 100 to 130 word rejection email. Open by thanking the candidate for applying. State clearly that they have not been selected to move forward. Do not include false encouragement or vague promises about future roles. Close with one sentence wishing them well in their search. No bullet points. No subject line needed.

**When to use it:** Friday afternoon when you have 40 unreviewed applicants to clear before the weekend and each one needs a real response, not a mail-merge ghost.

**Pro tip:** Set {internal_reason} to something specific like "missing required certification" or "salary expectation above band" so the AI calibrates the warmth level correctly. If you leave it vague, the output reads generic.

> You are an HR Manager declining a candidate after an initial phone screen.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Specific gap identified during screen: {skill_or_experience_gap}
> Years of experience candidate has: {candidate_experience_years}
> Years required for the role: {required_experience_years}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 120 to 150 word rejection email. Name the role specifically. Acknowledge the candidate's time on the call. Reference the experience gap without quoting internal notes or making it sound like a legal disclaimer. Close with a genuine, one-line wish for their search. No platitudes like "we were blown away by your background."

**When to use it:** After a phone screen where the candidate was clearly under-levelled but engaged and professional — you want to be honest without burning the relationship.

**Pro tip:** If the gap is seniority rather than skills (e.g. candidate has 2 years, role needs 6), specify that clearly in {skill_or_experience_gap}. The AI will frame it as a level mismatch rather than a deficiency, which reads better and is legally safer.

> You are an HR Manager sending a rejection to a candidate who applied speculatively, not for a live vacancy.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Type of speculative application: {cv_drop / linkedin_message / referral_introduction}
> Department they expressed interest in: {department_name}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Current hiring status for that department: {no_openings_planned / possible_openings_in_future}
>
> Write a 90 to 120 word response. Acknowledge their initiative. Be honest about the current position. If {current_hiring_status} is "possible_openings_in_future", include one sentence inviting them to watch your careers page — do not promise they will be contacted. If it is "no_openings_planned", do not suggest future contact. Close warmly.

**When to use it:** When a strong candidate has reached out through LinkedIn or via a mutual connection and you need to decline without closing the door entirely — or close it cleanly when there genuinely is no pipeline.

**Pro tip:** Do not use this prompt if the speculative application came through a formal referral program. In that case, the referrer also needs communication, and this email alone will not cover it.

> You are an HR Manager sending a high-volume batch of post-application rejections for a graduate scheme that received over 500 applications.
>
> Role: {graduate_scheme_name}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Application stage rejected at: {online_application / video_interview / assessment_centre}
> Number of positions available: {number_of_positions}
> Tone: {encouraging_but_honest}
>
> Write a single rejection email that will be personalised only by inserting {candidate_name} at the top. Keep it to 100 words exactly. Do not make false promises. Acknowledge competition was high. Include one sentence about where candidates can find future opportunities. Avoid any language that could be read as discriminatory or implying the candidate can reapply for this exact intake.

**When to use it:** Graduate intake season when you need one tight, legally clean email that goes to hundreds of candidates and still sounds human.

**Pro tip:** Have your legal or compliance team review the output once before you send to the full batch, not after. The prompt intentionally avoids discriminatory framing, but if your assessment centre used any psychometric tools, check your data protection obligations before referencing the stage name.

> You are an HR Manager rejecting a candidate after a job fair or campus recruiting event where you collected their details but are not progressing them.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Event name and date: {event_name_and_date}
> Role or function they expressed interest in: {target_function}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Reason not progressing: {no_matching_vacancy / experience_level_mismatch / application_not_submitted_formally}
>
> Write a 110 to 130 word email. Reference the event by name so the candidate remembers the context. Do not imply you were impressed if you were not. If {reason_not_progressing} is "application_not_submitted_formally", direct them to the careers page with one clear sentence. Close without promising to keep their CV on file unless your company genuinely does this.

**When to use it:** Two weeks after a campus event when the stack of business cards and scanned QR codes finally needs to be processed and closed out.

**Pro tip:** Candidates who met you in person experience email rejections more sharply than cold applicants. One specific detail — even just the event name — makes the email feel personal without requiring individual writing time.

## Post-First-Interview Rejections

> You are an HR Manager declining a candidate after a first-round interview with a hiring manager.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Interviewer name (for sign-off context): {interviewer_name}
> Specific reason for not progressing (internal): {internal_reason}
> Feedback permitted to share: {yes / no}
> If yes, one-sentence feedback: {feedback_sentence}
> Tone: {warm / direct / formal}
>
> Write a 140 to 170 word rejection email. Thank the candidate for their time. State clearly they have not been selected to progress. If {feedback_permitted} is "yes", include the {feedback_sentence} framed constructively in one short paragraph. If "no", do not invent vague feedback — simply omit it. Do not ask the candidate to reapply unless the hiring manager has explicitly approved this. Close professionally.

**When to use it:** The Monday morning after a Friday interview when the hiring manager has given you their decision and you need to close the loop before the candidate follows up.

**Pro tip:** If {feedback_permitted} is "no" because of a legal concern (e.g. you're in a jurisdiction with strict employment law around written feedback), do not let the AI fill the gap with soft, vague statements like "we found someone whose profile was a closer fit." That phrasing sounds like feedback without being specific enough to be useful, and it frustrates candidates more than a clean decline.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email after a panel interview where the candidate was strong but the role was filled internally.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Internal candidate who got the role: {do_not_name — describe outcome only}
> Department: {department_name}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Whether to invite future applications: {yes / no}
>
> Write a 150 to 180 word email. Acknowledge the candidate was competitive. Explain the role has been filled without naming or implying who the internal candidate was. If {invite_future_applications} is "yes", include a specific instruction (e.g. "follow our careers page") not a vague "we'll keep you in mind." Do not apologise excessively. One clean close.

**When to use it:** When a strong external candidate has gone through a full first-round panel and the role was quietly earmarked internally — a situation that requires honesty without creating a grievance.

**Pro tip:** Never include language like "we were very close to offering you the role." Even if true, it gives the candidate grounds to question the process and can escalate into a formal complaint in some jurisdictions. The prompt avoids this by default — don't add it back in manually.

> You are an HR Manager declining a candidate after a first interview for a sales role where they failed to meet the minimum commission structure expectation.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {sales_role_title}
> Salary or OTE expectation stated by candidate: {candidate_expectation}
> Actual OTE range for role: {role_ote_range}
> Gap: {gap_amount_or_percentage}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 120 to 140 word rejection email. Be direct about the reason being a compensation misalignment — do not hide it. Frame it as a mismatch rather than a failing. Do not suggest the candidate lower their expectations. Close by wishing them success finding a role that matches their target earnings. Tone: honest and respectful.

**When to use it:** After a first-round sales interview where the OTE gap came up and both sides knew it was a problem — sending a vague "we decided to go in a different direction" email does nobody any favours here.

**Pro tip:** Naming the compensation misalignment explicitly is better for your employer brand than a vague decline. Sales candidates talk to each other. A clear, respectful email about compensation lands better than a non-answer.

> You are an HR Manager rejecting a referred candidate (referred by a current employee) after a first interview.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Referring employee name: {referrer_name}
> Role interviewed for: {job_title}
> Reason for decline: {brief_internal_reason}
> Should the referrer be notified separately: {yes / no}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 130 to 160 word rejection email to the candidate only. Acknowledge the referral with one sentence of thanks. Decline clearly. Do not reveal internal deliberations or quote the referrer's assessment. Close with one line. If {referrer_notified_separately} is "yes", do not mention that in this email. Keep tone: professional and considerate.

**When to use it:** When a current employee referred their friend or former colleague and you need to decline — the most socially awkward rejection in the hiring cycle.

**Pro tip:** Generate a second, separate email for the referring employee at the same time using a different prompt. Sending the candidate's rejection without briefing the referrer first creates an uncomfortable situation where the referrer hears from the candidate before hearing from you.

> You are an HR Manager declining a candidate who interviewed well but is being rejected because the role was paused or cancelled before a second interview could be scheduled.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Stage reached: {first_interview_complete}
> Reason for pause or cancellation: {role_paused_due_to_budget / headcount_freeze / org_restructure}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Is re-opening likely: {yes_within_6_months / unlikely / unknown}
>
> Write a 140 to 160 word email. Be honest that the role has been paused or cancelled. Do not imply the candidate was rejected on merit. If {re_opening_likely} is "yes_within_6_months", tell them explicitly you may reach out and give a rough timeline. If "unlikely" or "unknown", do not make promises. Express genuine appreciation for their time. Tone: direct and respectful.

**When to use it:** During a hiring freeze or reorg when a candidate has been through a real interview and deserves a real explanation — not a copy-paste decline that makes them think they failed.

**Pro tip:** If you are in a jurisdiction where pausing a role mid-process carries legal implications (e.g. discrimination claims based on protected characteristics of candidates who were in-process), run this email past HR legal before sending. The prompt is written neutrally, but the context matters.

## Post-Final-Round and Offer-Stage Rejections

> You are an HR Manager declining a candidate who reached the final round but was not selected, where another candidate received the offer.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Number of final-round candidates: {number_of_finalists}
> Key reason other candidate was selected (do not state explicitly — inform tone only): {differentiating_factor}
> Feedback permitted: {yes / no}
> One-sentence feedback if yes: {feedback_sentence}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 170 to 200 word rejection email. Open by acknowledging they reached the final stage, which is a genuine accomplishment. State clearly that another candidate has been selected. If {feedback_permitted} is "yes", include {feedback_sentence} in a brief second paragraph. Do not use the phrase "we went with someone who was a better fit" — it is meaningless. Close with a specific next step if they should stay in touch, or a clean close if not. Tone: warm and direct.

**When to use it:** After a final-round decision has been made and the offer has been verbally accepted by the winning candidate — do not send this until the winning candidate has verbally accepted, in case they decline.

**Pro tip:** Wait until your first-choice candidate has accepted (even verbally) before sending this. If they decline and you want to go back to your second-choice candidate, you cannot unsend a rejection email. Build a 24-hour hold into your process.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who was the runner-up and whom you genuinely want to consider for future roles.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role they applied for: {job_title}
> Future role type they would suit: {future_role_description}
> Anticipated timeline for future opening: {timeframe}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Hiring manager who supports keeping in touch: {hiring_manager_name}
>
> Write a 160 to 190 word email. Decline clearly for the current role. Then — only because you mean it — extend a specific invitation to stay connected, referencing {future_role_description} and {timeframe}. Do not make this section vague. Include one concrete action (e.g. "connect with {hiring_manager_name} on LinkedIn" or "watch for [specific job family] on our careers page"). Tone: warm and genuine.

**When to use it:** When the runner-up was genuinely excellent and you have a real pipeline reason to stay in contact — not as a consolation tactic you use for every finalist.

**Pro tip:** Only use this prompt when you actually mean the future role reference. Candidates remember vague promises. If you send "we'd love to consider you in future" to every runner-up with no follow-through, it damages your employer brand more than a clean decline would.
