## Early-Stage Rejections: Phone Screen and Application Review

> You are an HR manager sending a rejection email to a candidate who applied for an open role but will not be progressed past the initial application review.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role applied for: {job_title}
> Department: {department}
> Reason for rejection (internal, do not include verbatim): {rejection_reason — e.g. missing required qualification, salary expectation mismatch, overqualified}
> Company tone: {tone — e.g. warm and human / professional and brief / formal}
> Recruiter name and sign-off: {recruiter_name}
>
> Write a 120 to 150 word rejection email. Do not use the internal reason verbatim. Open by thanking the candidate for their time. State clearly they will not be moving forward. Close with one genuine sentence that leaves a positive impression of the company. Do not promise to keep their CV on file unless instructed. Use plain language, no corporate filler.

**When to use it:** Monday morning after the weekend application batch drops and you need to clear 20+ early-stage rejections before your 10am standup.

**Pro tip:** If your {rejection_reason} is salary mismatch, tell ChatGPT explicitly "do not reference compensation in the email"—otherwise it often hints at it in ways that feel passive-aggressive to candidates.

> You are an HR manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who completed a phone screen but will not progress to a first interview.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Phone screen date: {screen_date}
> Interviewer name: {interviewer_name}
> Rejection reason (internal only): {rejection_reason — e.g. communication style not aligned, technical knowledge below bar, availability mismatch}
> Tone: {warm / neutral / concise}
> Recruiter sign-off: {recruiter_name}
>
> Write a 150 to 180 word rejection email. Reference that you appreciated the conversation on {screen_date}. State clearly the candidate will not be progressed. Give one brief, constructive sentence that is honest but not harsh—do not fabricate feedback you don't have. Close with a professional sign-off from {recruiter_name}.

**When to use it:** After a block of phone screens when you need to send same-day rejections before candidates follow up chasing feedback.

**Pro tip:** The "one brief constructive sentence" constraint stops ChatGPT from either over-explaining or saying nothing useful. If you genuinely have no specific feedback to share, set {rejection_reason} to "strong field of candidates" and the output will soften that sentence appropriately.

> You are an HR manager rejecting a candidate who applied speculatively without a live vacancy, and the role they are targeting does not currently exist or is frozen.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role they expressed interest in: {target_role}
> Reason there is no opening: {reason — e.g. headcount freeze, role does not exist at this level, restructure underway}
> Company: {company_name}
> Tone: {professional and warm / brief and direct}
> Recruiter name: {recruiter_name}
>
> Write a 100 to 130 word email. Be clear there is no current opportunity. Do not overpromise about future openings. If appropriate based on {reason}, include one sentence about following the company on LinkedIn for future roles. Sign off warmly.

**When to use it:** When speculative applications pile up during a hiring freeze and you need a response that is honest without burning goodwill.

**Pro tip:** Do not leave {reason} as "headcount freeze" if your company has announced layoffs publicly—candidates will read through vague language and it damages trust. Use "we are not actively hiring for this type of role right now" as your variable instead.

> You are an HR manager sending a bulk-style rejection email to candidates who applied for a high-volume entry-level or graduate role and did not make the shortlist.
>
> Role: {job_title}
> Number of applicants (for internal context only): {applicant_volume}

> Company name: {company_name}

>
> Write a 130 to 160 word rejection email suitable for a large group of candidates. Do not make it feel automated or cold. Thank candidates for their interest in {company_name}. State clearly they have not been shortlisted. Include one genuine sentence acknowledging competition for the role. Encourage them to apply for future roles if honest. Sign off from {recruiter_name_or_team}.

**When to use it:** After closing applications for a graduate scheme or early-career role where you received 300+ applications and need one clean email to send to 250 non-shortlisted candidates.

**Pro tip:** Add "Do not use the phrase 'we had many strong candidates'" to the prompt—ChatGPT defaults to it constantly and candidates have seen it so many times it reads as dismissive.

>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Assessment or filter used: {screening_tool — e.g. HireVue video screen, CV keyword filter, skills test}

> Can candidates reapply in future: {yes / no / yes_after_timeframe}

**Pro tip:** If your company uses AI-based screening, check your jurisdiction's employment law before mentioning the tool by name—some regions (EU AI Act, certain US states) require specific disclosures that go beyond a standard rejection email.

## Post-Interview Rejections: First and Second Round

> You are an HR manager writing a rejection email to a candidate after a first-round interview who showed genuine potential but was not the strongest fit for this specific role.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role interviewed for: {job_title}
> Interview date: {interview_date}
> Interviewer(s): {interviewer_names}
> Reason for not progressing (internal framing): {reason — e.g. technical gap in one core area, another candidate stronger on specific experience, culture alignment concern}
> Tone: {warm and honest / professional / encouraging}
> Is there another role they might suit: {yes — specify role / no}
> Recruiter name: {recruiter_name}
>
> Write a 180 to 220 word rejection email. Reference the interview on {interview_date} and thank {candidate_name} by name. State clearly they will not be progressed to the next stage. Include one specific, honest line of positive feedback—not generic praise. If {yes}, include a sentence redirecting them to the other role. Close from {recruiter_name} in the specified tone.

**When to use it:** After first-round interviews when you genuinely liked a candidate but they lost out to a stronger field and you want to preserve the relationship for future hiring.

**Pro tip:** The "one specific, honest line of positive feedback" variable is where this email earns goodwill or loses it. Give ChatGPT a real detail: e.g. "they showed strong stakeholder communication instincts" rather than leaving it open—otherwise the output defaults to "you impressed us" which candidates don't believe.

> You are an HR manager sending a rejection email to a candidate after a second-round panel interview for a mid-senior role.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}, level: {seniority — e.g. Senior Manager / Head of / Director}
> Panel members who interviewed them: {panel_names}
> Date of second interview: {interview_date}
> Core reason they were not selected (internal): {reason — e.g. another candidate had deeper domain expertise, leadership style not aligned to team needs, compensation gap}
> Feedback cleared to share: {one_shareable_feedback_point — or write "none cleared"}
> Tone: {respectful and direct / warm / formal}
> Recruiter name: {recruiter_name}
>
> Write a 200 to 250 word rejection email. Acknowledge the significant time {candidate_name} invested in the process. Thank the panel by name if fewer than 3 people. State clearly and early that they have not been selected. If {one_shareable_feedback_point} is not "none cleared", include it framed constructively. Close by leaving the door open to future roles if genuine. Sign off from {recruiter_name}.

**When to use it:** When a candidate has been through two rounds with multiple senior stakeholders and deserves a response that matches the effort they invested—not a three-line generic email.

**Pro tip:** "Leaving the door open" language only lands well if it's specific. Tell ChatGPT to write "we would welcome your application for future {specific_team_or_function} roles" rather than the hollow "we'll keep you in mind."

> You are an HR manager writing a rejection email to an internal candidate—an existing employee—who applied for an internal promotion or lateral move and was not selected.
>
> Employee name: {employee_name}
> Current role: {current_job_title}
> Role they applied for: {target_role}
> Their manager (cc'd or aware): {manager_name}
> Reason not selected (internal framing): {reason — e.g. external candidate stronger on specific experience, development areas identified that need time, role requirements shifted}
> Next step agreed with their manager: {agreed_next_step — e.g. development plan to revisit in 6 months / no specific plan yet}
> Tone: {direct and supportive / formal / honest and encouraging}
> Recruiter name: {recruiter_name}
>
> Write a 200 to 240 word email. Be direct early that they were not selected. Acknowledge their willingness to apply internally—it took courage. Reference the agreed next step ({agreed_next_step}) if it exists. Do not make promises about future opportunities that aren't real. This email will likely be discussed with their manager, so avoid anything that contradicts feedback already given. Close warmly from {recruiter_name}.

**When to use it:** Before a 1:1 conversation with an internal candidate who applied and was rejected—send this in advance so they have time to process before the face-to-face.

**Pro tip:** Always check with the hiring manager before sending—if the manager hasn't had their own conversation yet, this email arriving first blindsides everyone. The variable {agreed_next_step} forces you to have that alignment meeting before you draft anything.

> You are an HR manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who made it to final-round interview but was not offered the role because another candidate was selected.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Final interview date: {interview_date}
> Total interview stages completed: {number_of_stages}
> Reason for not selecting them (internal): {reason — e.g. marginal difference in presentation task score, other candidate had specific sector experience}
> Feedback you can share: {shareable_feedback — or "none at this stage"}
> Open to staying in touch for future roles: {yes / no}
> Recruiter name: {recruiter_name}
>
> Write a 220 to 270 word rejection email. Open by acknowledging directly that this is disappointing news after {number_of_stages} stages. Do not open with "thank you for your application"—this candidate is beyond that. State clearly they were not selected and that another candidate was offered the role. If {shareable_feedback} is not "none at this stage", include it. If {yes}, be specific about what kind of future role you'd reach back out about. Close with genuine respect from {recruiter_name}.

**When to use it:** When a final-stage candidate gets pipped at the post and you need an email that acknowledges the weight of that outcome rather than minimising it.

**Pro tip:** Opening with "I have some disappointing news" outperforms any softened opener for final-stage rejections—it respects the candidate's intelligence and reduces the whiplash of a positive-sounding opening followed by a rejection. Add this instruction explicitly to the prompt if ChatGPT defaults to something upbeat.

>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Type of assessment: {assessment_type — e.g. 2-hour technical coding test, 30-minute case study, take-home strategy task}

> Feedback cleared to share: {shareable_feedback — or "none"}
> Tone: {respectful and honest / professional / concise}
> Recruiter name: {recruiter_name}
>
> Write a 170 to 210 word rejection email. Explicitly acknowledge the time they put into {assessment_type}. State clearly they will not be progressed. If {shareable_feedback} is not "none", include one honest paragraph of feedback—do not vague it out with generic praise. Close by thanking them for the effort, not just the interest. Sign off from {recruiter_name}.

**When to use it:** After returning scored assessments when you owe candidates a response that acknowledges they gave you real work, not just a CV.

**Pro tip:** If {estimated_time} is more than 3 hours, consider whether your rejection email should also offer a debrief call—mention this to ChatGPT and it will add a line offering one. Candidates who do substantial unpaid work expect more than an email.
