## Post-Phone-Screen Rejections

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who completed a phone screen but will not be moving forward.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role applied for: {job_title}
> Reason for rejection (internal, do not state explicitly): {rejection_reason}
> One genuine positive from the call: {one_genuine_positive}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Tone: {warm_and_brief / professional_and_direct}
>
> Write a 120 to 150 word rejection email. Open with thanks for the candidate's time on the call. Name the positive without over-inflating it. Decline clearly in one sentence — do not use passive language like "we've decided to move in a different direction" without also making the outcome obvious. Do not offer false hope. Close with a genuine, specific wish for their search. No bullet points. No attachments. Plain prose only.

**When to use it:** Monday morning, after the Friday phone screen batch, when you have 12 rejections to send before the hiring manager asks for updates.

**Pro tip:** If the rejection reason is "cultural fit," replace it with something specific — communication style, pace preference, scope mismatch — before pasting into the prompt. Vague internal notes produce vague emails that feel hollow even when the tone is warm.

> You are an HR Manager writing a same-day rejection email to a candidate who completed a phone screen less than 24 hours ago and who the team unanimously passed on due to a skills gap.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Skills gap identified: {specific_skill_or_experience_gap}
> How long they have been in the job market (if known): {job_search_duration_or_unknown}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Tone: {direct / compassionate}
>
> Write a 100 to 130 word rejection email. Decline in the first two sentences so the candidate isn't kept reading. Briefly reference the skills gap in a way that is honest but not clinical — phrase it as a requirement mismatch, not a personal failing. If {job_search_duration_or_unknown} is not "unknown," acknowledge their patience in one sentence. Close with a concrete action they can take (e.g. checking your careers page in 6 months). Do not use the phrase "we'll keep your CV on file" unless you actually will.

**When to use it:** When the hiring manager sends a Slack message asking you to "let that last candidate know quickly" and you need something respectful out the door before 10am.

**Pro tip:** Setting the tone variable to "direct" when the candidate asked for feedback on the call will often produce a slightly more substantive decline — useful when you want to honour that implicit expectation without writing a full feedback email.

> You are an HR Manager writing a phone-screen rejection for a candidate who was strong but lost out to a slightly better-matched applicant.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Their strongest quality: {strongest_quality}
> What the selected candidate had that they did not: {differentiating_factor}
> Whether to invite future applications: {yes / no}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 150 to 180 word rejection email. Open warmly. Acknowledge what made them a competitive candidate by naming {strongest_quality} specifically. Decline clearly. If {whether_to_invite_future_applications} is "yes," include a direct and specific invitation — not a vague "we'd love to stay in touch." If "no," close with a sincere wish for their search without the invitation. Do not explain what the selected candidate had. Do not frame this as "you were our second choice."

**When to use it:** When you've advanced two strong candidates to final stage, one offer is accepted, and you owe the runner-up a rejection that doesn't feel like an afterthought.

**Pro tip:** Candidates who were close know they were close. An email that names one specific strength lands far better than a generic decline and significantly reduces the chance they post a negative experience on Glassdoor.

> You are an HR Manager writing a phone-screen rejection for a candidate who is clearly overqualified for the role and unlikely to stay long-term.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Their current or most recent level: {current_seniority_level}
> The level the role is actually designed for: {role_seniority_level}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Tone: {honest / diplomatic}
>
> Write a 130 to 160 word rejection email. Do not use the word "overqualified" — it is legally and professionally risky and rarely lands well. Instead, frame the decision around role scope and growth trajectory. Be direct that they will not be progressing. If tone is "honest," include one sentence explaining the scope mismatch plainly. If "diplomatic," focus on alignment without detailing the gap. Do not suggest a more senior role unless one actually exists. Close professionally.

**When to use it:** When a 15-year industry veteran applies for an entry-level position and you need to decline without it reading as dismissive of their experience.

**Pro tip:** Never promise to "share their profile internally for senior roles" unless a senior role is actively open. Candidates follow up on that, and a broken promise at the rejection stage creates more damage than no promise at all.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who completed a phone screen for a role that has since been put on hold or cancelled internally.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Whether the role may reopen: {likely / unlikely / unknown}
> Expected timeframe if likely: {timeframe_or_not_applicable}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Tone: {transparent / standard_professional}
>
> Write a 120 to 150 word rejection email. Explain clearly that the role is on hold and the candidate is not progressing — do not bury this. If tone is "transparent," you may disclose that the pause is due to an internal decision and is not a reflection on the candidate's suitability. If "standard_professional," keep it brief and factual. If {whether_the_role_may_reopen} is "likely," include {timeframe_or_not_applicable} and invite them to reapply when it does. Do not make commitments you cannot honour.

**When to use it:** When a headcount freeze lands mid-process and you have five candidates in the pipeline who deserve a real explanation rather than a generic "we've moved in a different direction."

**Pro tip:** "Transparent" tone on a role-on-hold rejection often produces better employer brand outcomes than a vague standard decline — candidates are more likely to reapply and less likely to feel misled.

## Post-Interview Rejections (First Round)

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who completed a first-round panel interview but will not be progressing to the next stage.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Interview format: {panel / one_to_one / technical_screen}
> One specific strength the panel noted: {panel_noted_strength}
> Primary reason for not progressing (internal use): {primary_rejection_reason}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 160 to 200 word rejection email. Thank them for attending and for the time they invested in preparation. Reference the interview format so the email feels personal, not mass-sent. Name {panel_noted_strength} as a genuine acknowledgement. Decline clearly. Do not share {primary_rejection_reason} verbatim, but do not replace it with a meaningless phrase either — find a professional way to reference the decision. Close with a specific, non-hollow good wish. No bullet points.

**When to use it:** After a busy interview week when you have six first-round rejections to send before the shortlist goes to the hiring manager.

**Pro tip:** Including the interview format ("Thank you for joining us for the panel session on Thursday") takes five seconds but signals to the candidate that someone actually remembers who they are. It meaningfully reduces complaints and reply emails asking for feedback.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who interviewed well but whose salary expectations exceed the role's approved budget, and the budget cannot flex.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Their stated salary expectation: {candidate_salary_expectation}
> The approved salary band top: {approved_band_top}
> Whether the band may change in future: {yes_with_timeline / no / unknown}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 140 to 170 word rejection email. Be honest that the decision is driven by a compensation mismatch, not by their interview performance. Do not imply the candidate should accept less — this is an alignment issue, not a negotiation opener. If {whether_the_band_may_change_in_future} is "yes_with_timeline," invite them to reconnect at that point with a specific time reference. If "no" or "unknown," close without a conditional invitation. Keep the tone respectful of their market value.

**When to use it:** When a strong candidate has a number you simply cannot meet, and you want to decline honestly without burning the relationship or implying they were wrong to ask for what they're worth.

**Pro tip:** Candidates who receive an honest compensation-based rejection are far more likely to reapply or refer others than candidates who suspect they were turned down for another reason but were given a vague decline. Transparency here is good employer brand strategy.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who was a strong cultural fit but lacked the technical depth required for the role.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Technical area where the gap was identified: {technical_gap_area}
> How significant the gap was: {minor_gap / moderate_gap / significant_gap}
> Whether the company offers this type of work at a more junior level: {yes / no}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 170 to 210 word rejection email. Open by acknowledging the quality of their engagement. Decline with one clear sentence. Reference {technical_gap_area} honestly — frame it as a requirement of the role, not a judgement of their capability overall. Calibrate language to {how_significant_the_gap_was}: a minor gap warrants encouragement to apply again; a significant gap should not include false encouragement. If {whether_the_company_offers_this_type_of_work_at_a_more_junior_level} is "yes," mention it specifically with the role type. Do not bullet-point feedback — keep it in prose.

**When to use it:** When the hiring manager says "great person, just not ready" after a first interview and you need to write a rejection that reflects that nuance without being patronising.

**Pro tip:** Do not include "we encourage you to build your skills in {technical_gap_area}" unless you are prepared for the candidate to reply asking for resources. That reply then needs a response. Only add development commentary if you have time to handle the follow-up.

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who applied for a role but whose interview revealed they were actually more interested in a different function than the one they applied for.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role they applied for: {applied_role}
> Function they actually expressed interest in: {actual_interest_function}
> Whether that function is currently hiring: {yes_with_role_name / no}
> Company name: {company_name}
> Tone: {helpful / brief}
>
> Write a 130 to 160 word rejection email. Decline for the applied role. If tone is "helpful," acknowledge the mismatch you noticed and gently surface it — do not make it sound like an accusation. If {whether_that_function_is_currently_hiring} is "yes_with_role_name," include a specific referral to that opening. If "no," close without raising the alternative function as a dead end. Keep it human and non-bureaucratic.

**When to use it:** When an interview goes sideways because the candidate clearly wanted a different team, and you want to close the loop honestly rather than rejecting them for "not being the right fit" with no context.

**Pro tip:** Only reference the alternative function if you are willing to make a warm introduction or direct them to a live posting. A vague "there may be other opportunities" in this context reads as dismissive filler.

> You are an HR Manager writing a first-round rejection email to a candidate who completed a video interview asynchronously and whose recorded answers did not demonstrate the required communication skills for the role.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> Asynchronous video platform used: {video_platform_name}
> Communication skill gap observed: {specific_communication_gap}
> Whether feedback was promised during the process: {yes / no}
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 150 to 180 word rejection email. Thank them for completing the asynchronous interview. Decline clearly. If {whether_feedback_was_promised_during_the_process} is "yes," include one specific, constructive sentence referencing {specific_communication_gap} framed as a role requirement, not a personal critique. If "no," decline without detailed feedback. Do not over-explain. Close professionally without offering further review.

**When to use it:** When your ATS flags low-scoring async video responses and you need to clear a backlog of rejections before the shortlist is presented on Wednesday.

**Pro tip:** If feedback was promised and the gap is "unclear answers," that phrase alone is too vague to be useful. Replace it before pasting with something specific: "answers that did not address the question asked" or "responses under 30 seconds on questions requiring detailed examples." Specificity makes feedback usable, not painful.

## Final-Stage and Offer-Adjacent Rejections

> You are an HR Manager writing a rejection email to a candidate who reached the final interview stage but did not receive the offer.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Role: {job_title}
> How many final-stage candidates there were: {number_of_finalists}
> What differentiated the hired candidate (internal, do not disclose directly): {differentiating_factor}
> Length of the candidate's process: {number_of_interview_rounds} rounds over {total_weeks} weeks
> Company name: {company_name}
>
> Write a 200 to 250 word rejection email. Open by acknowledging the length and quality of their process. Decline clearly in the first paragraph — do not make them read to the end to find out. In the second paragraph, acknowledge how competitive the process was without fabricating a compliment. Do not say "you were our second choice." Close with a genuine and specific offer to be a reference point for future roles or to stay in the company's talent network — only include this if it is a real offer, not a courtesy line.

**When to use it:** When you've just signed the offer letter for your new hire and you have one or two finalists who spent five weeks in your process and deserve more than a two-line email.

**Pro tip:** Final-stage candidates are the most likely to discuss their experience publicly. An email that acknowledges the investment they made — specifically the number of rounds and weeks — without being melodramatic signals genuine respect and dramatically reduces the risk of a negative Glassdoor review.
