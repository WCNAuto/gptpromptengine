## Post-Application Rejection (Before Interview Stage)

> You are an HR Manager at {company_name} sending a post-application rejection email to a candidate who applied for {job_title}.
>
> Candidate first name: {candidate_first_name}
> Role they applied for: {job_title}
> Stage they reached: Application review — no interview offered
> Volume context: {high_volume_role: yes / no} — if yes, note that you received a large number of applications
> Tone: {warm / professional / brief}
> Invite to future roles: {yes / no}
>
> Write a 120 to 160 word rejection email. Open by thanking them for applying. State clearly in the second sentence that they have not been selected to move forward. Do not give specific feedback — this was application stage only. If invite to future roles is yes, close with one sentence encouraging them to watch the careers page. No hollow phrases like "we were blown away by the quality of applicants." Sign off as the Talent Team at {company_name}.

**When to use it:** Monday morning when you have 40 application-stage rejections queued from the weekend and a hiring manager chasing you for the shortlist by noon.

**Pro tip:** If {high_volume_role} is yes, the AI will add a volume caveat automatically — this protects you if a candidate asks why they didn't get feedback. Remove it only if the role received fewer than 20 applicants, where volume framing reads as a brush-off.

> You are an HR Manager at {company_name} rejecting a candidate who applied for {job_title} but whose application was incomplete or did not meet a mandatory requirement.
>
> Candidate first name: {candidate_first_name}
> Missing or unmet requirement: {missing_requirement} (e.g. right to work, required qualification, minimum years experience)
> Tone: {matter-of-fact / apologetic}
> Reapply eligible in future: {yes / no}
> Company careers page URL: {careers_url}
>
> Write a 100 to 130 word rejection email. Be clear that the application could not proceed because of {missing_requirement}. Do not imply the candidate was otherwise strong — you have not reviewed the full application. If reapply eligible is yes, include one sentence pointing to {careers_url}. Avoid any language that could imply the requirement was subjective or negotiable.

**When to use it:** When a candidate slips through an ATS filter but clearly lacks a non-negotiable requirement — right to work documentation missing, CIMA/CPA licence required, visa sponsorship not available.

**Pro tip:** Never soften "we cannot proceed" to "unfortunately we are unable to consider your application at this time" — that phrasing invites follow-up asking what "at this time" means. The AI defaults to cleaner language; keep it.

> You are an HR Manager at {company_name} sending a bulk post-application rejection to candidates who applied for {job_title} and reached the resume review stage.
>
> Role: {job_title}
> Hiring team sign-off name: {recruiter_name}
> Tone: {warm / neutral}
> Did the role close early due to internal hire: {yes / no}
> Candidate first name placeholder to personalise: {candidate_first_name}
>
> Write a 110 to 140 word rejection email suitable for bulk sending with mail-merge personalisation. Include {candidate_first_name} as the salutation variable. If role closed early due to internal hire is yes, include one brief, honest sentence acknowledging the role was filled internally — do not fabricate feedback. Close by wishing the candidate well in their search. Sign off from {recruiter_name}, {company_name} Talent Team.

**When to use it:** End of a recruitment round where you are closing out 80+ candidates in an ATS batch send and need one well-worded message that works for all of them without sounding robotic.

**Pro tip:** Paste this output into your ATS email template exactly as written, then set {candidate_first_name} as the merge field. Test with your own email address first — ATS systems occasionally drop the variable and send "Dear ," to everyone.

> You are an HR Manager at {company_name} rejecting a candidate who applied speculatively (not to a live role) via the company's general enquiry inbox or LinkedIn.
>
> Candidate first name: {candidate_first_name}
> Type of speculative approach: {speculative_type} (e.g. cold LinkedIn message, CV via info@ email, referral from employee)
> Relevant upcoming roles: {upcoming_roles: yes / no}
> If yes, describe briefly: {upcoming_role_description}
> Tone: {warm / professional}
>
> Write a 130 to 170 word response. Acknowledge the approach positively without committing to anything. State clearly that there is no current role that matches their background. If upcoming roles is yes, mention {upcoming_role_description} briefly and direct them to watch the careers page or reapply when live. Do not offer to "keep their CV on file" unless your company has a process for this — instead use "we will reach out if a relevant role opens." Sign off from the Talent Team.

**When to use it:** Friday afternoon clear-out of the general inbox before the weekend — speculative CVs and LinkedIn cold messages that have been sitting unanswered for more than five days.

**Pro tip:** "We'll keep your CV on file" is a legal liability in GDPR-compliant regions unless you have a documented process and explicit consent for data retention. The prompt avoids this phrase by default. If your company does have a talent pool programme, add that detail as a variable and let the AI include it with the correct framing.

> You are an HR Manager at {company_name} rejecting an internal candidate who applied for {job_title} but will not be moving to interview.
>
> Candidate name: {candidate_name}
> Their current role: {current_role}
> Their manager aware of application: {manager_aware: yes / no}
> Reason category (do not disclose verbatim): {reason_category} (e.g. stronger external pipeline, skills gap, role scope mismatch)
> Tone: {sensitive / direct}
> Next step offered: {next_step} (e.g. career conversation with HR, development plan discussion, reapply next cycle)
>
> Write a 180 to 220 word rejection email. Open with appreciation for their interest and courage in applying internally. State clearly they are not progressing to interview. Do not disclose {reason_category} verbatim — frame it as the selection being highly competitive or the panel prioritising a specific experience set. Offer {next_step} concretely. Close warmly, acknowledging their value in their current role. This email will be sent before any verbal conversation — keep the tone one that doesn't blindside them.

**When to use it:** When an internal applicant didn't make the shortlist and their manager has flagged they are expecting good news — you need the email ready before the hiring manager accidentally says something first.

**Pro tip:** If {manager_aware} is no, add "I will be in touch separately to discuss next steps" rather than suggesting a career conversation — you may need to loop in their manager before that offer is made. Revise that closing line manually after the AI drafts it.

## Post-First Interview Rejection

> You are an HR Manager at {company_name} sending a rejection email to a candidate after a first-round interview for {job_title}.
>
> Candidate first name: {candidate_first_name}
> Interview format: {interview_format} (e.g. 30-minute video screen, in-person competency interview)
> Interviewer name(s): {interviewer_names}
> Offer feedback: {offer_feedback: yes / no}
> If yes, one honest, neutral feedback theme: {feedback_theme} (e.g. the panel were looking for more hands-on experience with X, communication style was less structured than the role requires)
> Tone: {warm / professional}
>
> Write a 150 to 200 word rejection email. Thank them for attending the {interview_format}. State they have not been selected to proceed. If offer feedback is yes, include one paragraph using the STAR model to frame {feedback_theme} constructively — name the gap without framing it as a personal failing. Close by wishing them well. Do not invite them to reapply unless specifically instructed — omit that option if not told to include it.

**When to use it:** Day after a first-round interview block when you have five to eight candidates to reject before the second-round invites go out.

**Pro tip:** The STAR framing for feedback keeps the AI from producing vague filler like "the panel felt other candidates were a stronger fit." That phrase adds nothing and invites a follow-up asking what "stronger fit" means. A one-sentence STAR-framed observation is more useful to the candidate and less likely to generate a grievance.

> You are an HR Manager at {company_name} rejecting a candidate after a first-round phone screen for {job_title} where a deal-breaker emerged during the conversation.
>
> Candidate first name: {candidate_first_name}
> Deal-breaker category: {dealbreaker_category} (e.g. salary expectations significantly above budget, location/relocation requirement cannot be met, notice period incompatible with start date)
> Salary budget disclosed during screen: {budget_disclosed: yes / no}
> Tone: {direct / empathetic}
> Suggest reapplying to different roles: {suggest_other_roles: yes / no}
>
> Write a 120 to 150 word rejection email. Reference the phone conversation briefly. State clearly that the role cannot meet {dealbreaker_category} — be specific enough that the candidate understands this is a logistics mismatch, not a performance issue. If {budget_disclosed} is yes, confirm the budget figure was shared during the call so there are no surprises. If suggest other roles is yes, add one sentence directing them to the careers page. Do not hedge or leave room for negotiation if the deal-breaker is firm.

**When to use it:** Same day as the phone screen when the mismatch emerged in the first ten minutes and you want to close the loop before they follow up asking for a decision.

**Pro tip:** If the deal-breaker is salary and you did not disclose the budget during the call, set {budget_disclosed} to no — the AI will frame the mismatch without revealing a number you haven't shared. Do not manually add the salary figure to the output unless your hiring manager has signed off on disclosing it externally.

> You are an HR Manager at {company_name} rejecting a candidate after a first-round interview where they interviewed well but the role has been paused or put on hold.
>
> Candidate first name: {candidate_first_name}
> Job title: {job_title}
> Reason for pause: {pause_reason} (e.g. headcount freeze, restructure, budget review — use only what can be communicated externally)
> Likely timeline to reopen: {reopen_timeline} (e.g. Q1 2027, unknown, 3 to 6 months)
> Keep candidate warm: {keep_warm: yes / no}
> Tone: {transparent / diplomatic}
>
> Write a 160 to 200 word email. Open by thanking them for the interview and affirming the conversation was positive. Explain that the role has been paused due to {pause_reason}. State {reopen_timeline} honestly — if it is unknown, say so. If keep warm is yes, ask their permission to re-engage when the role reopens and confirm you will reach out directly. Do not promise a role or imply they are guaranteed an offer when it reopens. Close professionally.

**When to use it:** When a headcount freeze lands mid-process and you have three candidates in first-round limbo who deserve an honest update rather than silence.

**Pro tip:** "We would love to reach out when the role reopens — would you be happy for us to do so?" is not a data consent form. If your company is subject to GDPR or equivalent legislation, this email should be followed up with a formal talent pool opt-in. Flag this to your legal or compliance team before sending if the reopen timeline is longer than 30 days.

> You are an HR Manager at {company_name} sending a rejection after a first-round group or panel interview for {job_title}, where multiple candidates interviewed on the same day.
>
> Candidate first name: {candidate_first_name}
> Interview date: {interview_date}
> Number of candidates interviewed that day: {candidate_count}
> Panel members who interviewed them: {panel_names}
> Feedback offered: {feedback_offered: yes / no}
> Tone: {warm / concise}
>
> Write a 140 to 170 word rejection email. Acknowledge the group interview format briefly — it helps contextualise the competitive nature of the decision without sounding dismissive. Thank them for their time on {interview_date}. State the decision clearly. If feedback is yes, include one line of neutral, constructive feedback. Thank {panel_names} on behalf of the business. Close with genuine good wishes. Do not make the email sound like a form letter even though multiple people are receiving a version of it — keep the salutation and any specific references personalised.

**When to use it:** After an assessment centre or group interview day where you interviewed six candidates for two spots and need rejections out by end of business the same day.

**Pro tip:** If the group was large (10+), avoid referencing the exact candidate count in the email — "we interviewed 14 people" can feel dehumanising. The AI defaults to a vaguer "highly competitive process" framing when {candidate_count} is high. You can override this manually if transparency is a company value.

> You are an HR Manager at {company_name} rejecting a candidate after a first-round interview who was referred by a current employee.
>
> Candidate first name: {candidate_first_name}
> Job title: {job_title}
> Referring employee name: {referring_employee_name}
> Referring employee's relationship to candidate: {referral_relationship} (e.g. former colleague, friend, family member)
> Has the referring employee been told the outcome yet: {referrer_notified: yes / no}
> Tone: {warm / professional}
>
> Write a 160 to 190 word rejection email. Acknowledge the referral warmly but without over-crediting it — make clear the decision was made on role fit, not the referral relationship. State clearly they have not been selected. If {referrer_notified} is yes, acknowledge they may already be aware. Do not ask the candidate to give feedback on the process in this email — that creates awkwardness when the referrer is a current employee. Close genuinely. Do not include "please don't let this put you off applying in future" unless specifically instructed — it can feel patronising in referral contexts.

**When to use it:** When a colleague's referral didn't make it through first round and you need to send the rejection before the referring employee finds out through the grapevine.

**Pro tip:** If {referrer_notified} is no, send this email and then immediately message the referring employee directly — do not let them find out from the candidate first. Draft that internal message separately; it needs a different tone and level of detail than this rejection.
