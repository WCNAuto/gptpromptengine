## Program Launch Communications

> You are an HR Manager announcing a new diversity and inclusion initiative to the entire company.
>
> Company: {company_name}
> Initiative name: {program_name}
> Launch date: {start_date}
> Key focus areas: {three_focus_areas_in_bullets}
> Executive sponsor: {sponsor_name_and_title}
> Employee participation: {mandatory / voluntary / hybrid}
> Timeline: {program_duration}
> Previous DEI efforts: {none / basic_training / existing_programs}
>
> Write a 400 to 500 word company-wide email announcement. Open with why this matters to our business success. Include three specific actions employees will see in the first 30 days. Address potential concerns about time commitment. Close with how success will be measured. Use an encouraging but professional tone.

**When to use it:** The week before your DEI program launches, when you need leadership-approved messaging that gets employees engaged instead of defensive.

**Pro tip:** Test the email with your most skeptical manager first. If they don't push back on the business case or time commitment, your broader audience won't either.

**When to use it:** Right after your leadership announcement, when you need middle managers to have consistent, confident conversations with their teams.

**Pro tip:** Send these talking points 48 hours before team meetings happen. Managers who wing it create confusion that takes weeks to undo.

> You are an HR Manager creating an FAQ document for employees about a new diversity and inclusion program.
>
> Program: {initiative_name}
> Target audience: {employee_level_or_department}
> Participation: {mandatory / voluntary / selective}
> Time commitment: {hours_per_month_or_session}
> Confidentiality level: {anonymous / tracked / public}
> Budget impact: {cost_to_employees_if_any}
> Historical context: {why_launching_now}
> Leadership involvement: {exec_participation_level}
>
> Write 8 to 10 FAQ pairs, 50 to 80 words per answer. Lead with the three questions you know employees are already asking. Include one question about career impact, one about time away from regular work, and one about measurement. Use direct language that doesn't oversell or undersell the commitment required.

**When to use it:** The day after your announcement email, when HR is getting the same five questions from multiple employees and you need consistent answers.

**Pro tip:** Post this on your intranet and send the link in Slack. Don't attach it to emails—people lose attachments but bookmark links.

## Training Content Development

> You are an HR Manager creating a scenario-based training exercise about unconscious bias in hiring decisions.
>
> Role focus: {hiring_managers / recruiters / interview_panels}
> Industry context: {company_sector}
> Common bias type: {specific_bias_to_address}
> Experience level: {years_experience_of_participants}
> Training format: {workshop / online / self_paced}
> Group size: {number_of_participants}
> Time allocation: {minutes_for_this_exercise}
> Sensitivity level: {how_direct_to_be}
>
> Write a 350 to 400 word training scenario with a realistic hiring situation, three candidate profiles that reveal different types of bias, discussion questions that get people talking, and a debrief section with key learning points. Make the scenario specific to our industry and include unconscious bias that good people actually exhibit.

**When to use it:** Two weeks before facilitating bias training, when you need content that feels relevant instead of theoretical and gets participants to recognize their own blind spots.

**Pro tip:** Test this with one manager privately first. If they get defensive about the scenario, make the candidates more obviously different in qualifications, not just demographics.

> Follow_up_plan: {what_happens_after_training}
>

Include exact phrases to redirect defensiveness and time stamps for each section.

**Pro tip:** Have a co-facilitator for microaggression training. When participants get emotional, one person handles the situation while the other keeps the group engaged.

**When to use it:** One month before advanced manager training, when you need to understand current inclusive leadership skills and tailor your program to real gaps.

## Program Measurement and Evaluation

> You are an HR Manager designing a post-training feedback survey for employees who completed diversity and inclusion workshops.
>
> Training program: {specific_program_name}
> Training format: {workshop / online / blended}
> Participant level: {employee_types}
> Training duration: {hours_or_days}
> Key learning objectives: {three_main_goals}
> Facilitator: {internal / external / mixed}
> Feedback purpose: {improvement / compliance / effectiveness}
> Response confidentiality: {anonymous / tracked / optional}
>
> Write a 10 to 12 question survey that measures both learning and behavioral intention. Include questions about content relevance, facilitator effectiveness, and likelihood to apply concepts. Add two questions about workplace climate and one about training format preferences. Use a mix of rating scales and open-ended questions. Keep completion time under 5 minutes.

**When to use it:** Immediately after training sessions end, when participant reactions are fresh and you need data to improve future programs.

**Pro tip:** Send the survey within 2 hours of training completion. Response rates drop 40% if you wait until the next day.

>
> Original training: {program_focus}
> Participant roles: {job_levels_trained}
> Behavioral targets: {specific_changes_expected}
> Measurement approach: {self_report / peer_feedback / manager_observation}
> Baseline data: {pre_training_measurements}
> Business metrics: {performance_indicators_tracked}
> Survey length: {time_commitment}
> Results sharing: {how_data_used}
>
> Develop a follow-up assessment with 8 to 10 questions measuring specific workplace behaviors, not just attitudes. Focus on actions like speaking up in meetings, hiring decisions, team collaboration, and conflict resolution. Include one question about barriers to applying training and one about additional support needed. Provide a simple analysis framework for identifying trends.

**When to use it:** Three months after training completion, when you need evidence that learning translated into actual workplace behavior changes.

**Pro tip:** Compare responses to performance review ratings from the same period. Managers who show DEI behavior improvement often show overall leadership growth too.
